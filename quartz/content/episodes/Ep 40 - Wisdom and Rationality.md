# Ep 40 - Wisdom and Rationality

## What this lecture argues
Ep 40 develops wisdom in depth — grounding it in systematic transframing, distinguishing it from knowledge and expertise, and identifying its perspectival-participatory aspects — then begins the Rationality Debate by introducing both the experimental evidence for human irrationality and Cohen's competence/performance response.

**Wisdom as systematic transframing**: McKee and Barber's convergence (seeing through illusion and into reality) is unpacked via Piaget. Children make *systematic* errors (conservation failures, egocentrism) that reflect systematic mis-constraints. They eventually undergo a qualitative developmental shift — not an insight into *this* conservation problem, but an insight into *conservation as a type of error*. This is a **transframing** insight: it changes competence (sensibility transcendence), not just content. Wisdom = the adult-to-sage relationship mirrors the child-to-adult relationship: the sage has undergone systematic transframing relative to the adult, just as the adult has relative to the child. Wisdom is not accumulation of propositions but the cultivation of a radically more adequate salience landscape (Ep 40).

**Wisdom vs. knowledge — two senses of "how"**: McKee and Barber show wisdom is about *how* you know, not *what* you know. (1) **Process vs. product**: wisdom attends to processing, not just products of cognition — this directly links wisdom to rationality (Stanovich: the rational person cares about processing for its own sake). (2) **Descriptive vs. interpretive knowing** (Kekes): descriptive knowledge = grasping the facts; *interpretive knowing* = grasping the *significance* of the facts = understanding. Wisdom requires understanding, not mere description. This invokes RR: understanding is relevance realization applied to the content of one's knowledge. What does this fact mean? What does it connect to? What does it imply for how I should act? (Ep 40).

**Perspectival-participatory dimension of wisdom**: "I am wise" is a **pragmatic self-contradiction**: stating it places the speaker in the perspective and identity of someone who is not wise (since the wise person knows what they don't know; claiming wisdom from within wisdom would be claiming certainty one doesn't have). This is not a logical contradiction but a perspectival-participatory one — it reveals that wisdom cannot be had or stated; it can only be enacted from within a certain kind of identity. This connects to awe/humility: the wise person is confronted with inexhaustibility, which induces the original humility (Ep 40).

**Expanded definition of rationality**: Rationality cannot be reduced to facility with syllogistic logic. Vervaeke's definition: rationality = **reliably and systematically overcoming self-deception, and affording flourishing / optimization of cognition**. Key features: (1) **Domain-general** — unlike expertise (domain-specific, often doesn't transfer, may even interfere in adjacent domains), rationality must apply across all domains of life; (2) **Process over product** — the rational person pays attention to *how* they are reasoning, not just what conclusions they reach; this is **critical detachment** — the ability to evaluate an argument independently of the conclusion it leads to; (3) **Goals come under revision** — as one optimizes cognition, the goals themselves are reviewed and revised; not just pursuing fixed ends more effectively (Ep 40).

**The Rationality Debate**: Multiple experiments show humans reliably fail to follow rational principles they explicitly acknowledge: (a) the lily pad doubling problem (misleaping insight), (b) evaluating the quality of arguments based on whether you agree with their conclusions rather than their logical structure (failing critical detachment), (c) **belief perseverance** — maintaining beliefs after the evidence for them has been entirely debunked (the suicide note experiment). The same pattern in all cases: people acquiesce to the principle when stated, but reliably fail to apply it. This seems to show systematic self-deception.

**Cohen's response**: The standard for rationality must come from us (autonomy of reason — Kant/Plato; Euthyphro: if the gods just command it, it's not good; reason must be self-governing). Ought implies Can: we can only be held to standards we are capable of meeting. Therefore: we must be the source of these standards AND be capable of meeting them. How to account for the experimental failures? **Competence vs. performance** distinction (Chomsky): competence = what you are capable of; performance = what you actually do. Implementation processes intervene between competence and performance (tiredness, distraction, emotional load). We idealize competence from performance by subtracting implementation errors. Cohen argues all the experimental errors must be performance errors, not competence failures — therefore humans ARE rational at the level of competence. Next episode: Stanovich's rebuttal (Ep 40).
