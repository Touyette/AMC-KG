# Ep 05 - Plato and the Cave

## What this lecture argues
This episode develops Plato's synthesis of Socrates and Pythagoras into the first systematic psychological theory and an account of wisdom as anagogic ascent. Plato's response to Socrates' death is to ask why people are so foolish — and his answer is the Tripartite Psyche: reason, thymos (spiritedness), and appetite, each operating by different principles and generating inner conflict when misaligned. The Myth of the Cave is introduced as a mythological representation of the anagogic process: the upward movement from shadows to direct contact with the Good (the source of both intelligibility and being) is a participatory transformation, not a passive acquisition of beliefs. The 4 Kinds of Knowing are introduced (propositional, procedural, perspectival, participatory), with emphasis on participatory knowing as the deepest mode. Hyperbolic Discounting explains why appetite so reliably overrides reason. Logos is introduced as structural-functional organization — the real pattern that is simultaneously how we know something and what makes it be what it is. The episode closes by previewing Aristotle's dynamical development of these ideas.

## Concepts introduced or developed
- [[The 4 Kinds of Knowing]] — introduced here; the framework of propositional, procedural, perspectival, and participatory knowing
- [[Propositional Knowledge]] — first of the four; knowing-that, subject to truth and falsity
- [[Procedural Knowledge]] — second; knowing-how, embodied skill
- [[Perspectival Knowledge]] — third; knowing what it's like, irreducible to propositions
- [[Participatory Knowledge]] — fourth and deepest; knowing through identity and mutual transformation
- [[Tripartite Psyche]] — Plato's three-part soul: reason (man), thymos (lion), appetite (monster)
- [[Thymos]] — the spirited part; motivated by honor and shame; operates in the social-cultural domain
- [[Hyperbolic Discounting]] — explains why the appetite so reliably overrides reason; introduced via Ainslie
- [[Logos]] — the structural-functional organization of a thing; simultaneously what makes it be what it is and how we can know it
- [[Anagoge]] — developed as Plato's term for the upward transformative movement from shadows to the Good
- [[Myth of the Cave]] — introduced as the mythological representation of anagogic transformation

## Thinkers & traditions
- [[Plato]] — primary figure; creates the first psychological theory and the anagogic framework
- [[Platonism]] — the tradition inaugurated here
