# Ep 16 - Christianity and Agape

## What this lecture argues
Ep 16 deepens the account of Agape and introduces **Gnosis** as participatory knowing-through-love. Agape is not merely one kind of love among three; it is the pre-existent creative process by which non-persons become persons — it preceded us, flows through us, and makes us conduits for others' personhood. Agape therefore has a sacrificial component (for-giving: giving before it is earned) and is the condition of forgiveness. Paul's famous 1 Corinthians 13 passage is read not as romantic love but as an account of how Agapic knowing transforms identity, enabling one to "know as one is known" — full participatory conformity (Gnosis) rather than propositional knowledge. Paul's own conversion (Saul → Paul) is the paradigmatic personal Kairos: a higher state of consciousness, followed by receiving Agape from those he persecuted, followed by desert reflection, yielding a new identity. However, Paul's projection danger is noted: the degree to which one is self-ignorant, participatory knowing is darkened by projection. Paul projects his inner conflict onto the cosmos — hence God's inner conflict between justice and Agape, old man and new man. This grammar (of Agape, of personhood, of redemption from inner conflict) has become embedded in Western culture; we carry its expectations without the metaphysical machinery, which is part of the meaning crisis.

## Concepts introduced or developed
- [[Gnosis]] — introduced: participatory knowing bound up with Agapic love; knowing-as-being-known; transformation of identity into conformity with what is loved; distinct from propositional or mystical/magical knowing
- [[Agape]] — deepened: Agape pre-exists the individual and flows through them; giving before earning ("for-giving"); sacrificial because it creates the condition for another's personhood; Paul's passage as the clearest articulation; the danger of projection in participatory knowing
- [[Metanoia]] — deepened: personalization of the Exodus — old Saul (law, justice, condemnation) → new Paul (Agape, freedom); the inner-conflict narrative as Christianity's grammar of transformation
