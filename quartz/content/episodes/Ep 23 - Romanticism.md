# Ep 23 - Romanticism

## What this lecture argues
Ep 23 traces the Romantic movement as the first pseudo-religious ideology: a pan-European attempt to recover lost contact with reality by moving from rational cognition into the irrational imagination. Rooted in Kant's insight that the mind imposes structure on experience (making reality recede into the unknowable "Thing in Itself"), the Romantics invert Kant — they seek reality by moving away from rationality rather than toward it. This produces "decadent Romanticism" still operative in romantic love, but it fails to provide practices, institutions, or systematic psycho-technologies. Schopenhauer deepens the Romantic intuition into the Will to Live — a blind, irrational drive that frames all experience and is exposed as the root of nihilism. Nietzsche attempts to redeem the will as self-transcendence (Will to Power) but cannot provide machinery for overcoming self-deception, and so his project remains dangerously one-sided. Together, Romanticism and nihilism become inextricably linked cultural forces.

## Concepts introduced or developed
- [[Romanticism]] — developed as a tradition and as a pseudo-religious ideology; analysed using the Kantian framework
- [[Nihilism]] — introduced fully here via Schopenhauer and Nietzsche
- [[Imagination (Romantic)]] — the Romantic faculty between perception and reason, medium for making contact with reality outside of logic
- [[Will to Live]] — Schopenhauer's irrational, framing, auto-poetic drive that filters all experience
- [[Pseudo-Religious Ideology]] — coined: a secular movement that performs the functions of religion but without transformative psycho-technologies
