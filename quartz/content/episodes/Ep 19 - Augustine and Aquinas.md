# Ep 19 - Augustine and Aquinas

## What this lecture argues
Augustine is the pivot-point where the entire ancient meaning-structure achieves its most powerful synthesis. His personal crisis — sexual addiction, Manichaean worldview, mystical ascent through Plotinus he cannot hold — drives him to a crucial insight: at the heart of reason is love, and what addiction damaged is his capacity to love, not his capacity to reason. Agape (Christianity) can heal what Neoplatonism alone could not. He integrates the three orders — Nomological (Aristotle's account of world-structure), Normative (Neoplatonic anagoge from less real to more real), and Narrative (Christian history moving toward consummation) — into a mutually supporting synthesis. This corresponds exactly to the three dimensions of meaning that modern psychology identifies: coherence, significance, and purpose. The Confessions is not a dry treatise but an autobiography — participatory, perspectival Gnosis modelling how the reader can go through the same transformation. This synthesis sustains civilization for a thousand years.

Then the fracturing begins. The Great Schism (1054) separates East and West; the West loses its deep Neoplatonic mystical theology. A shift in reading practices follows: from oral, communal, transformative Lectio Divina — which cultivates procedural, perspectival, and participatory knowing — to silent, individual, propositional reading aimed at inner logical coherence. Avicenna (Neoplatonic) is replaced by Averroes (purely Aristotelian); priority shifts to definitions and propositions. This produces the "intensive self" (Kranz): the self conceived as inner coherent language rather than as trans-projectively connected to the world. Aristotle is rediscovered through the Crusades, and Aquinas faces the task of integrating him with Christianity. His solution — distinguishing natural (reason/science) from supernatural (faith/will) — is brilliant but fateful: it pulls love and reason apart, making faith the willful assertion of propositions rather than participatory knowing. Science and spirituality begin their divorce.

## Concepts introduced or developed
- [[Three Dimensions of Meaning]] — introduced: coherence (intelligibility), significance (mattering), purpose (direction); Heintzelman's empirical framework maps onto the Three Orders; Augustine's synthesis satisfied all three simultaneously
- [[Three Orders]] — deepened: Augustine achieves full integration of all three orders into a single mutually supporting synthesis; Aquinas begins their separation by segregating natural/supernatural
- [[Intensive Self]] — introduced: Kranz's term for the shift from the extensive self (trans-projectively connected to world via conformity) to the self conceived as inner propositional coherence; forerunner of Descartes
- [[Lectio Divina]] — introduced as practice: oral, communal, meditative engagement with sacred text as psycho-technology for perspectival-participatory transformation; explicitly contrasted with silent propositional reading
- [[Agape]] — deepened: Augustine's insight is that Agape heals the capacity to love that lies at the heart of reason; Gnosis requires Agape
- [[Gnosis]] — deepened: the Confessions is Gnosis through and through — first autobiography as participatory existential manual for self-transformation

## Thinkers & traditions
- [[Augustine of Hippo]] — primary figure; personal crisis, three-order synthesis, Confessions as Gnosis
- [[Thomas Aquinas]] — natural/supernatural distinction; love moves will not reason; initiates science-spirituality divorce
- [[Plotinus]], [[Paul of Tarsus]] — catalysts for Augustine's conversion and synthesis
- [[Neoplatonism]] — absorbed into Augustine's synthesis; partially lost in the West after 1054
