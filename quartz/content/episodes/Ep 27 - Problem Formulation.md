# Ep 27 - Problem Formulation

## What this lecture argues
Ep 27 demonstrates the profound power of Newell and Simon's Problem Space formalism by showing that combinatorial explosion makes algorithmic (certainty-seeking) processing impossible for virtually all real problems — vindicating the point that rationality cannot equal logic. Heuristics are necessary but biased. The critical step is exposing Newell and Simon's own essentialist heuristic: they assumed all problems are the same, trivialising problem formulation. In fact, most real-world problems are ill-defined: they lack clear initial states, goal states, operators, or path constraints. What's needed is Problem Formulation — the process of zeroing in on relevant information to construct a workable representation. The Mutilated Chessboard experiment demonstrates that shifting from a covering strategy (combinatorially explosive) to a parity strategy (salient colour difference) collapses the problem space instantly. This shift is insight, and insight is precisely the self-corrective, recursive reorganisation of Relevance Realization.

## Concepts introduced or developed
- [[Problem Formulation]] — introduced and centrally defined: the cognitive process of constructing a problem representation by zeroing in on relevant information; the missing piece in Newell and Simon
- [[Well-Defined vs Ill-Defined Problems]] — introduced: well-defined problems have clear IS/GS/operators; ill-defined ones require formulation before they can be solved
- [[Combinatorial Explosion]] — developed: used to show why algorithms cannot work for most real problems and why heuristics and insight are necessary
- [[Insight]] — developed: reframed as the conversion of poor problem formulation into good problem formulation; central to rationality alongside logic
- [[Heuristics]] — extended: the essentialist heuristic explains Newell and Simon's own blind spot
