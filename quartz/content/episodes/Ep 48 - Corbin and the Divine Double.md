# Ep 48 - Corbin and the Divine Double

## What this lecture argues
Ep 48 moves from Heidegger to Corbin, using the rose poem of Angelus Silesius (via Eckhart) as the bridge. The lecture develops three interlocking ideas. First, physis (non-teleological blossoming-from-itself) and Eckhart's "live without why" as the name for vertical ontological engagement — exapting narrative's training in non-logical identity toward depth rather than horizontal time. Second, Corbin's foundational distinction between the *imaginary* (purely subjective inner imagery) and the *imaginal* (transjective mediation between the abstract intelligible and concrete sensible worlds, constantly transformative transframing) — the imaginal is what makes gnosis possible, and literalism/fundamentalism destroys it. Third, the Mediterranean motif of the **divine double** (Stang): the self one aspires to become, normatively prior to the existing self, to be realized through aspirational gnosis — Corbin's "angel" is the imaginal symbol of this divine double, and the S1 → S2 aspiration structure (Callard) is its cognitive-science articulation.

**Physis and the rose**: Heidegger's commentary on Angelus Silesius's poem ("the rose is without why / it blooms because it blooms") draws on the Greek *physis* — blossoming forth from itself, pure self-grounded emergence. The rose shines (phenomenological appearance) while simultaneously withdrawing (its ground is inexhaustible). Eckhart's maxim "live without why" is a non-teleological way of being: not meaninglessness, but moving beyond the having-mode's narrative teleology. The universe itself may be "like the rose" — grounded in itself, combinatorially inexhaustible, more accurately represented by physis than by a grand purposive narrative (Ep 48).

**Narrative → non-logical identity → vertical ontology**: Narrative trains us in non-logical identity: John born in Hamilton ≠ John in Toronto, yet they are the same person. But this training in non-logical identity can be *exapted* from the horizontal time axis to the vertical ontological axis: instead of self-transcendence through narrative teleology (having a grand purpose), one engages the depths of one's own being in contact with the depths of Being itself. This is the being mode as vertical ontological movement — Stoic wisdom extended (stop seeking fame and glory; go deeper) (Ep 48).

**Imaginary vs. imaginal (Corbin)**: The imaginary is purely subjective inner imagery — controllable, private, known to be unreal. The imaginal is fundamentally different: it is *transjective*, mediating between (a) the abstract intelligible world and the concrete sensible world, AND (b) the subjective and the objective. The Cartesian grammar had severed both pairs (mind/matter; subject/object), eliminating the imaginal between them. Corbin argues this loss of the imaginal is the loss of gnosis. The imaginal is also not static: it is always in transformative transframing — a living, moving mediation. Fundamentalism and literalism freeze the imaginal, destroying it by locking everything into one pole or the other (Ep 48).

**Symbol in the imaginal mode**: Four properties of a symbol understood imaginalIy (not imaginarily): (1) *Translucent*: you look at it and simultaneously through it — both together, in dialogue; (2) *Transjective*: neither purely subjective nor purely objective; (3) *Trajective*: it puts you on a trajectory of transformation; (4) *Transformative*: it changes you at the identity level, not just epistemically. (5) *Transtemporal/transspatial*: the movement it enacts is ontological, not a narrative movement through time and space. If you lose the imaginal, you lose all of this — you can no longer do gnosis (Ep 48).

**Divine double**: Stang (*Our Divine Double*) identifies a widespread Mediterranean motif in the Hellenistic and post-Hellenistic period (Gnosticism, early Christianity, Manichaeism, Neoplatonism): the divine double — the self one is aspiring to become, which is archetypically more real than the current self; one's spiritual path is to realize the non-logical identity between the existing self (S1) and the divine double (S2/the angel). This directly subverts the decadent romantic grammar (true self is something you have and express); instead, the true self is something realized through being-mode self-transcendence. Corbin's "angel" is the imaginal symbol of the divine double — not a literally winged being but an imaginal representation of the aspirational self (Ep 48).

**Connection to aspiration (Callard)**: The S1 → S2 movement toward the divine double is the same structure as Callard's aspiration (Ep 45). Both Callard and LA Paul agree: this transition cannot be reasoned, deliberated, or decided into from the S1 standpoint. What bridges the gap is the symbol (the angel/divine double as placeholder) operating in the imaginal mode — a gnosis-as-serious-play that makes the non-logical identity workable. The divine double is the symbol that enables the aspirational trajectory of rationality and wisdom (Ep 48).
