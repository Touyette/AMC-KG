# Ep 08 - The Buddha and Mindfulness

## What this lecture argues
This episode pivots from the Greek Axial tradition to the Indian Axial Revolution, centering on Siddhartha Gautama as the embodiment of that revolution. The mythological biography of Siddhartha is read as a perennial narrative about modal confusion and its resolution: the palace represents total immersion in the having mode, and the Four Signs (sickness, old age, death, mendicant) shatter that immersion irreversibly. The Middle Path is introduced as the path between self-indulgence and self-denial — both of which remain in the having mode because both are framed around the self as something to be controlled or eliminated. Sati (mindfulness) is recovered in its original meaning: not merely "paying attention to the present moment" but remembering or recovering the being mode — a participatory, modal memory rather than a recollection of facts. The episode then begins a detailed cognitive scientific re-examination of mindfulness, distinguishing the language of training from the language of explaining, and distinguishing states (being present, non-judging) from traits (insightfulness, reduced reactivity). The Langer soft-vigilance model of attention is introduced, contrasting forced concentration with "inter-esse" (renewed interest). The episode ends with attention identified as an optimization process rather than a spotlight.
