# Ep 14 - Epicureans, Cynics, and Stoics

## What this lecture argues
Ep 14 turns from the Axial Revolution in India to its aftermath in the Mediterranean — the Hellenistic period of **Domicide**. Alexander's conquests shattered the Polis-rooted world, producing an age of anxiety: disconnection from place, government, language, history, and shared religion. Three philosophical schools respond to this crisis, each exemplifying Socrates' physician-of-the-soul model. The Epicureans diagnose anxiety about death and offer a therapy of mortality acceptance and friendship as the deepest source of meaning. The Cynics (Antisthenes → Diogenes) diagnose attachment to contingent, culturally-constructed goods; their response is radical enactment of natural and moral law over cultural purity codes, distinguishing guilt (violated moral principle) from shame (violated cultural purity code). The Stoics (Zeno) synthesize both: the problem is not *what* you set your heart on but *how* — mindless, reactive co-identification distorts the agent-arena relationship; the solution is attention to the process of co-identification itself, integrating Platonic rationality with Cynic provocation.

## Concepts introduced or developed
- [[Domicide]] — introduced: two forms — physical destruction of home, and cultural domicide (home exists but no longer feels like home); drives the Hellenistic meaning crisis; resonates with modernity's cosmic un-homing
- [[Existential Anxiety]] — introduced via Tillich: fear = observable, determinate threat (you know what to do); anxiety = nebulous threat (you don't know what to do); the Hellenistic and modern meaning crises are fundamentally anxiety crises, not fear crises
- [[Agent-Arena Relationship]] — deepened: Stoic insight that mindless co-identification MARs the formation of identity; attention to the process of assuming and assigning identities is the key to maintaining agency under domicide
