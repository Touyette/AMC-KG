# Ep 41 - What is Rationality

## What this lecture argues
Ep 41 deepens the Rationality Debate by working through three responses to the experimental evidence for systematic human irrationality, arriving at the conclusion that rationality cannot be identified with either logical competence or intelligence, and that its missing piece is a non-inferential normativity on construal plus a cultivated cognitive style (active open-mindedness).

**Stanovich and West vs. Cohen — errors are systematic, not performance errors**: Cohen claimed all experimental failures are performance errors (tiredness, distraction) not competence failures. Stanovich and West's decisive reply: performance errors are *not systematic* — they vary with circumstances. Competence failures *are* systematic — they form a positive manifold across tasks (like Piaget's conservation failures in children). The reasoning tasks (lily pad, argument evaluation, belief perseverance) do form a strong positive manifold (gr). Therefore the errors are at the competence level, not performance level. Cohen's argument is correct that we must be the source of our standards, but his conclusion that all errors are performance errors rests on two hidden premises: that competence is (a) single and (b) static. Both are wrong. We have multiple competencies (dual processing) that can conflict with each other, and those competencies develop over time (Ep 41).

**Cherniak's Finitary Predicament**: Cherniak's independent argument: formal logic and probability theory cannot be the normative standard for human rationality because applying them comprehensively within the finitary predicament would produce combinatorial explosion — cognitive suicide. The right response to the finitary predicament is relevance realization. Therefore the scientists are imposing the wrong normative theory. Stanovich and West's rebuttal: Cherniak is correct that computational limitations are real — but this describes *intelligence* (the capacity to deal with computational limitations = relevance realization = g), not rationality. Intelligence and rationality are empirically distinct: the correlation between g and gr (the general factor of reasoning performance) is at best 0.3. Intelligence is necessary but far from sufficient for rationality (Ep 41).

**Smedslund's fallacy vs. misunderstanding**: A third response from Smedslund (1970): interpreting the experiments as showing fallacy (correct understanding + wrong inference) is not cleanly separable from misunderstanding (wrong construal + correct inference given that construal). Understanding itself involves drawing the right identities, contradictions, and implications — i.e., reasoning. So if participants understand the problem correctly, they reason in a scientist-like way to formulate it; but then they reason non-scientist-like when solving it. This pragmatic inconsistency makes the fallacy attribution problematic (Ep 41).

**Normativity on construal**: Stanovich and West's proposed solution to the impasse: we need an *independent* normativity on how problems are *construed* (sized up, formulated) — one that doesn't rely on inferential norms to evaluate itself. Vervaeke: this normativity is already studied in insight problem-solving research. A bad construal = combinatorial explosion, misleading salience, unresolvable ill-definedness. A good construal = manageable search space, relevant constraints, well-defined structure. Therefore *insight* (non-inferential) is a core component of rationality alongside *inference*. Rationality = insight + inference, not inference alone (Ep 41).

**Active open-mindedness — the missing piece**: The cognitive style that predicts rationality (beyond g): Baron's active open-mindedness — trained dispositional sensitivity to one's own biases (confirmation bias, essentialism bias, availability bias) plus active counteraction of them, to the right degree (not maximized, which would trigger combinatorial explosion). Predicted by *need for cognition*: curiosity about problems + wonder. This connects directly to Stoic self-examination and CBT. The more rationality is understood this way, the more it overlaps with wisdom (Ep 41).
