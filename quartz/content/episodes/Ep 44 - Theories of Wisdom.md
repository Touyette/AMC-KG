# Ep 44 - Theories of Wisdom

## What this lecture argues
Ep 44 critiques three wisdom theories (Baltes & Staudinger, Ardelt, Sternberg) using RR machinery, introduces the crucial product/process theory distinction, and converges on the claim that wisdom requires understanding (interpretive knowledge), agape, multi-perspectival self-reflection, and dynamic optimization — all presupposing relevance realization.

**Product theory vs. process theory**: Vervaeke's central meta-level critique of modern psychological theories of wisdom: they are all *product theories* — they specify what wisdom looks like (its features, criteria) but not how one becomes wise. A *process theory* begins with: (a) what is foolishness? (b) how does it develop? (c) how can it be overcome? (d) what flourishing does overcoming it afford? Ancient wisdom traditions (Platonic, Buddhist, Stoic) are process theories. Both are needed — product and process theories should be in dialogue with each other. This is Vervaeke and Ferraro (2013)'s central insight (Ep 44).

**Further criticism of Baltes & Staudinger**: Boundedness by Simon's bounded rationality is explicitly cited, and metaheuristics as compressed relevance realization are shown in their own quotes ("reduced quickly to their essentials without being lost in the never-ending process of information search"). Main criticism: product theory only. Secondarily: doesn't thematize perspectival and participatory knowing explicitly, despite experimental evidence (Solomon effect) pointing to their centrality (Ep 44).

**Ardelt's critique and three-dimensional model**: Ardelt (2004)'s most important critique of Baltes & Staudinger: they confuse having theoretical knowledge about wisdom with being a wise person (modal confusion). Descriptive/propositional knowledge about wisdom is necessary but nowhere near sufficient. The wise person must *realize* these truths within a process of self-transformation — embodying and enacting them, not just possessing them propositionally. Three mutually influencing dimensions: (1) **Cognitive**: grasping the *significance* (not just the fact) of information — Kekes's *interpretive knowledge* vs. descriptive knowledge; points to understanding + RR. (2) **Reflective**: multi-perspectival, self-examination, self-awareness, self-insight; perspectival knowing turned recursively on oneself; overcomes egocentrism perspectivally. (3) **Affective**: compassion, best understood as agape; overcomes egocentrism motivationally by orienting care toward the conditions for others' flourishing. Cognitive + Reflective together overcome egocentrism from two directions. Agape → meaning in life (Wolf's connection): wisdom is not reducible to virtue; it also connects to meaning in life through the affective dimension (Ep 44). Criticisms of Ardelt: lacks explicit account of transformative experience; still more product than process theory; no independent account of foolishness.

**Sternberg's Balance Theory (1998)**: Wisdom as balance (= optimization, not spatial balancing) of intrapersonal, interpersonal, and extrapersonal interests toward adapting to, shaping, and selecting environments — mediated by tacit understanding (implicit processing, grasping significance). The intrapersonal/interpersonal/extrapersonal triangle ≈ the three dimensions of meaning in life (connection to self, others, world). Balance = Piaget's equilibration (assimilation ↔ accommodation); coping with novelty vs. proceduralization. Tacit understanding = implicit comprehension of significance, directly invoking RR. Directed toward virtue + meaning in life (Vervaeke replaces Sternberg's "common good"). Values run alongside but are underspecified. Criticisms: product theory only; foolishness ≠ imbalance (that's circular); "common good" is anachronistic to specific cultures; tacit understanding/knowledge needs more explicit development (Ep 44).
