# Ep 43 - Wisdom and Virtue

## What this lecture argues
Ep 43 establishes the deep connection between wisdom and virtue by working through Schwartz & Sharpe's argument about the inadequacy of virtue lists, introduces phronesis/sophia as the Aristotelian framework for wisdom, critiques the expertise model of phronesis, and presents the Berlin Wisdom Paradigm (Baltes & Staudinger) as the first major empirical theory of wisdom — finding it deeply reliant on relevance realization.

**Wisdom and virtue — the problem of relevance, conflict, specification, and development**: Schwartz & Sharpe (2006) argue that virtue lists (Peterson & Seligman's positive psychology catalog) are feature lists that treat virtues as logically independent — which leads to the false implication that one should maximize each virtue. But virtues conflict (honesty vs. kindness; mentoring vs. strict objectivity) and require contextual judgment about which is relevant. Three problems: (1) **Relevance** — which virtue applies here?; (2) **Conflict** — how to balance opposing virtues?; (3) **Specification** — rules like "be kind" cannot specify their own application conditions. Vervaeke adds (4) **Development** — sometimes the best response is to recognize one lacks a needed virtue and must aspire to cultivate it. The higher-order ability that addresses all four is wisdom (specifically phronesis). Virtues are not independent but interdependent — every virtue is a way of being wise in a particular kind of situation (Ep 43).

**Sophia vs. phronesis — opponent relationship**: Schwartz & Sharpe correctly identify phronesis (contextual sensitivity, situational judgment, know-how) as central to virtue but downplay sophia (cross-contextual principles, ontological depth perception). Vervaeke argues we need both in an opponent relationship: sophia = awareness of cross-contextual principles (not rules, not propositions alone — a deep perception of generalizable structural features); phronesis = contextual sensitivity that puts principles into process. Good wisdom: principles → processes (putting principles into practice) AND processes → principles (practicing in a principled manner). Neither alone is sufficient (Ep 43).

**Phronesis ≠ expertise**: Schwartz & Sharpe describe phronesis as expertise. Vervaeke argues this conflates contextual sensitivity with domain-specificity. Expertise is domain-specific (tennis expertise doesn't transfer to squash; may interfere). Phronesis must be domain-general (I can be foolish in every domain simultaneously; wisdom should help in every domain). Domain-general capacities — intelligence, rationality, relevance realization — model phronesis better than expertise (Ep 43).

**Berlin Wisdom Paradigm (Baltes & Staudinger)**: Wisdom as a "metaheuristic (pragmatic) to orchestrate mind and virtue toward excellence" — a heuristic for managing heuristics that underlies excellent human functioning. Five criteria: (a) rich factual knowledge about fundamental pragmatics of life; (b) rich procedural knowledge about fundamental pragmatics of life; (c) lifespan contextualism (perspectival knowing — zoom out for the big picture, zoom in for the particular); (d) relativism of values and priorities (Vervaeke critiques: substitute fallibilism + humility); (e) recognition and management of uncertainty (finitary predicament). Empirical method: give people challenging dilemmas, have trained judges evaluate responses against the five criteria. This initiated the empirical study of wisdom (Ep 43).

**Solomon Effect — dialogical wisdom**: Baltes & Staudinger experimental finding: discussing problems with a significant other, OR simulating/imagining an internal dialogue, both outperform having additional time alone. Vervaeke reads this as evidence for the Solomon effect: first-person framing locks us inside our own perspective (our framing is transparent to us — we see through it, not at it); third-person or dialogical perspective breaks this lock, making salient what was invisible from within. Practically: internalizing others (the Stoic sage, Socratic dialogue) can be as effective as actual dialogue (Ep 43).
