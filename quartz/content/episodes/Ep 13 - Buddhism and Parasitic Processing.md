# Ep 13 - Buddhism and Parasitic Processing

## What this lecture argues
Ep 13 uses the Buddha's post-enlightenment teaching to show how higher states of consciousness translate into cognitive science — and vice versa. It opens with Batchelor's interpretation-crisis argument: trying to understand Buddhism either purely from within a tradition (myopic, belief-fixated) or purely from outside (loses transformative relevance) both fail; what's needed is a way to engage that is simultaneously transformatively relevant and intellectually rigorous. This mirrors the Socratic project. The Four Noble Truths are then reframed as **Four Ennobling Provocations** — not propositions to believe but prompts to enact transformation. The central mechanism of suffering is reframed as **Dukkha** (loss of agency/entrapment, not pain) and explained cognitively through two interlocking spirals: **Parasitic Processing** (internal — the adaptive cognitive machinery of heuristics and self-organization turns self-destructive through negative feedback) and **Reciprocal Narrowing** (Marc Lewis — external — the agent-arena relationship narrows until no alternatives remain, as in addiction). The Eightfold Path is then described as the Buddha's response: a counteractive dynamical system that simultaneously intervenes across cognition, character, and consciousness.

## Concepts introduced or developed
- [[Parasitic Processing]] — introduced: the adaptive cognitive machinery (heuristics, representativeness, availability, confirmation bias, encoding specificity, complexity) turns against the agent in a self-reinforcing spiral ending in fatalism and loss of agency
- [[Dukkha]] — deepened (introduced Ep 08): reframed as loss of agency and entrapment (wheel off its axis), not as pain; connected to the monkey-and-pitch parable; all of life is *threatened by* Dukkha, not *constituted by* suffering
- [[Reciprocal Narrowing]] — introduced: Marc Lewis's model of addiction as learned agent-arena narrowing; as cognitive flexibility decreases, the world's options decrease reciprocally; addiction is participatory-perspectival learning of no-agency; the inverse of anagoge
- [[Four Noble Truths]] — deepened (introduced Ep 08): reframed via Batchelor as Four Ennobling Provocations; the point is to enact the transformation, not to assent to propositions; third truth = recover agency; fourth = counteractive dynamical system
