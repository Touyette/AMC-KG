# Ep 21 - Martin Luther and Descartes

## What this lecture argues
Ep 21 traces how two titanic responses to the emerging meaning crisis — the Protestant Reformation and Cartesian rationalism — share a common grammar: the isolated individual mind as the final authority. Luther interiorizes salvation into individual conscience, abolishes the monastery-university pairing that kept wisdom institutionalized (Sapiential Obsolescence), and inadvertently trains cultural narcissism by teaching that we are inherently worthless yet deserving of unearned positive regard. Descartes responds to the same anxiety by proposing that the mind, turned into a machine of certainty through mathematical method, can find stable ground — inventing Cartesian graphing as a new psycho-technology. Hobbes radicalizes this: if cognition is computation, a material machine could be made to think, which threatens to eliminate the soul entirely. The episode establishes that the scientific revolution and the Protestant Reformation must be read together as co-authors of the meaning crisis.

## Concepts introduced or developed
- [[Sapiential Obsolescence]] — coined here: the loss of institutions dedicated to wisdom cultivation, leaving knowledge bound only to political power
- [[Protestant Work Ethic]] — introduced as an unofficial response to the anxiety of not knowing whether one is saved
- [[Individual Conscience]] — Luther's elevation of the private inner authority to the sole arbiter of spiritual truth
- [[Mind-Body Problem]] — set up by Hobbes's proposal of Artificial Intelligence and Descartes's counter-arguments (developed fully in Ep 22)
- [[Psycho-Technology]] — extended: Cartesian graphing identified as one of history's most powerful psycho-technologies
- [[Relevance Realization]] — implicitly prefigured in the contrast between Cartesian certainty and the richer knowing Pascal later defends
