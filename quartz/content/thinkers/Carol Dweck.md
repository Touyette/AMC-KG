# Carol Dweck

> Professor of Psychology at Stanford; developed the fixed/malleable mindset framework; demonstrates the existential dimension of intelligence and rationality.

## Role in the argument
Dweck's research is used by Vervaeke to demonstrate that rationality is an existential issue — the way one identifies with one's own cognitive processes profoundly affects reasoning ability, problem-seeking, and self-deception. Her experiments show that fixed mindset children (who treat intelligence as a static trait) avoid challenge, attribute error to permanent defects, produce self-deception (lying about performance), and perform worse after encountering hard problems. Malleable mindset children do the opposite — they seek challenge, attend to process, and improve (Ep 42).

The key manipulating variable — whether an experimenter praises the trait ("you're so smart") or the process ("you worked really hard") — shows that the existential orientation toward one's intelligence is trainable and context-sensitive, not merely a personality trait. This has direct implications for education, parenting, and any practice designed to cultivate rationality.

Vervaeke uses Dweck to bridge Stanovich's dual processing account of rationality (a cognitive-scientific account) and the claim that rationality is deeply tied to identity and mode of being — making it inseparable from the project of wisdom (Ep 42).
