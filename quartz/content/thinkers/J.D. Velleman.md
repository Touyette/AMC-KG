# J.D. Velleman

> American philosopher (b. 1952); developed the concept of the reflectiveness gap (the "Hamlet problem"); argues that the capacity for self-reflection can undermine rather than enable decisive commitment; his work on self-constitution and agency is important for Vervaeke's account of the perennial problem of the reflectiveness gap.

## Role in the argument
Velleman enters AMC through his analysis of what Vervaeke calls the [[Reflectiveness Gap]] — the way that the distinctly human capacity for self-reflection can become a source of paralysis rather than freedom. The Hamlet problem: Hamlet is paralyzed not because he can't figure out what to do but because his very capacity to reflect on his motivations, obligations, and the consequences of action prevents him from committing (Ep 36, Ep 42).

**The Hamlet problem**: Velleman's version of the reflectiveness gap: the human capacity for second-order desires (desires about desires) and self-conscious deliberation means that one can always step back from any first-order commitment and ask "but should I really be committed to this?" This stepping back, indefinitely iterable, can produce a paralysis of reflection in which no commitment feels fully grounded. The more reflectively aware one is, the more susceptible to this paralysis (Ep 36).

**Self-constitution**: Velleman's constructive response: the self is constituted through its commitments — there is no pre-given self that then makes choices, but a self that emerges through and is shaped by the choices it makes. This creates a circularity that is not vicious but constitutive: one makes commitments, those commitments constitute who one is, and that constitution grounds further commitments. The reflectiveness gap is addressed not by finding an unquestionable foundation but by the aspiration structure: committing in order to become the self that can endorse the commitment (Ep 42).

**Connection to aspiration and transformation**: Velleman's analysis of self-constitution connects to Callard's aspiration and Vervaeke's account of wisdom: becoming wise requires committed practice before one is wise enough to fully endorse the practice; becoming a person of good character requires habituating virtue before one has the character that would naturally choose virtue. Self-constitution is always proleptic — ahead of where one currently is (Ep 42, Ep 45).
