# Keiji Nishitani

> Japanese philosopher of the Kyoto School; student of Nishida Kitaro; author of *Religion and Nothingness*; in AMC known for the Buddhist parallel to Tillich's no-thingness: the aspect-identity shift of shunyata as inexhaustible source of meaning.

## Role in the argument
Nishitani appears in AMC primarily through Tillich's discussion of the no-thingness of God. Vervaeke invokes *Religion and Nothingness* as an extended philosophical examination of the same aspect-identity shift that Tillich makes from the Christian side: when one deeply participates the no-thingness of shunyata (Buddhist emptiness), rather than experiencing nihilism or void, one encounters an inexhaustible source of meaning cultivation that cannot be drained by despair (Ep 50).

**The parallel to Tillich**: Where Tillich argues that the no-thingness of God overcomes the nothingness of despair via an aspect-identity shift, Nishitani argues the same for shunyata: when you stop trying to push away the nothingness and instead participate it imaginally, it reveals itself as pointing to an inexhaustible ground of meaning. The nothingness of nihilism is not the same as the no-thingness of the Buddhist emptiness properly understood.

**The critique of Nietzsche**: Nishitani argues that Nietzsche came close to this insight ("if you stare long enough into the abyss, it begins to stare back at you") but did not stare long enough. Nietzsche felt the abyss but could not make the shift into the non-thingness that reveals itself as inexhaustible. This is Nishitani's analysis of Nietzschean nihilism (Ep 50).

Nishitani thus provides the Buddhist corroboration of Tillich's non-theism: the same move (aspect-identity shift from nothingness-of-despair to no-thingness-as-inexhaustible-ground) is available within a non-theistic framework, confirming that this is a structural feature of serious engagement with the meaning crisis, not a distinctively Christian response.
