# Keith Stanovich

> Emeritus Professor of Applied Psychology at University of Toronto; central contributor to the rationality debate; coined *dysrationalia*; developed the intelligence/rationality dissociation.

## Role in the argument
Stanovich is Vervaeke's primary interlocutor for the scientific account of rationality and its relationship to wisdom. His key contribution to the rationality debate: demonstrating with West (2000) that the errors in reasoning experiments form a strong positive manifold (gr), proving they are systematic (competence-level) failures, not performance errors as Cohen claimed. This demolishes Cohen's conclusion while preserving his insight (that we are the source of our standards) by introducing multiple, developmental competencies rather than a single, static one (Ep 40, 41).

Stanovich's most important empirical finding: the correlation between general intelligence (g) and reasoning performance (gr) is at best 0.3. Intelligence and rationality are therefore dissociable — one can be highly intelligent and poorly rational (*dysrationalia*) because the very processes that make us intelligent (heuristic relevance realization) are the same processes that, when unchecked by a second system, generate systematic self-deception (Ep 41).

His proposed account of what makes someone rational beyond intelligence: (a) *mindware* — the quality of the psycho-technologies one has internalized; and (b) a *cognitive style* — active open-mindedness (from Baron). The connection to wisdom deepens: rationality on this account is a cultivated disposition of self-corrective attention, not a static competence (Ep 41).

Stanovich also names the need for *normativity on construal* — an independent standard for how problems are formulated, not just how inferences are drawn. He leaves this largely open; Vervaeke fills it with insight problem-solving research and relevance realization (Ep 41).
