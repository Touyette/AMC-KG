# Martin Buber

> Austrian-Jewish philosopher; developed the I-Thou vs. I-It distinction (being mode vs. having mode); provides a key criticism of Jung's intra-psychism.

## Role in the argument
Buber's primary contribution to AMC is twofold: (1) the I-Thou / I-It distinction (introduced earlier in the series), which parallels Fromm's being mode / having mode distinction and Heidegger's aletheia vs. forgetfulness of Being; (2) a pointed criticism of Jung's individuation as intra-psychic.

**Criticism of Jung**: Buber argues that Jung's Kantianism traps the imaginal within the subject — individuation is understood as something happening inside the psyche rather than as a genuine transjective encounter with what transcends the psyche. Jung's model can't properly account for the genuine I-Thou encounter — the mode of being in which the other (person, world, God) is encountered as genuinely other, irreducibly beyond my projections and inner contents. For Buber, this is a form of psychic narcissism: the apparent encounter with the Self/sacred second self is actually just an encounter with deeper layers of oneself, never genuinely exceeding the ego's sphere. Corbin makes a related criticism from the Neoplatonic direction: the imaginal should disclose the depths of *the world*, not only the depths of *the psyche* (Ep 49).

**Convergence with Vervaeke**: Buber's I-Thou relation is the being mode encounter — participatory, transjective, not manipulative. The loss of I-Thou in favor of I-It is the modal confusion at the heart of the meaning crisis. This connects to Heidegger's forgetfulness of Being and to Vervaeke's analysis of having mode dominance (Ep 49).
