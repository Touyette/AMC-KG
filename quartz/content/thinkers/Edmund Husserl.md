# Edmund Husserl

> Founder of phenomenology; argued that modern philosophy had severed us from direct contact with the world and that disciplined attention to intentionality could restore it.

## Role in the argument
Husserl represents one of the first major systematic responses to what Vervaeke calls the meaning crisis. His project — phenomenology — is an attempt to restore a **contact epistemology**: to get back to "the things themselves" after Cartesian and Kantian philosophy had erected a wall of subjective representations between mind and world. Husserl explicitly names the crisis of his era in his late work *Crisis of European Sciences*, and *Cartesian Meditations* shows his attempt at a rigorous first-person foundation for knowledge (Ep 46).

The core tool is **intentionality**: the observation that all consciousness is directed — every mental act (perception, thought, desire) is *of* or *toward* something. Husserl analyzes this directedness into *noesis* (the act of intending) and *noema* (the object-as-intended). The phenomenological attitude is not ordinary introspection — it is a disciplined, reflective, experimental, exploratory attention to the transjective structure of experience itself, probing how world is disclosed through consciousness and how consciousness is always already oriented toward world (Ep 46).

Using Vervaeke's vocabulary: phenomenology is a probative attention paid to perspectival knowing of the transjective relationship. This is why 4E cognitive science is deeply influenced by phenomenology — both model cognition as fundamentally relational and world-engaging, not as inner-representation followed by outer-inference.

**Heidegger's critique**: Husserl, despite his aims, remained trapped within the Cartesian cultural cognitive grammar — still operating from within subjectivity, making phenomenology ultimately a form of idealism rather than the realism it promised. More fundamentally, Husserl did not account for *participatory knowing*: the modal way in which agent and arena are constitutively engaged before any act of intentional directedness occurs. Without this ground, the intentional structure floats without contact. Merleau-Ponty partially repairs this via his account of embodied perception (Ep 46).
