# Graham Harman

> American philosopher (b. 1968); developer of Object-Oriented Ontology (OOO); argues that objects always withdraw from all relations and access — they can never be fully reduced to their effects, relations, or descriptions; for Vervaeke, provides the ontological vocabulary for the "thing beyond itself" structure of sacredness.

## Role in the argument
Harman enters AMC through his concept of object withdrawal — the idea that every object exceeds all our access to it, that there is always an irreducible surplus beyond what is registered in any relation or description. Vervaeke connects this to the [[Thing Beyond Itself]] structure: the experience of sacredness involves encountering an object as more than it appears — as containing an inexhaustible depth that withdraws from any complete grasp (Ep 47).

**Object withdrawal**: Harman argues (against what he calls "undermining" — reducing objects to their components — and "overmining" — reducing objects to their effects and relations) that objects are real but withdrawn: they exist independently of any relation, including all human cognition of them. The moon is not reducible to its physical composition (undermining) or to what it does in various contexts (overmining): it is a real object that exceeds all its relations. This withdrawal is not a defect of our knowledge but an ontological feature of objects as such (Ep 47).

**The thing beyond itself**: The "thing beyond itself" is Harman's phrase (developed from Heidegger) for the way objects point beyond their present appearance to an inexhaustible depth. A hammer is not just a hammer-in-use or a hammer-at-hand; it has a kind of reality that exceeds all our dealings with it — which becomes dramatically apparent when it breaks. This structure connects to Vervaeke's account of sacredness as the experience of transjectivity: the quality of inexhaustibleness that generates the religious response (Ep 47).

**Connection to Heidegger**: Harman develops his OOO partly from Heidegger's analysis of ready-to-hand vs. present-at-hand: ordinary use (ready-to-hand) does not reveal the full reality of a thing, and breakdown (revealing it as present-at-hand) gives a different but also incomplete access. Harman radicalizes this: all access is partial; withdrawal is the fundamental ontological condition of objects (Ep 47).
