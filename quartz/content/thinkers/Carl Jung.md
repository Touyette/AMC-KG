# Carl Jung

> Swiss psychiatrist and psychoanalyst; one of the four prophets of the meaning crisis; argues that modern man has lost his soul (= his relationship to the sacred second self); develops individuation as the psychological process of aspiration toward the Self.

## Role in the argument
Jung enters AMC's prophets arc through the bridge of the sacred second self: his *Modern Man in Search of a Soul* addresses the same meaning crisis Vervaeke has been tracking — modern life has severed the individual's connection to the depth dimension of the psyche. For Jung, "soul" names the relationship to what Vervaeke calls the sacred second self (Ep 49).

**Against Freud**: Freud's hydraulic model treats the psyche as a Newtonian pressure machine (steam engine logic: pressure builds, seeks release, drives behavior). Jung replaces this with an **organic/autopoietic model**: the psyche is a self-organizing dynamical system that neither makes itself by pure activity nor merely receives its form from outside, but *participates in its own emergence*. This makes individuation an aspirational process, not a pressure-management process (Ep 49).

**Archetypes**: Jung's psychological analog of Platonic forms. Archetypes are structural-functional organizations — virtual engines — that regulate how the psyche self-organizes by shaping salience landscaping. They are NOT mere mental images (that is the imaginary misreading). The hero archetype active in someone means their salience landscape is being reorganized heroically, not that they carry hero-images. Archetypes are best understood adverbially (a way of anagogically coming-to-be) and as imaginal entities (transjective, mediating between intelligible and sensible, not subjective fantasies). They have a life of their own within the self-organizing psyche (Ep 49).

**Ego and Self**: The ego is the archetype of the conscious mind. The Self (capital S) is the archetype of the archetypes — like Plato's Form of the Good (the form that regulates how all other forms are forms), the Self is the ultimate virtual engine that constellates all other virtual engines so the psyche can continue its autopoietic self-organization. The Self is what Vervaeke calls the sacred second self (Ep 49).

**Individuation**: The lifelong process of the ego entering into dialogue with the Self through the imaginal archetypes. As the ego engages an archetype imaginally, that engagement is internalized, transforming the ego's perspectival knowing and participatory being → the ego indwells the world differently → it can better engage the archetype → etc. This anagogic loop is individuation. It is neither passive reception nor active making but participation in emergence. Active imagination is the psychotechnology Jung develops to facilitate this (Ep 49).

**Shared with Corbin and Barfield**: All three oppose fundamentalism and literalism for the same reason: literalism reduces the imaginal to the imaginary, causing psychic inflation (the ego pretends to be complete in itself rather than aspiring to the Self). Jung is deeply influenced by Gnosis, Kant, and the Romantic tradition. He and Corbin met regularly at the Eranos conferences (Ep 49).

**Jung's limitation (Buber's and Corbin's criticism)**: Jung's Kantianism leads him to treat individuation as fundamentally intra-psychic — happening within the subject. For most of his writing, the archetypes are understood ultimately as subjective rather than transjective. Corbin: this reduces the imaginal to the imaginary — the mystical should disclose the depths of the *world*, not just the psyche. Buber: Jung can't properly account for the I-Thou encounter because he lacks a way of representing the transjective relationship. Vervaeke: Jung correctly provides the psychology (how internalization works) that Corbin lacks; Corbin provides the transjective/world-disclosive dimension (how individuation engages reality, not just inner contents) that Jung undersells (Ep 49).
