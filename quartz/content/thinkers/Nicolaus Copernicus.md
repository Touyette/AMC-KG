# Nicolaus Copernicus

> The astronomer who shows that all the Aristotelian marks of good knowing can be satisfied — clear senses, rational agreement, no distorting medium — and we can still be systematically wrong; experience is a veil, not contact with reality.

## Role in the argument
Vervaeke's use of Copernicus is primarily epistemological rather than astronomical (Ep 20). The claim that the Earth orbits the Sun is not what matters most; what matters is what this implies about the reliability of experience.

The Aristotelian marks of knowing are: the relevant sense organ functions; the medium is not distorting; after rational discussion, other people agree. These are the criteria by which one determines whether a perception is real. Now consider geocentrism: we all watch the sun rise in the East and set in the West. Our eyes work perfectly. The air is clear. There is calm, sober agreement after rational discussion. Every Aristotelian criterion is satisfied. And we are wrong. It is an illusion. Copernicus shows — without being able to answer any of the classical objections to heliocentrism — that the math is simply better when you put the Sun at the center. And that is sufficient for the new way of thinking.

The epistemological implication is catastrophic: *if all the normal marks of knowing can be satisfied and we're still wrong*, then ordinary experience does not put us in contact with the world. Experience is a **veil** between mind and world, not a window onto it. The only narrow channel that breaks through is mathematics. Everything else — all the richness of perceptual experience — is untrustworthy as a guide to reality. This is the beginning of the experience-as-veil problem that Galileo will formalize with the primary/secondary quality distinction: if experience doesn't track reality, and only math does, then all the non-mathematical qualities of experience (color, beauty, meaning, purpose) are not in the world (Ep 20).

Vervaeke notes that Copernicus himself does not fully appreciate these implications. He simply prefers the math.
