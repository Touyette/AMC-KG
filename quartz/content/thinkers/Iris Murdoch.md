# Iris Murdoch

> British philosopher and novelist (1919–1999); developed a Platonic moral philosophy centered on "moral attention" — the quality of one's seeing as the primary moral act; articulated the "mother-in-law example" as a paradigm of sensibility transcendence; important for Vervaeke's account of how perspectival knowing is the foundation of ethics.

## Role in the argument
Murdoch enters AMC primarily through her concept of moral attention and the mother-in-law example as the paradigm of [[Sensibility Transcendence]]. Her Platonic moral philosophy argues that the quality of one's attention — how one sees — is prior to and more fundamental than what one decides to do. This aligns with Vervaeke's account of perspectival knowing and the salience landscape as the primary moral-cognitive terrain (Ep 41).

**Moral attention**: In *The Sovereignty of Good*, Murdoch argues against existentialist ethics (ethics as radical choice) that the fundamental moral act is not choosing but seeing: attending correctly to what is there, without the distortions of ego-driven fantasy, self-justification, and desire for control. "Moral vision" is the capacity to see others as they truly are, rather than as projections of one's own needs. This is relevance realization as a moral phenomenon: the salience landscape itself is the moral terrain (Ep 41).

**The mother-in-law example**: Murdoch's famous thought experiment: a mother-in-law initially views her daughter-in-law as "common, vulgar, unserious." She then undertakes a sustained effort of moral attention — looking again, more carefully, more charitably, less defensively — and gradually comes to see the daughter-in-law differently: "lively, spontaneous, energetic." Nothing in the daughter-in-law's behavior has changed; what has changed is the mother-in-law's perception. For Vervaeke, this is sensibility transcendence: a restructuring of the salience landscape that is itself the moral achievement, prior to any behavioral change (Ep 41).

**Good as the transcendent standard**: Murdoch's Platonic move: just as Plato posited the Form of the Good as the ultimate standard of being and knowing, Murdoch argues that "Good" functions as a transcendent standard for moral attention that draws the self out of its self-centered fantasy toward reality. The Good is not a property of objects but the criterion of adequate attention to them (Ep 41).
