# Thomas Aquinas

> The systematizer who preserves the Axial worldview under Aristotelian pressure by reformulating two-worlds mythology into natural/supernatural — and in doing so initiates the divorce of reason and love, science and spirituality.

## Role in the argument
Vervaeke presents Aquinas as the figure who faces an impossible tension and solves it brilliantly but fatefully (Ep 19). After the Great Schism (1054) the West loses deep Neoplatonic mystical theology. Reading shifts from oral, transformative Lectio Divina toward silent, propositional engagement aimed at inner logical coherence — the intensive self is emerging. Then Aristotle is rediscovered through the Crusades: a body of thought with tremendous scientific explanatory power that does not fit the existing Christian worldview. Aquinas must integrate Aristotle without abandoning Christianity.

His solution is to reformulate the two-worlds mythology. Instead of a continuum between levels of reality that one ascends through love-and-reason together (Plotinus, Augustine), Aquinas posits two fundamentally distinct domains: the **natural** world, accessible through reason and science; and the **supernatural** world, accessible only through faith. The Aristotelian science of the natural world is given its domain; Christianity retains the supernatural. This preserves both.

But the cost is catastrophic for the ancient synthesis. In Plotinus and Augustine, love moves reason — love of the true and the real is what drives rationality toward wisdom. For Aquinas, love moves the will. Faith becomes the willful assertion of propositions that cannot be known through reason, driven by love of God. The distinction between love and reason is now structural. Science and spirituality, which were fully integrated in Neoplatonism and in Augustine, are now fundamentally separate: if it is scientific, it is not spiritual; if it is spiritual, it is not scientific.

The hidden danger, which Aquinas does not foresee, is this: as science becomes more successful at explaining the natural world, the supernatural world becomes progressively less *viable* — not just less believable propositionally, but less liveable as an existential home. And if the supernatural world becomes unliveable, the entire grammar of meaning (nomological + normative + narrative orders) collapses. This sets up the meaning crisis (Ep 19–20).
