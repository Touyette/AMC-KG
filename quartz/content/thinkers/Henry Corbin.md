# Henry Corbin

> French philosopher, Iranologist, and Islamic scholar; one of the first major translators of Heidegger into French; developed the imaginal and the divine double as the central categories for understanding gnosis and spiritual transformation.

## Role in the argument
Corbin is one of the four prophets of the meaning crisis in AMC's prophets arc. His contribution is to take Heidegger's participatory knowing / gnosis as dynamic coupling and make it practically navigable through two innovations: (1) the distinction between the imaginary and the imaginal, and (2) the recovery of the divine double from Persian Sufi and Mediterranean Hellenistic sources. For Corbin, the meaning crisis (forgetfulness of Being, loss of gnosis) is addressed not by abstract argument but by the restoration of the imaginal capacity (Ep 48).

**Background**: Deeply influenced by Heidegger (was one of the first major translators of Heidegger into French; his translation of *Introduction to Metaphysics* was seminal for Heidegger's reception in France), by Neoplatonism, and — most especially — by Persian Sufism and the Neoplatonic elements within it. Corbin read Persian Sufi poetry and philosophy extensively and deeply; he explicitly opposed fundamentalism and literalism as antithetical to the spiritual project of gnosis. He was also in dialogue with Jung (Corbin cites and engages Jung more openly than Jung acknowledges Corbin) (Ep 48).

**Imaginary vs. imaginal**: The key distinction. The *imaginary* is purely subjective inner imagery — private, controlled, known to be unreal. The *imaginal* is transjective: it mediates between the abstract intelligible world and the concrete sensible world, between the subjective and the objective. It is always in transformative transframing. The Cartesian grammar (mind/matter, subject/object splits) eliminated the imaginal from our cognitive grammar; for Corbin this is the deep source of the modern spiritual crisis, because without the imaginal there is no gnosis (Ep 48).

**Symbol and the imaginal**: A symbol understood imaginally is translucent (you look at AND through it simultaneously), transjective, trajective (puts you on a transformation trajectory), transformative (identity-level change), and transtemporal-transspatial (ontological not narrative movement). Losing the imaginal means losing the capacity for this kind of symbol-engagement — and thereby losing gnosis, aletheia, and the being mode (Ep 48).

**The angel and the divine double**: Corbin's most important concrete application: the "angel" in Persian Sufi poetry is the imaginal symbol of the divine double — the self one aspires to become. This is not a literal being but an imaginal one: the more real, more complete self toward which one's spiritual path moves. For Corbin, the S1 → S2 movement (current self → divine double) is the structure of gnosis, aspiration, and salvation. It cannot be accomplished through inference or decision — only through imaginal practice (Ep 48).

**Gnosis as play of being**: Corbin (and Robert Avens in *The New Gnosis*) describe gnosis as the "play of being" — a serious play (Vervaeke's term) in which the imaginal mode enables a participatory engagement with being that is simultaneously self-transformative and world-disclosive. This is the response to the meaning crisis: not an argument but an existential-imaginal practice (Ep 48).

**Vervaeke's ambivalences about Corbin**: Corbin is a beautiful and lyrical writer (influenced by Persian poetry), but this can make him hard to follow — the beauty can be mistaken for the understanding. He also has limitations as an interpreter (as a French scholar of Persian culture, he will have missed things). But he is "insightful and important" — his imaginal distinction is genuinely clarifying and has significant implications for responding to the meaning crisis (Ep 48).
