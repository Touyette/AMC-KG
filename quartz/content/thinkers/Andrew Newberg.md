# Andrew Newberg

> American neuroscientist and physician (b. 1966); pioneered "neurotheology" — the scientific study of the neural correlates of religious and mystical experience; used brain imaging (SPECT scans) to study practitioners of meditation and prayer in meditative peaks; identified characteristic patterns of frontal-parietal activity in mystical states.

## Role in the argument
Newberg enters AMC as an independent empirical support for the Continuity Hypothesis. His brain-imaging research on meditation and mystical experience supports Vervaeke's claim that higher states of consciousness are not discontinuous from ordinary cognition but represent the exaptation and intensification of the same cognitive machinery — particularly the relevance realization systems involving frontal (attention, self-reference) and parietal (spatial body-schema, self-other boundary) cortex (Ep 11, Ep 38).

**Frontal-parietal dynamics in mystical states**: Newberg's research found that advanced meditators, during peak states, show characteristic patterns: increased frontal lobe activity (sustained attention, self-referential processing) paired with decreased parietal lobe activity (body-schema, self-other boundary). The parietal deactivation correlates with the reported dissolution of the subject-object boundary and the sense of unity or non-duality. Vervaeke uses this as evidence that the "binding" of self and world characteristic of mystical states is a real cognitive transformation, not merely a subjective feeling (Ep 11, Ep 38).

**Neurotheology**: Newberg's project — finding the brain mechanisms of religious experience — overlaps with Vervaeke's Continuity Hypothesis but with important differences. Newberg tends toward a strong interpretation (the brain states just are mystical states); Vervaeke wants to argue that these states track something real about the organism-environment relationship. The brain imaging provides the empirical anchor without settling the metaphysical question (Ep 38).

**Independent support**: Newberg's work is "independent" in the sense that his neuroscientific findings were not developed in conversation with Vervaeke's cognitive-philosophical framework — they converge on similar conclusions from different directions. This convergence counts as plausibility-generating for the Continuity Hypothesis (Ep 38).
