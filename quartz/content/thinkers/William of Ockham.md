# William of Ockham

> The Franciscan scholastic who makes God's will primary over his reason — generating Nominalism, severing the conformity theory of knowing, and laying the groundwork for the experience-as-veil problem.

## Role in the argument
Vervaeke presents Ockham as a pivotal figure in the fragmentation of the Augustinian synthesis (Ep 20). The key move: unlike the whole Neoplatonic-Augustinian tradition (where God's reason, his intelligibility, is the source of his being), for Ockham God's primary faculty is **will**. God creates the world by an act of will — he asserts it into existence. He is not bound by rational principles of organization; any order in the universe is contingent, arbitrarily imposed by God's fiat.

This has two interlocking consequences. Theologically, it aligns with Aquinas's already-established move: faith is the will asserting what cannot be known by reason. God's will is supreme; the rational order of the natural world is just what God happened to will; it could have been otherwise. Philosophically, it generates **Nominalism**: since God imposes order arbitrarily, and we are made in God's image (also primarily will-beings), we too impose whatever order we find by how we speak. Universals — "book," "human," "tree" — are not features of the world; they are names (nomina) we apply. Only raw particulars exist in reality; the patterns are ours (Ep 20).

The consequence for knowing is devastating: the conformity theory (the mind conforming to the world's rational structure) loses its grounding. The world has no inherent rational structure to conform to. Knowing becomes inner coherence of language about raw particulars. The world, stripped of inherent rational order, becomes fundamentally absurd. This feeds into the experience-as-veil problem that Copernicus will dramatize and Galileo will formalize (Ep 20).
