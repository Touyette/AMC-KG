# Albert Camus

> French-Algerian philosopher and novelist (1913–1960); articulated the philosophy of the absurd — the confrontation between human meaning-demand and the universe's silence; Vervaeke treats the absurd as a precise diagnosis of the meaning crisis.

## Role in the argument
Camus gives Vervaeke's narrative a precise philosophical name for one dimension of the meaning crisis: the **absurd**. The absurd is not irrationality or randomness but the specific confrontation between the human demand for meaning, coherence, and intelligibility on one side, and the universe's complete indifference to that demand on the other. Neither side is wrong; the absurd is the clash between them (Ep 36).

**The absurd**: Camus articulates the absurd in "The Myth of Sisyphus" (1942) as the fundamental philosophical question: given the absurd, should one commit suicide, pursue philosophical hope (which Camus treats as a form of dishonest "philosophical suicide"), or embrace revolt? Camus argues for revolt: living with full awareness of the absurd without either resolving it (hope) or fleeing it (suicide). This is an existentialist response — dignified but not transformative; coping with homelessness rather than recovering a home (Ep 36).

**Camus and alienation**: The absurd names the specific form of alienation from the ground of meaning — the sense that the world is alien to meaning-seeking. Vervaeke uses this as a phenomenological description of what the meaning crisis feels like from the inside: not just boredom or sadness but the quality of an indifferent universe that fails to respond to the human meaning-demand (Ep 36).

**Limits of Camus's response**: Like Sartre and Heidegger, Camus' revolt is a philosophy of heroic endurance rather than transformation. It correctly diagnoses the absurd but does not recover the participatory knowing that would dissolve it. What Vervaeke's account offers that Camus does not: a naturalistic account of how the sense of resonance and meaning can be genuinely restored through the recalibration of relevance realization — not by denying the absurd but by recovering the cognitive and relational structures that make participation possible.
