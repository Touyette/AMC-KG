# Ursula Goodenough

> American cell biologist (b. 1943); author of *The Sacred Depths of Nature* (1998); a founding voice in religious naturalism — the attempt to find genuine religious meaning and orientation in the natural world understood scientifically without the need for supernatural additions; coined the concept of "transcendence into" as an alternative to supernatural transcendence.

## Role in the argument
Goodenough enters AMC as a scientific voice for what Vervaeke calls the "deep naturalism" or the "religion that is not a religion" — the possibility of encountering genuine sacredness through the depths of nature rather than by positing a supernatural realm above nature. Her concept of transcendence into (inward and downward into complexity and depth, rather than outward and upward to a supernatural realm) is a key formulation of how scientific naturalism can recover sacredness without the two-worlds mythology (Ep 38, Ep 39).

**Transcendence into**: Goodenough argues that the deep structure of natural systems — the complexity of cellular biology, the depth of evolutionary history, the integration of ecological systems — calls forth a genuine response of awe, wonder, and ultimate concern, not despite being purely natural but because of the depth of their naturalistic reality. This is not a poetic addition to science but a genuine relationship with what is real. "Transcendence into" is the movement into the depths of this reality rather than "transcendence above" into a supernatural realm (Ep 38).

**Religious naturalism**: Goodenough's religious naturalism holds that the natural world, understood scientifically, already provides everything religion is for: a sense of where we come from (evolutionary cosmology), what we belong to (the web of life), what calls for reverence (complexity, emergence, the miracle of consciousness), what grounds ultimate concern (the conditions for life), and what provides context for ethics (our place in the natural order). The two-worlds mythology is not needed because this-world is already inexhaustibly deep (Ep 39).

**Convergence with Vervaeke**: Vervaeke uses Goodenough to support the claim that the response to the meaning crisis does not require accepting premodern metaphysics. If the natural world, understood through the deep continuity hypothesis, is itself the seat of relevance realization and participatory knowing, then the sacredness one encounters in genuine religio is encounter with what is deepest in nature — not an escape from it. Goodenough provides an independent scientific voice for this position (Ep 38, Ep 39).
