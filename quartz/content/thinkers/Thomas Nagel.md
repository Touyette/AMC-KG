# Thomas Nagel

> American philosopher (b. 1937); known for "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" (establishing the irreducibility of subjective experience to objective description) and the concept of "the view from nowhere" (the aspiration to a perspective-less objectivity that paradoxically requires a perspectival subject); for Vervaeke, an articulator of the absurdity structure and the limits of scientific objectivism.

## Role in the argument
Nagel enters AMC through his analysis of absurdity and the view from nowhere. His argument that consciousness (and with it, meaning) cannot be fully accommodated by an objective-scientific description of reality directly supports Vervaeke's account of what the meaning crisis IS: the Cartesian grammar demands an objective viewpoint that systematically excludes everything that makes life meaningful from the perspective of the subject living it (Ep 36, Ep 22).

**The view from nowhere**: In *The View from Nowhere*, Nagel argues that the drive for scientific objectivity — for a perspective-free account of reality — is both genuinely valuable and deeply problematic. It is valuable because it reveals truths not accessible from any particular perspective; it is problematic because the more successfully one adopts a view from nowhere, the more one loses the first-person perspective that is the home of meaning, value, and experience. The aspiration to complete objectivity produces the alienation characteristic of the meaning crisis (Ep 22).

**What is it like to be a bat?**: Nagel's famous paper demonstrates that the subjective character of experience — what-it-is-like to be a particular kind of organism — cannot be captured by any third-person physical description. This irreducibility of perspectival knowing to propositional knowing is what Vervaeke also argues: the four kinds of knowing are not reducible to propositional knowledge, and the attempt to reduce them produces the meaning crisis (Ep 22).

**The absurd**: In "The Absurd," Nagel characterizes the human condition as absurd — not in Camus's dramatic sense but in a more analytical one: humans are the only beings capable of taking up the "view from outside" on their own lives and finding them arbitrary, while simultaneously being incapable of not living from the inside perspective that treats things as mattering. The absurd is the ineliminable collision of these two perspectives (Ep 36).
