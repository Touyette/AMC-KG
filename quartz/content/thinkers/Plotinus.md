# Plotinus

> Egyptian-Roman Neoplatonist philosopher (c. 204–270 CE); founder of Neoplatonism; the central figure for Vervaeke's account of how the Platonic participatory-anagogic framework was developed into its most sophisticated pre-Christian form and transmitted to subsequent mystical traditions.

## Role in the argument
Plotinus represents the culmination of the ancient participatory tradition. His account of the soul's ascent from matter through Nous (mind) to the One provides the template for every subsequent mystical tradition that Vervaeke draws on: Christian Neoplatonism (Augustine, Dionysius, Eckhart), Islamic philosophy (Avicenna, Corbin), and the Western esoteric tradition (Barfield). Vervaeke uses Plotinus extensively to articulate what a non-onto-theological account of the sacred ground of reality looks like — the One is not a being but the principle of all being, known not by propositions but by union (Ep 18).

**The One**: Plotinus's first principle is the One — so utterly simple that it transcends Being, thought, and all predication. The One is not a god in any anthropomorphic sense; it is the principle of unity from which all multiplicity flows. It cannot be known as an object but only by the soul's union with it — by becoming one through the dissolution of the subject-object divide. This is the supreme participatory knowing (Ep 18).

**Emanation and return**: Reality, for Plotinus, is structured as a series of emanations from the One: Nous (the realm of pure intelligence and the Forms), then Soul (the animating principle of the cosmos and individual souls), then Matter (the limit of emanation, barely real). The soul's task is return (epistrophe) — the re-ascent through Nous toward the One. The philosophy of the Enneads is a systematic account of this emanation and return, and Vervaeke reads it as one of the most rigorous accounts of anagoge in the tradition (Ep 18).

**Plotinus and the meaning crisis**: What Vervaeke finds in Plotinus is the positive alternative to onto-theology: a philosophy in which the ground of being is not a supreme being (the demiurge, the Old Testament God) but a principle that transcends all predication. This anticipates Tillich's "ground of being" and the apophatic tradition. Plotinus shows that the sacred can be understood non-theistically (in the onto-theological sense) while remaining the genuine ground of meaning and reality (Ep 18, Ep 50).

**Limitation**: Plotinus's system, for Vervaeke, has the characteristic weakness of Gnostic-adjacent Platonism: it tends toward otherworldliness — the material world is the lowest emanation, barely real, and the soul's ascent is an ascent away from matter. What is missing is a positive account of how the material, embodied, relational world participates in the sacred, rather than being the dimension from which one escapes. This limitation is corrected by the Christian incarnational tradition and, more fully, by Barfield's Final Participation (Ep 18, Ep 50).
