# Søren Kierkegaard

> Danish philosopher (1813–1855); proto-existentialist; philosopher of inwardness, qualitative transformation, and the paradox of faith; key figure in Vervaeke's account of the Romantic-existentialist response to the meaning crisis.

## Role in the argument
Kierkegaard represents the inner edge of the Romantic counter-movement: while the Romantics responded to the loss of meaning with aesthetics, organicism, and nature-philosophy, Kierkegaard turned the problem inward. The crisis of meaning is a crisis of the self's relationship to itself and to what ultimately matters. His analysis of the stages of existence, the paradox of self-creation, and the anxiety of freedom places him at the origin of the existentialist tradition as it bears on Vervaeke's argument (Ep 24, Ep 46).

**Stages of existence**: Kierkegaard described three stages or "spheres" of existence: (1) the aesthetic stage — living for pleasure, novelty, and intensity; avoidance of boredom; ultimately self-defeating because no external satisfaction addresses the interior emptiness; (2) the ethical stage — commitment to duty, social roles, and moral universals; provides structure but cannot address the deeper personal relationship to the absolute; (3) the religious stage — the individual's direct relationship with God, requiring a "leap" that cannot be mediated by aesthetics or ethics. These stages map onto the transformation question: what kind of knowing and what kind of self does each stage correspond to? (Ep 24).

**The paradox of self-creation**: Vervaeke develops Kierkegaard's insight into the [[Paradox of Self-Creation]]: you cannot choose to become the kind of self that makes different choices, because the choice requires the very capacity you are trying to develop. Kierkegaard's solution is the leap — a proleptic, aspiration-driven commitment that is motivated not by current capacity but by the kind of self one is becoming. This parallels the aspiration structure in Vervaeke's account of transformative experience (Ep 49).

**Anxiety**: Kierkegaard's analysis of anxiety (Angst) as the "dizziness of freedom" — the vertigo of facing unlimited possibility with no given nature to constrain it — is the existentialist phenomenology of the meaning crisis. Anxiety is not fear of a specific threat but the response to the structure of freedom itself; this is why addressing it requires transformation, not information (Ep 46).
