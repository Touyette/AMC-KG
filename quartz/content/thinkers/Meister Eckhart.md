# Meister Eckhart

> The central figure of the Rhineland Mystics who transforms the normative order: shifts from anagogic ascent to descent spirituality, making will-negation (Gelassenheit) the core of spiritual life — a move with enormous consequences through to Heidegger.

## Role in the argument
Vervaeke treats Eckhart as the pivotal figure of the **Rhineland Mystics** — the spiritual-philosophical current within German Christianity that responds to Aquinas's natural/supernatural split by reconceiving what spirituality means (Ep 20). Given that reason can no longer ascend to God (the supernatural domain is closed to reason), the Rhineland Mystics draw on Neoplatonic currents still alive within Christianity and propose a different model.

Eckhart's key move: it is not that Love moves the will. Rather, Love is how the will moves — specifically, when the will negates itself. My will normally presses outward, asserts itself, creates patterns (in Ockham's terms: imposes order). That self-assertion is the opposite of Agape. To become a conduit for divine Agape — to let God flow in — I must negate my will's self-assertion and make an interior space. Eckhart calls this **Gelassenheit** (releasement, letting-go): releasing one's grip on one's own will and the ego's projects. The complementary movement is **Durchbruch** (breakthrough): the eruption of the divine through the emptied self.

The consequence for the normative order is profound. Before Eckhart, the normative order involved ascending toward what is more real and good through love-and-reason together — self-transcendence cultivated by wisdom. After Eckhart, the normative order is restructured around **self-negation**: the essential spiritual act is to battle against your own will-assertion. What was biographical and contingent in Paul and Augustine (they happened to experience inner conflict) becomes valorized as the essential form of spiritual life. The connection between spirituality and wisdom — understood as the education of self-transcendence — is severed (Ep 20).

Heidegger later picks up Gelassenheit as a philosophical category for a different relationship to Being: openness rather than control, dwelling rather than mastery. This shows the reach of Eckhart's conceptual innovation beyond its original theological context (Ep 47+).
