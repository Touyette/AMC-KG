# Paul Baltes

> German psychologist; with Ursula Staudinger, developed the Berlin Wisdom Paradigm; pioneered the empirical and experimental study of wisdom.

## Role in the argument
Baltes & Staudinger create the first major empirical theory of wisdom and the experimental methodology to study it. Their Berlin Wisdom Paradigm frames wisdom as a "metaheuristic (pragmatic) to orchestrate mind and virtue toward excellence" — a heuristic for managing heuristics that requires relevance realization even if it doesn't explicitly theorize RR. Their five-criteria framework (factual knowledge, procedural knowledge, lifespan contextualism, fallibilism/humility, management of uncertainty) is the benchmark against which subsequent empirical theories of wisdom position themselves (Ep 43).

Their experimental findings about the Solomon effect (dialogue or simulated dialogue > additional thinking time) provide empirical grounding for the Socratic tradition of dialogue as a wisdom-cultivating practice, and for the Stoic practice of internalizing wise interlocutors.

Vervaeke's criticisms: (a) wisdom should not be understood as expertise (domain-specific); (b) the paradigm lacks explicit accounts of perspectival and participatory knowing, which are necessary for understanding the qualitative developmental transformations that constitute wisdom (Ep 43).
