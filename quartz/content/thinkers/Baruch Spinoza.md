# Baruch Spinoza

> Dutch philosopher (1632–1677); developed a pantheist metaphysics in which God and Nature are identical (Deus sive Natura); articulated the concept of conatus (every thing's drive to persist in its being); developed scientia intuitiva (intuitive science) as the highest form of knowing; for Vervaeke, a key early modern figure who resisted Cartesian dualism and maintained a participatory account of knowing.

## Role in the argument
Spinoza enters AMC as an early modern counter-voice to Cartesian dualism. Where Descartes split reality into two substances (mind and matter), Spinoza argued for a single substance (God/Nature) with infinite attributes, of which thought and extension are the two we can access. This monism avoids the mind-body problem while maintaining that mind and world are aspects of the same reality — a position more compatible with participatory knowing than Cartesian dualism (Ep 22).

**Deus sive Natura (God or Nature)**: Spinoza's identification of God with Nature was his most radical and scandalous move. God is not a personal being who created the world but the infinite substance that IS the world — not supernatural but the immanent ground of all that is. This made Spinoza the prototype of religious naturalism: the sacred is not beyond nature but nature itself, understood in its most adequate way (sub specie aeternitatis — under the aspect of eternity) (Ep 22).

**Conatus**: Every individual thing (person, stone, organism) has conatus — the drive to persist in its own being, to maintain and augment its existence. Conatus is the immanent teleology of things: not directed toward an external end but constitutive of what each thing is. For Vervaeke, conatus is a naturalistic version of the aspiration structure — the organism's inherent drive toward its own flourishing — which grounds the wisdom tradition's practices without requiring supernatural teleology (Ep 22).

**Scientia intuitiva**: Spinoza's highest form of knowing — scientia intuitiva (intuitive science, or knowledge of the third kind) — is the direct, non-inferential apprehension of particular things in relation to God/Nature. It is not mystical in the sense of being irrational but is a form of rational insight that grasps the whole in the part. This is closer to participatory knowing than to propositional knowledge — the kind of knowing Vervaeke is rehabilitating (Ep 22).
