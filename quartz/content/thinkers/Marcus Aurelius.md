# Marcus Aurelius

> Roman emperor (121–180 CE) and Stoic philosopher; author of the *Meditations* — a private journal of daily Stoic self-examination that was never intended for publication; the paradigm of the philosopher-king; the last of the Five Good Emperors; governed the Roman Empire for 19 years while practicing daily prosoche.

## Role in the argument
Marcus Aurelius enters AMC as the paradigmatic case of Stoic spiritual exercise in action: the *Meditations* is what prosoche actually looks like as a sustained practice. For Vervaeke, the *Meditations* is not a philosophical treatise but a performance — evidence that a full human life of enormous complexity and responsibility can be governed by Stoic self-cultivation practices. It is also the key text for Pierre Hadot's argument that ancient philosophy was practiced as a way of life, not as a system of doctrine (Ep 14, Ep 43).

**The Meditations as prosoche**: Marcus's journal records his ongoing application of Stoic principles to the events of his day, his own impulses, his fears, his obligations. He is not reporting Stoic doctrine but practicing it — using writing as a form of prosoche (self-attention) and self-dialogue with the internalized sage. Vervaeke cites Marcus as evidence that the Stoic psycho-technologies were genuinely transformative practices, not merely theoretical positions (Ep 43).

**The view from above**: One of Marcus's characteristic exercises is the "view from above" — imaginatively ascending to see oneself and one's life from a cosmic perspective. From this vantage point, the concerns that produce anxiety (reputation, political reversals, the opinions of others) appear correctly small, while the genuinely important things (acting rightly, attending well, exercising one's rational faculty) retain their significance. Vervaeke uses this as a paradigm of the kind of perspectival transformation that wisdom training achieves (Ep 43).

**Philosopher-king as practical ideal**: Marcus embodies what Plato theorized as the philosopher-king — the person who has undergone genuine philosophical transformation and thus can govern with practical wisdom. He is not incorruptible because of external constraints but because his RR machinery has been recalibrated by Stoic practice to attend to what genuinely matters. Vervaeke uses Marcus to argue that wisdom-cultivation is not a withdrawal from the world but can be fully integrated with practical agency (Ep 14, Ep 43).
