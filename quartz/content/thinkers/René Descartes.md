# René Descartes

> French philosopher and mathematician (1596–1650); founded modern epistemology and philosophy of mind through his method of radical doubt, the cogito, and the mind-body distinction; his framework became the cultural grammar of Western modernity, constituting what Vervaeke calls the Cartesian Cultural Grammar.

## Role in the argument
Descartes is the pivot of AMC's historical narrative — the thinker whose framework crystallized the conditions for the meaning crisis. His mathematical-mechanical worldview, combined with his sharp mind-body dualism and his elevation of propositional certainty as the criterion of genuine knowledge, created the cultural grammar that modern Westerners still inhabit by default. He is not a villain but a genius who articulated with unprecedented clarity the consequences of the scientific revolution — consequences that include the draining of meaning from the world (Ep 22).

**The method of radical doubt**: Descartes sought indubitable foundations for knowledge by doubting everything he could possibly doubt. What survives: the cogito — I think, therefore I am. The thinking thing (res cogitans) cannot be doubted because the act of doubting itself confirms that something is thinking. This anchors Descartes' reconstruction of knowledge in the inner certainty of the thinking subject, inaugurating the subjective turn in epistemology (Ep 22).

**Mind-body dualism**: The cogito establishes that mind (res cogitans: thinking substance, unextended, non-spatial) and body (res extensa: extended substance, spatial, mechanistic) are categorically distinct. This is [[Mind-Body Problem]]: how can two categorically distinct substances interact? The dualism generates cascading problems: how can mind know the external world? How can mental states cause physical events? The answers drive the entire trajectory of modern philosophy (Ep 22).

**Primary and secondary qualities**: Following Galileo, Descartes distinguishes the "real" properties of bodies (mathematical, primary: size, shape, motion) from the "merely apparent" properties that exist only in the perceiving mind (secondary: color, sound, warmth, beauty, meaning). This [[Primary and Secondary Qualities]] distinction drains the world of everything that makes it humanly significant and relocates these qualities in the subjective interior — a major structural move in the production of the meaning crisis (Ep 22).

**The Cartesian Cultural Grammar**: Descartes' framework — even as philosophy moved past his specific doctrines — became the implicit grammar of modern culture: mind is inner, world is outer; propositional certainty is the gold standard; meaning is subjective; the body is a machine. This grammar is what Vervaeke calls [[Cartesian Cultural Grammar]]: not a set of explicit beliefs but the pre-reflective structure through which modern Westerners default to thinking about themselves and the world (Ep 22).
