# Epictetus

> Greek Stoic philosopher (c. 50–135 CE); born enslaved; later freed; never wrote himself — his teachings survive through the *Discourses* and the *Enchiridion* (Manual) compiled by his student Arrian; the most psychologically acute of the Roman Stoics; his central contribution is the dichotomy of control: some things are "up to us" (our judgments, impulses, desires, aversions) and some are not (body, reputation, wealth, circumstances).

## Role in the argument
Epictetus enters AMC as the Stoic who most clearly articulates prosoche as an internalized practice that addresses the perennial problems. His dichotomy of control is a systematic proposal for recalibrating the agent's relevance realization machinery: attend only to what is genuinely in your power (your own judgments and impulses), and you will cease to be disturbed by what is not. Vervaeke uses Epictetus to show how Stoic spiritual exercises constitute a real psycho-technology for addressing existential anxiety and modal confusion (Ep 14, Ep 43).

**The dichotomy of control**: "Some things are in our control and others not." What is in our control is our own rational faculty — our judgments, impulses, desires, and aversions. What is not in our control is everything external — body, reputation, wealth, political circumstances, other people's actions. Epictetus argues that virtually all suffering arises from confusing these two domains: treating things outside our control as if they were inside it, and consequently becoming anxious, frustrated, and agitated. The Stoic practice is to redirect attention and care to what is actually in our power (Ep 14).

**Internalizing the sage**: Epictetus developed the practice of asking, in every situation, what the Stoic sage would do or judge — and using this as the standard for one's own judgment. The sage is not an external authority but an internalized model for the well-calibrated relevance realization system: the sage attends to what genuinely matters, responds to what genuinely calls for response, and is undisturbed by what is outside the domain of rational agency. Vervaeke connects this to the concept of internalizing an exemplar as a form of perspectival transformation (Ep 43).

**Prosoche**: Epictetus's prosoche is the sustained moment-to-moment practice of attending to one's own ruling faculty (hegemonikon) — noticing impulses before they become actions, judging them against the standard, and acting only from well-reasoned judgments. This is not a one-time discipline but an ongoing practice of metacognitive self-attention. Hadot and Vervaeke both identify prosoche as the central Stoic spiritual exercise — the one that makes the others possible (Ep 43).
