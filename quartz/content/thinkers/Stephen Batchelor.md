# Stephen Batchelor

> British author and teacher (b. 1953); former monk in both Tibetan and Korean Zen traditions; proponent of "secular Buddhism" — an agnostic, pragmatic engagement with Buddhist practice that brackets metaphysical claims (rebirth, karma as cosmic justice) while retaining the transformative potential of the practices; author of *Buddhism Without Beliefs* (1997) and *Alone With Others* (1983).

## Role in the argument
Batchelor enters AMC as a thinker who articulates the "interpretation crisis" within Buddhism: neither traditional insider interpretations (which require accepting pre-modern metaphysics) nor secular outsider interpretations (which dismiss the depth of the practices) are adequate. This double failure maps onto the broader meaning crisis: the same oscillation between fundamentalism and dismissive rationalism occurs in the Christian context. Batchelor represents the attempt to recover the transformative core of a tradition without its pre-modern worldview (Ep 39).

**Secular Buddhism**: Batchelor's project is to strip Buddhism down to what he calls its "axial" core: the Four Ennobling Provocations (Batchelor's translation of the Four Noble Truths) as a pragmatic program for transforming one's relationship to suffering, craving, and cessation — while remaining agnostic about rebirth, karma, and cosmology. The practices work because of what they do to the agent's RR machinery, not because of their cosmic context. Vervaeke finds this move congenial but worries it loses the participatory and perspectival depth (Ep 39).

**Four Ennobling Provocations**: Batchelor's re-translation of the Four Noble Truths: rather than doctrines about suffering (Dukkha is the truth; its origin is craving; cessation is possible; the path is the Eightfold Path), he reads them as provocations — challenges to a particular kind of engagement: embrace suffering fully, let go of craving, experience cessation, cultivate the path. These are tasks, not truth-claims. Vervaeke adopts this re-reading in his own engagement with Buddhism (Ep 39).

**The interpretation crisis**: Batchelor articulates that Buddhism faces an interpretation crisis: traditional interpreters who stay inside the tradition are implausible to modern science-educated practitioners; secular interpreters who step outside the tradition lose the transformative depth and the community of practice. Neither works. This is structurally identical to the crisis within Christianity that Vervaeke identifies. The parallel suggests the meaning crisis is about the collapse of wisdom traditions, not just about specific doctrines (Ep 39).
