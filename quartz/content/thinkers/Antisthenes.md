# Antisthenes

> Athenian philosopher (c. 446–366 BCE); student of Socrates; founder of the Cynic school; believed that virtue alone is sufficient for happiness and that external goods (wealth, honor, pleasure, social convention) are not just unnecessary but actively corruptive of the philosophical life.

## Role in the argument
Antisthenes enters AMC as the founder of Cynicism — one of the Hellenistic responses to domicide and the collapse of the polis. Where Zeno and the Stoics built a cosmic worldview that re-embedded the individual in the rational order of the universe, Antisthenes and the Cynics went the other direction: radical stripping away of everything external, leaving only the natural self in direct contact with reason and virtue. Vervaeke uses Cynicism to show the range of responses to the perennial problems that the Hellenistic period produced, and to note that the Cynic solution — pure interiority — anticipates both the Stoic move and elements of later asceticism (Ep 14).

**Philosophy as radical self-sufficiency**: Antisthenes argued that wisdom consists entirely in virtue (arete), and virtue is entirely internal — it requires no external goods, no education beyond a certain point, no social status or cultural membership. A person can be virtuous and wise in any circumstances, including slavery, exile, poverty, and illness. This is the most extreme form of the Hellenistic interiorization of meaning: the good life is entirely within, entirely accessible to anyone, entirely independent of fortune (Ep 14).

**Rejection of convention (nomos) for nature (physis)**: Antisthenes and the Cynics famously rejected social convention (nomos) in favor of nature (physis): conventional values (wealth, reputation, marriage, citizenship) are not genuinely good but are culturally imposed fictions that distract from genuine virtue. The Cynic life is lived according to what nature actually requires, stripped of cultural overlay. This anticipates Vervaeke's critique of the having mode — the accumulation of cultural goods as a substitute for genuine meaning (Ep 14).

**Legacy**: Antisthenes's most famous student was Diogenes of Sinope, who developed these ideas into the most radical enacted philosophy of antiquity. The Cynics' direct challenge to convention and authority was remembered and respected even by the later Stoics, who saw Cynicism as the "short path to virtue."
