# Paul of Tarsus

> Jewish-Christian apostle (c. 5–64 CE); wrote the earliest canonical Christian texts; articulated the theology of agape as cosmic transformative love, faith as trust-in-relation rather than intellectual assent, and the universalism of the Jesus movement across ethnic and social boundaries; for Vervaeke, the crucial figure who translated the Jesus event into the Hellenistic world.

## Role in the argument
Paul enters AMC in the episodes on early Christianity (Ep 15–16) as the figure who extended the Jesus movement into the Greco-Roman world and gave it its theological framework. Vervaeke focuses on two things: (1) Paul's concept of agape as a cosmic force — not just human love but the form in which reality's own generativity participates in persons; and (2) Paul's treatment of faith (pistis) as trust-in-relationship rather than intellectual assent to propositions — a form that later becomes codified into creedal religion (Ep 15, Ep 16).

**Agape**: Paul's development of agape (in 1 Corinthians 13, and throughout his letters) transforms it from a human affective disposition into a cosmic principle: God is agape, and participation in agape is participation in the ground of being. For Vervaeke, this is one of the most powerful articulations in Western history of the participatory-transformative knowing structure: agape is not something you feel toward others but something you participate in that transforms how you relate to everything (Ep 15).

**Faith as pistis**: Paul's "faith" (pistis) is not primarily propositional assent but a trust-relationship — entrusting oneself to the God who raised Jesus from the dead, and being transformed by that entrusting. The later credalization of Christian religion (through the Nicene Creed and Reformation disputes about faith) obscures this participatory dimension. Vervaeke reads Paul as preserving a non-credal, transformative understanding of faith that is closer to Tillich's "ultimate concern" than to modern propositional belief (Ep 15).

**Universalism and the in-group problem**: Paul's famous claim that "there is neither Jew nor Greek, neither slave nor free" (Galatians 3:28) is the extension of the agapic love beyond in-group solidarity. This universalism is both an expansion of the Jesus movement and a potential source of new problems: how do you maintain a community (which requires in-group structure) when agape is supposed to be universal? This tension — between communal religion and universal love — runs through all subsequent Christian history (Ep 15, Ep 16).
