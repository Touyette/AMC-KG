# Harry Frankfurt

> American moral philosopher (1929–2023); known for his analysis of hierarchical desire, wanton vs. non-wanton agency, and caring as the fundamental structure of agency; Vervaeke uses his framework to analyze what makes authentic self-direction possible vs. the wantonness that characterizes a self in the grip of parasitic processing.

## Role in the argument
Frankfurt enters AMC through his work on desire, agency, and caring. His key distinction — between agents who identify with their desires (non-wantons) and those who do not (wantons: beings who have desires but are simply moved by whichever desire is strongest, without second-order concern) — maps onto Vervaeke's distinction between genuine self-direction and being "run by" one's parasitic processing (Ep 42).

**Wanton vs. non-wanton agents**: A wanton is a being that has desires and acts on them but has no second-order desires about which desires to have or act from. A non-wanton agent identifies with certain desires (makes them "their own" through a higher-order volition) and therefore has a form of freedom: they can act from what they care about rather than simply from what they happen to want. For Vervaeke, this distinction is central to the question of authentic self-cultivation: the wisdom traditions are training one to become a non-wanton agent — to act from what one most deeply cares about (Ep 42).

**Hierarchical desires**: Frankfurt's model of hierarchical desires (first-order desires: wanting X; second-order desires: wanting to want X, or not wanting to want X; second-order volitions: wanting a particular desire to be effective) provides a formal framework for the aspiration structure. One aspires to be the kind of person whose first-order desires align with what one reflectively endorses — this is the task of moral and existential development (Ep 42).

**Caring as the structure of agency**: Frankfurt's later work on "caring" (in *The Reasons of Love*) is especially relevant: what we care about constitutes what we are. The structure of caring is not merely preferring but being committed in a way that makes certain considerations deeply relevant and others less so. This connects to Vervaeke's account of how the salience landscape is structured by what one fundamentally cares about — caring and RR are intimately connected (Ep 42).
