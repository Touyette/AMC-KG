# Marc Lewis

> Canadian neuroscientist and memoirist (b. 1948); author of *Memoirs of an Addicted Brain* (2011) and *The Biology of Desire* (2015); developed the "Reciprocal Narrowing" model of addiction as a developmental process of agent-arena co-constitution rather than a disease model; uses dynamic systems theory to explain how addiction reorganizes both the neural substrate and the perceived affordance landscape.

## Role in the argument
Lewis enters AMC as a theorist of addiction whose model illustrates how relevance realization can become pathologically self-reinforcing. His Reciprocal Narrowing model provides a case study in how the agent-arena relationship can enter a destructive dynamic: the agent's relevance realization machinery becomes tuned to a narrowing set of affordances (the drug, the ritual, the social world of use), while simultaneously the arena — the perceived world of affordances — contracts to match the narrowed attention, producing a self-reinforcing loop that is phenomenologically compelling but developmentally regressive (Ep 41).

**Reciprocal Narrowing**: Lewis argues that addiction is best understood not as a disease (a brain disorder imposed on an otherwise normal agent) nor as a simple choice (a bad habit with moral failure) but as a developmental process: over time, the agent's motivational and attentional systems co-evolve with a narrowed range of goals and affordances. The drug or behavior becomes progressively more salient (commanding relevance realization) while everything else — relationships, projects, pleasures — becomes progressively less salient. This is relevance realization reorganizing the agent-arena relationship around a single attractor (Ep 41).

**Dynamic systems**: Lewis uses dynamic systems theory to model the non-linear, self-organizing quality of this process: small initial biases in attention and desire amplify through reciprocal feedback until a new stable attractor is reached. The same dynamical framework Vervaeke uses to understand wisdom (optimal grip, flow, transformation) also explains why addiction is so hard to escape: it is a stable dynamical state, not a mere habit (Ep 41).

**Addiction and meaning crisis**: Vervaeke uses Lewis to argue that addiction is not merely a neurological or moral problem but a problem of meaning: the reciprocal narrowing of the agent-arena relationship is a failure mode of the very capacity (relevance realization and participatory knowing) that constitutes meaningful engagement. Addiction replaces the ongoing, open-ended co-constitution of self and world with a closed, self-consuming loop. The path out requires not just stopping a behavior but reconstituting the agent-arena relationship — which is why spiritual and community-based recovery programs often work better than purely pharmacological ones (Ep 41).
