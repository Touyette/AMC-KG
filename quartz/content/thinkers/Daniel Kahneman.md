# Daniel Kahneman

> Israeli-American psychologist (1934–2024); Nobel laureate in Economics (2002); co-developer (with Amos Tversky) of prospect theory and the heuristics-and-biases research program; author of *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011); best known for the System 1 / System 2 dual-process framework for understanding cognition.

## Role in the argument
Kahneman enters AMC as the empirical psychologist who, with Tversky, documented the systematic ways human reasoning deviates from formal rationality norms — the heuristics and biases program. Vervaeke engages this work to illuminate the relationship between relevance realization and rationality: Kahneman's findings show that much reasoning is driven by fast, associative, automatic processing (System 1) that is ecologically adaptive but normatively unreliable under conditions far removed from ancestral environments. This motivates Vervaeke's account of why RR can produce both wisdom and self-deception (Ep 27).

**System 1 / System 2**: Kahneman's two-systems framework distinguishes fast, automatic, associative, and largely unconscious processing (System 1) from slow, deliberate, effortful, and rule-governed processing (System 2). System 1 generates relevance assessments, emotional responses, and rapid judgments without deliberation; System 2 checks, revises, or overrides these outputs when it is deployed. Most cognition runs on System 1; System 2 is expensive and rarely invoked unprompted. Vervaeke's relevance realization maps broadly onto System 1 operations — it is the fast, non-conscious framing that determines what counts as relevant before deliberation begins (Ep 27).

**Heuristics and biases**: Kahneman and Tversky's research program identified systematic deviations from Bayesian rationality — availability heuristic (frequency judged by ease of recall), representativeness heuristic (probability judged by similarity to prototype), anchoring effects, and others. These are not random errors but structured biases driven by the operation of relevance-sensitive heuristics. Vervaeke uses this to argue that the same machinery that enables fast, ecologically adaptive relevance assessment also generates predictable errors when transported into formal, abstract, or misleadingly framed problems (Ep 27).

**Rationality and dysrationalia**: Kahneman's framework connects to Keith Stanovich's work on dysrationalia: intelligent people (high System 2 capacity) can remain deeply irrational because System 2 is not routinely deployed to check System 1 outputs. Vervaeke uses this to argue that rationality requires not just System 2 capacity but the wisdom to know when to deploy it — which itself is a relevance realization problem (Ep 27).
