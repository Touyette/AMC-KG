# Martin Luther

> German theologian (1483–1546); initiator of the Protestant Reformation; developed the doctrines of sola fide (faith alone), sola scriptura (scripture alone), and the priesthood of all believers; his reforms, for Vervaeke, unintentionally accelerated the meaning crisis by credentializing religion and destroying the institutional wisdom-cultivation function of the Church.

## Role in the argument
Luther is the hinge figure in AMC's account of the Reformation's contribution to the meaning crisis. His theological innovations were motivated by genuine spiritual concerns — he sought to recover authentic religious life against the corruption of the institutional Church. But the unintended consequences of his reforms were structurally catastrophic for the meaning-bearing functions that religion had served (Ep 21).

**Sola fide and the credalization of religion**: Luther's doctrine that salvation comes through faith alone — not works, not sacrament, not priestly mediation — displaced the participatory, transformative, bodily practices of Catholic religion (pilgrimage, penance, confession, Mass) in favor of propositional assent (belief in the grace of God through Christ). This is the "credalization" move: religion becomes primarily about what one believes rather than what practices one engages in. In Vervaeke's terms, [[Religio]] (participatory cultivation) is displaced by [[Credo]] (propositional assent) (Ep 21).

**The priesthood of all believers and individual conscience**: Luther abolished the mediating function of the priesthood: every believer stands directly before God, with the Bible as the sole authority. This placed the individual conscience as the primary locus of religious authority — a profound individualization that contributed to the interiorization and privatization of religious life. Combined with the Cartesian grammar, this produced the modern picture of religion as an individual's private beliefs about transcendent realities (Ep 21).

**Sapiential obsolescence**: The institutional Church, whatever its failures, had been the primary institution for wisdom cultivation in Western Christendom: it ran schools and universities, provided confessors and spiritual directors, organized retreats and pilgrimage, preserved the contemplative traditions. By attacking the institutional Church's authority, Luther contributed to the [[Sapiential Obsolescence]] that Vervaeke identifies as a key condition of the meaning crisis: the loss of the institutions that had maintained the ecology of practices for wisdom development (Ep 21).

**The unintended meaning crisis**: Luther did not intend to create the meaning crisis. His concern was with authentic faith, not with dismantling the conditions for meaning. But the structural consequences of his reforms — credalization of religion, privatization of religious life, destruction of sapiential institutions — contributed decisively to the conditions that produced the crisis (Ep 21).
