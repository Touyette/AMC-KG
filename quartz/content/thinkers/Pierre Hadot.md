# Pierre Hadot

> French philosopher (1922–2010) and historian of ancient philosophy; established that ancient philosophy was practiced primarily as a way of life rather than as a system of doctrines; coined the concept of "spiritual exercises" to describe the concrete transformative practices that philosophers actually performed.

## Role in the argument
Hadot provides the historical warrant for Vervaeke's claim that the ancient philosophical traditions were primarily wisdom-cultivation systems — psycho-technologies for transforming the self — rather than theoretical frameworks for generating true beliefs. This is the key move that allows Vervaeke to retrieve ancient philosophy for a scientific age: what the ancients were doing was developing practices (prosoche, objective seeing, the view from above, contemplation) that enhanced the agent's capacity to engage reality, not just propositions about it (Ep 36, Ep 43, Ep 46).

**Philosophy as a way of life**: Hadot argued, against the dominant academic interpretation, that ancient philosophical schools (Platonic, Stoic, Epicurean, even Skeptical) were first and foremost communities of practice that used philosophical reasoning as a tool for achieving a certain way of being in the world — wisdom, equanimity, the philosophical life. The doctrine was secondary to the transformation it aimed to produce. This is exactly Vervaeke's account of psycho-technologies and the sapiential tradition (Ep 36).

**Spiritual exercises**: Hadot's concept of "spiritual exercises" — deliberate, repeated practices for transforming one's relationship to oneself and the world — directly maps onto Vervaeke's ecology of practices. Key exercises: prosoche (moment-to-moment self-attention), the view from above, objective seeing (seeing things as they are, stripped of projection), the premeditation of death, the practice of dialogue. Hadot saw these as genuinely transformative and not merely self-help (Ep 43).

**Prosoche**: Hadot's prosoche is the Stoic practice of continuous self-attention — noticing one's mental states and judging them against the sage's standard at every moment of the day. It is a sustained metacognitive practice; Marcus Aurelius's *Meditations* is its written trace. Vervaeke uses prosoche as a paradigm case of a wisdom-practice that integrates all four kinds of knowing into ongoing life (Ep 43).

**Objective seeing**: Hadot's "objective seeing" (described especially in his analysis of Marcus Aurelius) is the attempt to see things as they really are, stripped of the evaluative overlays that egocentrism and need project onto them. One eats food, not a delicacy; one walks toward an appointment, not toward glory. This demystifies in a way that is not cynical but liberating. Vervaeke connects this to the RR recalibration that wisdom training achieves (Ep 43).
