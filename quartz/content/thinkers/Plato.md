# Plato

> Athenian philosopher who synthesizes Socrates and Pythagoras into the first systematic psychological theory and the West's foundational account of wisdom as anagogic self-transformation.

## Role in the argument
Plato is traumatized by Socrates' death and sets himself the problem of explaining why people are so foolish as to kill the wisest human being. His answer is the Tripartite Psyche — three centers of motivation (reason, thymos, appetite) that operate by incompatible principles and generate inner conflict when misaligned. Wisdom, for Plato, is an internal justice in which the man (reason) has been taught, the lion (thymos) has been trained, and the monster (appetite) has been tamed, producing a reduction in inner conflict, a reduction in self-deception, and a clearer vision of reality. This process of improvement feeds on itself in an anagogic ascent. Plato's Myth of the Cave makes this ascent vivid: it is a participatory transformation, not a passive acquisition of beliefs, in which the self and its grasp of reality are mutually constitutive. Plato also introduces the 4 Kinds of Knowing, with participatory knowing as the deepest — the mode in which you conform yourself to the logos of what you know. Along with the Bible, Plato's works constitute the two feet of Western civilization, and Neoplatonism (developed from him) is the bedrock of Western spirituality (Ep 05).
