# Monika Ardelt

> Sociologist and gerontologist; developed the three-dimensional wisdom model (cognitive/reflective/affective); critiqued Baltes & Staudinger for modal confusion between knowing about wisdom and being wise.

## Role in the argument
Ardelt (2004)'s most important contribution to AMC is the critique that Baltes & Staudinger commit a *modal confusion*: their five-criteria product theory describes what wisdom looks like as a set of knowledge-states, but having theoretical knowledge about wisdom is not the same as being wise. The wise person must *realize* these truths within a process of self-transformation — they must embody and enact them, not merely possess them propositionally. This is one of the most significant criticisms in the wisdom literature and it points directly toward the need for a process theory of wisdom (Ep 44).

**Three-dimensional wisdom model** — mutually influencing dimensions:
- **Cognitive**: Grasping the *significance* of one's knowledge — Kekes's interpretive knowledge rather than mere descriptive knowledge. Not just knowing theoretical truths about life but understanding what they mean for one's own development and action. Vervaeke: this points to RR (grasping significance = construal + relevance realization).
- **Reflective**: Multi-perspectival, self-examination, self-awareness, self-insight. The capacity to take multiple perspectives and turn that perspectival ability back on oneself in self-reflection. Overcomes egocentrism perspectivally. Connects to the Solomon effect and the Stoic practice of self-examination.
- **Affective**: Compassion; Vervaeke argues agape is the best understanding of this — not generic warmth but care directed at the conditions for others' flourishing and meaning-making. Overcomes egocentrism motivationally. Connects wisdom to meaning in life in the way Wolf describes.

The cognitive and reflective dimensions together overcome egocentrism perspectivally; the affective dimension overcomes it motivationally. All three are mutually influencing (Ep 44).

Vervaeke's criticisms: Ardelt's model lacks an explicit account of transformative experience (LA Paul's work), a genuine process theory of how one becomes wise, and an independent account of foolishness. But her model moves significantly beyond Baltes & Staudinger by pointing toward participatory knowing and the deep connection between wisdom and meaning in life (Ep 44).
