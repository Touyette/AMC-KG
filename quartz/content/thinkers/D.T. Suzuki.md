# D.T. Suzuki

> Japanese Buddhist scholar (1870–1966); primary popularizer of Zen Buddhism in the West; author of "Essays in Zen Buddhism" (3 vols.); articulated prajna (non-propositional wisdom-knowing) for Western audiences; connected Zen and Western mysticism; for Vervaeke, a key conduit for participatory wisdom from the Buddhist tradition.

## Role in the argument
Suzuki enters AMC primarily as the transmitter of Zen Buddhism to the Western intellectual context. His articulation of prajna (prajña) — the non-propositional, direct wisdom-seeing that is the goal of Zen practice — connects the Buddhist tradition to Vervaeke's account of participatory knowing. Suzuki's identification of convergences between Zen and Meister Eckhart (in his conversations with Eckhart scholar Carl Suso Frank) is also relevant to Vervaeke's synthesis (Ep 37).

**Prajna and non-propositional knowing**: Suzuki developed for Western audiences the concept of prajna as the direct seeing-through that Zen practice aims to produce. Prajna is not inference, not conceptual understanding, not mediated by symbols — it is immediate contact with reality as it is, without the filter of conceptual categorization. This is what Vervaeke calls participatory knowing at its most direct form: the knowing-by that changes the knower and is not fully articulable as propositions (Ep 37).

**Zen and the Western mystical tradition**: Suzuki drew extensive parallels between Zen and Western mysticism — particularly between satori (the Zen experience of sudden enlightenment, often triggered by koan practice) and the experiences described by Meister Eckhart and other Christian mystics. For Vervaeke, these convergences provide evidence for the deep continuity hypothesis: across wildly different cultural contexts and vocabularies, these traditions are pointing to the same cognitive-existential transformations (Ep 37).

**Koan practice**: Suzuki helped introduce Western audiences to koan practice: the Zen method of presenting a teacher's question (often paradoxical or nonsensical to discursive reason) that cannot be resolved by ordinary thinking and requires a qualitative shift in cognitive mode. Vervaeke connects koan practice to FINSTING and insight problem-solving: the koan is a FINSTING device that produces an impasse so intractable that ordinary conceptual processing is exhausted and a non-propositional transformation becomes possible (Ep 37).
