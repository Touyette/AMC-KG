# Robert Sternberg

> Psychologist; developed the Balance Theory of Wisdom (1998) and the WICS model (Wisdom-Intelligence-Creativity-Synthesized); tireless in connecting wisdom research to pedagogy.

## Role in the argument
Sternberg's Balance Theory (1998) defines wisdom as the *optimization* (not spatial balancing) of interests (intrapersonal, interpersonal, extrapersonal) toward adapting to, shaping, and selecting environments, mediated by tacit understanding. Vervaeke reads this as invoking RR machinery throughout: "tacit understanding" ≈ grasping significance ≈ interpretive knowledge; "balance" ≈ Piagetian equilibration ≈ optimization; "adapting/shaping/selecting" ≈ the relevance-guided engagement with the agent-arena relationship; intrapersonal/interpersonal/extrapersonal ≈ the three dimensions of meaning in life (Ep 44).

Sternberg directed the theory toward the "common good" as its normative goal. Vervaeke critiques this as anachronistic (not all historically wise figures were committed to common-good liberalism) and proposes substituting virtue + meaning in life as the goals that wisdom serves (Ep 44).

Sternberg also developed the WICS model (connecting wisdom, intelligence, creativity) and edited foundational anthologies on wisdom and foolishness in psychology. His pedagogical efforts to bring wisdom cultivation into educational settings are significant: he tries to engineer psychotechnologies for wisdom — exactly what the meaning crisis response requires (Ep 44).

Vervaeke's criticisms: still a product theory (foolishness = imbalance is circular, not independent); tacit knowledge/understanding not sufficiently developed; values running alongside the model are underspecified; lacks an explicit account of transformative experience and the process by which one becomes wise (Ep 44).
