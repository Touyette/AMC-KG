# Aristotle

> Plato's greatest student and the founder of a dynamical, biological approach to wisdom that centers on character, virtue, and the actualization of potential through growth and development.

## Role in the argument
Aristotle complements and corrects Plato by developing an adequate account of change as growth. Where Plato tended to treat the forms as static, Aristotle is interested in how things develop over time and how the structural-functional organization (idos/logos) is enacted in self-organizing processes. He introduces the distinction between actuality and potential, and between causes (events that make things happen) and constraints (conditions that shape what is possible) — concepts that anticipate modern Dynamical Systems Theory. His account of wisdom goes beyond Plato by adding the dimension of character: wisdom is not just having the right alignment of the psyche but cultivating a virtual engine — a set of character-traits (virtues) that regulate your self-development so that you actualize your potential as a rational, reflective being. Akrasia is explained not as weakness of will but as inadequate character formation: you do the wrong thing not because you don't know better but because you haven't sufficiently trained the dispositions that would make the right action natural. Aristotle's Conformity Theory of Knowing — knowing as the mind taking on the same structural-functional organization as what is known — grounds his worldview in a participatory, contact epistemology. His geocentric cosmos, in which everything moves by natural motion toward its natural place, creates a perfectly attuned worldview for over a millennium (Ep 06, Ep 07).
