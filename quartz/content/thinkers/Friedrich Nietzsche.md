# Friedrich Nietzsche

> German philosopher (1844–1900); philosopher of nihilism, will to power, and the death of God; Vervaeke treats him as the most lucid diagnostician of the meaning crisis — the prophet who articulated what was happening and could not find a solution — and connects him to the prophetic tradition of Heidegger, Corbin, Jung, Tillich, and Barfield.

## Role in the argument
Nietzsche is the central diagnostician of the meaning crisis in AMC. Vervaeke reads him as the thinker who saw most clearly that the collapse of the Christian moral-metaphysical framework was not just a theoretical problem but an existential catastrophe — the destruction of the entire system that had given European culture its values, its sense of purpose, and its orientation to reality (Ep 23).

**The death of God**: Nietzsche's proclamation that "God is dead" (in *The Gay Science*, through the figure of the madman) is not an atheist's triumphalism but a diagnosis of cultural catastrophe. God is dead not because of an argument but because the structures that made the God-concept culturally living — the three orders, the Christian narrative, the participatory forms of life — have been destroyed by modernity. The madman arrives with a lantern in the marketplace and finds that no one understands what has been lost (Ep 23).

**Nihilism**: The consequence of the death of God is nihilism — not the intellectual doctrine that nothing has value but the lived condition of a culture that has destroyed its value system without replacing it. Nietzsche describes nihilism as the most disturbing guest that stands at the door: the recognition that the highest values devalue themselves, that what we most believed in is now void. This is the precise philosophical name for what Vervaeke calls the meaning crisis (Ep 23).

**Will to power and the Übermensch**: Nietzsche's proposed responses — the will to power (the creative self-overcoming that affirms life), the Übermensch (the type of human being capable of creating new values after the death of God), eternal recurrence (the supreme test: would you affirm your life if you had to live it again infinitely?) — are, for Vervaeke, genuine attempts that ultimately fail. They do not recover the participatory knowing that was lost; they substitute a new valorization of individual creative power that produces its own forms of idolatry (Ep 23).

**Nietzsche as proto-prophet**: Vervaeke places Nietzsche at the beginning of the "prophets of the meaning crisis" arc: the diagnosticians (Nietzsche, Heidegger) who saw the problem most clearly but could not find the solution, and the positive prophets (Corbin, Jung, Tillich, Barfield) who pointed toward the recovery. Nietzsche's diagnosis is indispensable; his cure is incomplete (Ep 23, Ep 46).

**Heidegger's critique**: Heidegger argued that Nietzsche remained trapped within the metaphysical tradition he tried to overcome: the will to power is still a metaphysics of presence (just valorizing process rather than stasis), and the Übermensch is still a variant of the Cartesian subject (a hyper-autonomous self-creator). What Nietzsche failed to do was question the very framework of subjectivity that the tradition had produced. This is Vervaeke's indirect agreement with Heidegger (Ep 47).
