# Mary Douglas

> British social anthropologist whose work on purity, danger, and the inter-categorical gives Vervaeke his cognitive account of **horror** — explaining why category violations are experienced as threatening rather than merely puzzling.

## Role in the argument
Douglas's *Purity and Danger* (1966) is the source for Vervaeke's account of horror as the encounter with the **inter-categorical**: things that violate the categorical distinctions by which a community organizes and makes intelligible its world. Her key insight, applied by Vervaeke (Ep 34): biblical purity codes (Leviticus) are not arbitrary hygiene rules but express deep cognitive distinctions — "unclean" creatures are those that violate the expected correlation of form, locomotion, and habitat. The pig has a cloven hoof (proper to cattle, which chew cud) but does not chew cud; shellfish are in the sea but do not swim in a fish-like way. They fall into cracks between categories.

Vervaeke takes Douglas's insight beyond dietary codes: purity systems are relevance-maintaining category systems, and violations register on a continuum from yucky → uncanny → eerie → horror, with horror occurring when violations target the *core* categorical distinctions on which intelligibility depends (person/animal, living/dead, consuming/generating). Monsters are the cultural articulations of these inter-categorical threats. This reframes horror not as fear of predation (physical danger) but as existential threat to cognitive grip on reality — the threat of madness, not death (Ep 34).

This connects to Vervaeke's broader account: RR is the real-time maintenance of our salience landscape and categorical structure. When that structure itself is threatened — when things are inter-categorical at a deep level — RR cannot establish optimal grip, and the result is the phenomenology of horror. Douglas's insight thus grounds the negative face of the numinous in the cognitive science of relevance realization.
