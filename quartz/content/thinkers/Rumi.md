# Rumi

> Persian Sufi poet and mystic (1207–1273); born Jalal al-Din Muhammad Balkhi; after the death of his spiritual companion Shams of Tabriz, produced the *Masnavi* and the *Diwan-e Shams* — two of the greatest mystical poetic works in any language; deeply shaped by Neoplatonism as filtered through Islamic philosophy.

## Role in the argument
Rumi enters AMC primarily as a symbol of the Persian Sufi tradition that Henry Corbin retrieved as a resource for the West's meaning crisis. Corbin's engagement with the imaginal world of Persian Sufism — the realm between intellect and sense, accessible through the creative imagination — was shaped by Sufi poets like Rumi, Suhrawardi, and Ibn Arabi. Vervaeke cites Rumi as evidence that the mystical traditions across cultures developed similar psycho-technologies for the cultivation of participatory knowing (Ep 49, Ep 50).

**The Reed Flute**: Rumi's *Masnavi* begins with the image of the reed flute crying for the reed bed from which it was cut — a symbol of the soul's longing for union with its source, which is simultaneously the source of its music. This participatory metaphor — loss and longing as the condition of creative expression — embodies the Platonic eros and the Neoplatonic anagoge in poetic form. For Vervaeke, it illustrates how the mystical traditions aesthetically transmitted the deepest insights of participatory knowing in forms that bypass propositional resistance (Ep 50).

**Fana**: Rumi's poetry consistently circles around fana (annihilation) — the dissolution of the ego-self in union with the beloved (God, truth, the One). This is structurally parallel to what Vervaeke calls transframing at its limit: not merely a shift in framing but a dissolution of the framing self. In the context of the Continuity Hypothesis, fana represents the far end of the continuum from ordinary insight through flow to mystical union (Ep 50).

**Corbin's retrieval**: Corbin's interpretation of Rumi and the Persian Sufi tradition as mapping an "imaginal world" (neither purely subjective imagination nor the external objective world) provided Vervaeke with a key resource: the tradition that most clearly developed the transjective middle ground between the Cartesian poles (Ep 49).
