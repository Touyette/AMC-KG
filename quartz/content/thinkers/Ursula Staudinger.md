# Ursula Staudinger

> German developmental psychologist (b. 1959); co-founder of the Berlin Wisdom Paradigm with Paul Baltes at the Max Planck Institute for Human Development; developed empirical methods for assessing wisdom as expert knowledge in the domain of fundamental life pragmatics.

## Role in the argument
Staudinger enters AMC as a co-developer of the Berlin Wisdom Paradigm — the most influential empirical research program on wisdom in psychology. Vervaeke engages the BWP to establish that wisdom is a real cognitive phenomenon with identifiable criteria, not just a vague honorific. The BWP identifies wisdom as a form of expert knowledge that integrates cognition, motivation, and life experience, providing the empirical counterpart to Vervaeke's philosophical account of wisdom as the dynamic integration of all four kinds of knowing (Ep 43).

**Berlin Wisdom Paradigm**: Staudinger and Baltes defined wisdom as "expert knowledge in the fundamental pragmatics of life" — knowing what matters in life, how to pursue it, and how to navigate uncertainty and change. They identified five criteria: rich factual knowledge about life, rich procedural knowledge about life, lifespan contextualism (understanding that life is shaped by its context), value relativism and tolerance (recognizing that different people have different but valid life paths), and knowledge about the uncertainties of life. Participants were presented with life dilemmas and their responses coded for these criteria (Ep 43).

**Wisdom as rare**: BWP research found that genuine wisdom (as they operationalized it) is rare — most people perform moderately on the criteria; a small number perform exceptionally well. This supports Vervaeke's claim that wisdom requires genuine development and cultivation, not just intelligence or experience. The Solomon Effect (Grossman) and the BWP converge: wisdom is a specific form of knowing that doesn't automatically accumulate with age or intelligence (Ep 43).

**Limitations Vervaeke notes**: The BWP focuses primarily on propositional and procedural knowledge — what one knows about life. Vervaeke argues this misses the perspectival and participatory dimensions of wisdom: the transformation of what one finds salient, how one encounters the world, and one's participatory self-understanding. The BWP is a valuable empirical anchor but doesn't capture the full ecology of wisdom that Vervaeke develops (Ep 43).
