# Epicurus

> Greek philosopher (341–270 BCE); founder of Epicureanism; developed a philosophy centered on ataraxia (tranquility) and aponia (freedom from bodily pain) as the highest pleasures; emphasized friendship, withdrawal from political life, and philosophy as therapy for existential fear; for Vervaeke, the Epicurean response to the domicide of the Hellenistic period.

## Role in the argument
Epicurus enters AMC in the context of the Hellenistic responses to the meaning crisis of the ancient world (Ep 14). The collapse of the polis — the political community that had provided one of the three orders — left Athenians and others without the cultural home that had structured meaning. Epicurus's response to this domicide was to create a substitute community: the Garden, a philosophical commune centered on friendship and mutual philosophical practice (Ep 14).

**Ataraxia and the therapy of desire**: Epicurus diagnosed fear and false desire as the sources of suffering: fear of death, fear of the gods, and the craving for pleasures that exceed what nature requires. His therapy was philosophical: argument against the fear of death (when death is, I am not; when I am, death is not — it cannot harm me), argument against fear of divine punishment (the gods are too blessed to concern themselves with human affairs), and the cultivation of simple pleasures and friendship as sufficient for happiness (Ep 14).

**The Garden as substitute polis**: The Epicurean Garden was a deliberate attempt to recreate the conditions for meaningful community outside the disrupted polis. In the Garden, men and women (including slaves and prostitutes — radically egalitarian by ancient standards) lived together in philosophical friendship (philia), practicing the mutual cultivation of wisdom. This is an early example of what Vervaeke calls a community of practice — a social structure organized around shared transformative practices (Ep 14).

**Domicide and the Garden**: Vervaeke reads Epicureanism as a sophisticated response to domicide: when the political home is destroyed, build a philosophical home. The Garden is religio in a small-scale, communal form — re-linking practitioners to each other and to the practices that cultivate ataraxia. The limitation is that the Garden is a withdrawal from rather than a transformation of the world (Ep 14).

**Contrast with Stoicism**: Epicurus's withdrawal from politics contrasts sharply with the Stoic engagement: the Stoics sought to remain active in whatever political sphere they inhabited (Marcus Aurelius governing an empire). Both were responses to domicide, but with opposite orientations — withdrawal vs. engagement. Vervaeke uses this contrast to explore the range of possible responses to the loss of the cultural home (Ep 14).
