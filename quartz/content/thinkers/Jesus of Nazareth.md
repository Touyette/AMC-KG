# Jesus of Nazareth

> Jewish teacher and prophet (c. 4 BCE–30 CE); Vervaeke focuses not on the theological question of his divinity but on his teachings and practices as wisdom-psychotechnology: his proclamation of agape, his call to metanoia (radical transformation), his practice of forgiveness, and his radical extension of the neighbor-love command as responses to the perennial problems of alienation, parasitic processing, and the reflectiveness gap.

## Role in the argument
Jesus enters AMC in Episodes 15–16 not as the object of Christological doctrine but as a practitioner and teacher of the wisdom-transformation tradition. Vervaeke reads Jesus's ministry in terms of what cognitive-existential transformation he was attempting to bring about in people — what psychotechnological practices he was developing and teaching for addressing the perennial problems (Ep 15).

**Agape**: Jesus's teaching of agape (love of neighbor, enemy, the stranger) is the most radical extension of the love-command in the Jewish tradition. For Vervaeke, agape is not an emotion but a mode of relation — the mode in which you attend to and care for the conditions for another's personhood and flourishing, regardless of prior relationship. This is a profound psychotechnology: it reconfigures the salience landscape so that others show up as ends in themselves rather than as instruments or obstacles (Ep 15, Ep 38).

**Metanoia**: Jesus's central call is to metanoia — conventionally translated "repentance" but more accurately: radical transformation of the mind, a restructuring of the salience landscape and mode of existence. Metanoia is not feeling sorry for past actions but becoming a different kind of person who perceives and acts differently. This is what Vervaeke means by transformative experience at the most radical level (Ep 15).

**Forgiveness as psychotechnology**: Jesus's practice of forgiveness — of tax collectors, prostitutes, the socially marginal — is a specific intervention into the structures of shame, exclusion, and reciprocity that normally constrain transformation. Forgiveness releases people from the social-cognitive grip of past identities and opens the space for genuine transformation. In cognitive terms, it interrupts the parasitic processing that locks people into shame cycles (Ep 15).

**The Kingdom of God**: Jesus's proclamation of the Kingdom (basileia) of God is not primarily a spatial or temporal claim ("a place you go after death") but an existential claim: a mode of existence in which the structures of agape, forgiveness, and mutual transformation are enacted. The Kingdom is present wherever these practices are active (Ep 15).
