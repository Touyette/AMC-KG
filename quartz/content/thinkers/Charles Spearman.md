# Charles Spearman

> British psychologist (1863–1945); pioneered the statistical technique of factor analysis and used it to discover the general factor of intelligence (g); argued that all cognitive performances tend to correlate positively (the Strong Positive Manifold), which implies a single underlying cognitive resource or capacity.

## Role in the argument
Spearman enters AMC as the discoverer of the empirical phenomenon that Vervaeke re-describes as relevance realization. The Strong Positive Manifold — the fact that all cognitive tests positively correlate, meaning that whoever does well at one test tends to do well at all others — implies a single underlying capacity g. Vervaeke's proposal is that g is best understood as relevance realization: the organism's domain-general ability to home in on what is relevant in an open-ended environment. This identification gives empirical grounding to the philosophical claim that RR is the central cognitive phenomenon (Ep 31, Ep 42).

**The Strong Positive Manifold**: When psychologists administer many different cognitive tests (vocabulary, spatial reasoning, working memory, pattern recognition, etc.), the scores correlate positively. If you're good at verbal ability, you tend to be good at spatial ability too. This was not theoretically expected and is called the "positive manifold." Spearman used factor analysis to identify the common factor — g — that underlies this correlation (Ep 31).

**g as relevance realization**: Vervaeke argues that what g is measuring is the organism's ability to allocate cognitive resources to what is relevant — to dynamically home in on what matters across wildly different domains. This is why g predicts performance across all cognitive tests: all cognitive tests require you to determine what is relevant in the test situation and allocate processing accordingly. The tests differ in what they require; what they have in common is that RR is needed for all of them. g is the psychometric trace of RR (Ep 31, Ep 42).

**Implications**: If g = RR, then intelligence and wisdom share a deep root. Wisdom is not intelligence applied to good ends but rather the full development of the same capacity that intelligence partially measures. And conversely, the dissociation of intelligence from rationality (dysrationalia) is explained: high g means high RR capacity but doesn't guarantee that RR is well-calibrated — that requires the virtue-cultivation that wisdom traditions provide (Ep 42).
