# Schleiermacher

> Friedrich Daniel Ernst Schleiermacher (1768–1834); German Protestant theologian and philosopher; founder of modern hermeneutics and modern liberal Protestant theology; argued that the essence of religion is not doctrine or ethics but a specific form of feeling — the "feeling of absolute dependence" (Gefühl schlechthinniger Abhängigkeit); deeply shaped by Romantic philosophy.

## Role in the argument
Schleiermacher enters AMC as the immediate intellectual predecessor that Rudolf Otto explicitly responds to and refines. Schleiermacher's account of religion as grounded in an irreducible form of feeling — not reducible to theology or morality — establishes the move that Vervaeke needs: that religion addresses a dimension of experience prior to propositional belief. Otto refines Schleiermacher by arguing the religious feeling is not primarily "absolute dependence" (too passive, too moral) but the numinous — the encounter with the wholly other that is both tremendum and fascinans (Ep 33).

**Feeling of absolute dependence**: Schleiermacher argued that the common ground of all religion, beneath the diversity of doctrines and practices, is a particular form of immediate self-consciousness: the feeling that one is not the ground of one's own existence, but is absolutely dependent on something that is. This is not ordinary emotion but a pre-reflective awareness. For Vervaeke, this anticipates the account of religio as prior to credo: religion is first a mode of experiencing one's relationship to what matters most, before it is a set of propositions about it (Ep 33).

**Hermeneutics**: Schleiermacher also founded modern hermeneutics — the theory of interpretation. He argued that understanding a text (or any human expression) requires a double movement: grammatical interpretation (understanding the linguistic structure) and psychological interpretation (entering the mental world of the author). This anticipates the participatory and perspectival dimensions of understanding that Vervaeke contrasts with merely propositional knowledge.
