# Eleanor Rosch

> American cognitive psychologist (b. 1938); developed prototype theory of categorization; co-author (with Francisco Varela and Evan Thompson) of *The Embodied Mind* (1991); one of the founding figures of 4E cognitive science and its convergence with Buddhist philosophy.

## Role in the argument
Rosch enters AMC at two points: (1) as the developer of prototype theory, which shows that human categorization is not based on necessary and sufficient features but on family resemblance to prototypical exemplars — a finding that supports the 4E account of cognition as ecologically embedded rather than formally logical; (2) as co-author of *The Embodied Mind*, which brought together enactivism, phenomenology, and Buddhist philosophy into the framework that directly informs Vervaeke's 4E cognitive science (Ep 38).

**Prototype theory**: Classical cognitive science (and logical categorization) assumed that concepts are defined by necessary and sufficient features: something is a "bird" if and only if it has wings, feathers, lays eggs, etc. Rosch's research showed that human categorization doesn't work this way: we use prototypes (robins) and judge category membership by resemblance to the prototype, not by feature-checking. Categories have fuzzy boundaries, graded membership, and basic levels (the most natural, cognitively efficient level of categorization). This is evidence that categorization is organism-environment relative, not purely formal (Ep 38).

**Basic level categories**: Rosch found that there is a "basic level" of categorization — the level at which categories are most informative and cognitively efficient for human purposes (chair, not furniture and not Eames chair; dog, not mammal and not collie). The basic level is determined by the structure of human embodied interaction with the world — the level at which our motor programs, perceptual gestalt, and semantic networks are most tightly correlated. This is relevance realization at work: the basic level is where the organism-environment interaction is most tightly coupled (Ep 38).

**The Embodied Mind**: Co-authored with Varela and Thompson, *The Embodied Mind* brought together Merleau-Ponty's embodied phenomenology, Varela's enactivism, and Buddhist Madhyamaka philosophy into a comprehensive critique of computational cognitive science. It argued that cognition is grounded in embodied, environmentally embedded action — not formal manipulation of internal representations. It also directly engaged with Buddhist philosophy of mind as a cross-cultural convergence on the same insights. This is the primary theoretical text for the 4E framework Vervaeke draws on (Ep 38).
