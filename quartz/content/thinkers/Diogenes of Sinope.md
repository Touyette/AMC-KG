# Diogenes of Sinope

> Greek Cynic philosopher (c. 412–323 BCE); student of Antisthenes; lived in a large ceramic jar (pithos) in Athens; owned nothing but a cloak, a staff, and a cup (which he eventually discarded when he saw a child drinking from cupped hands); Alexander the Great reportedly offered him any favor and he asked only that Alexander stand out of his light.

## Role in the argument
Diogenes enters AMC as the extreme case of enacted philosophy over propositional philosophy — a living performance of the Cynic ideal. For Vervaeke, Diogenes is important not as a philosophical writer but as a practitioner: someone who turned his entire life into an embodied argument about what matters and what doesn't. This distinction between propositional philosophy (doctrines to be believed) and enacted philosophy (a form of life to be lived) is central to Vervaeke's account of the sapiential tradition and why wisdom requires all four kinds of knowing, not just propositional (Ep 14).

**Enacted philosophy**: Diogenes famously did not write philosophy — he lived it. His interventions were performances: masturbating in public to challenge sexual shame; eating in the marketplace when convention said one should eat at home; walking with a lamp in daylight looking for "an honest man." These are not arguments but demonstrations — they enact the Cynic position that social convention has no rational basis and that the philosophical life requires actually disrupting these conventions, not just theorizing about them. For Vervaeke, this is an extreme case of participatory knowing as philosophical practice (Ep 14).

**Cosmopolitanism**: When asked where he was from, Diogenes is said to have answered "I am a citizen of the world" (kosmopolites) — the first recorded use of this term. This is not an abstract political position but a direct expression of the Cynic rejection of the polis as a source of identity and meaning. In the context of domicide (the loss of the polis as home-world), Diogenes's cosmopolitanism is a direct response: the philosophical life provides a home that no political catastrophe can take away (Ep 14).

**The encounter with Alexander**: The anecdote of Alexander standing before Diogenes (who was sunbathing) and offering him anything — and Diogenes asking only that Alexander stop blocking his light — became one of antiquity's paradigmatic stories. It encapsulates the Cynic judgment that external power and wealth are less valuable than the simple conditions for natural life; the man who wants nothing is more powerful than the man who rules the world.
