# Siddhartha Gautama

> The historical and mythological figure who embodies the Indian Axial Revolution; his awakening from modal confusion becomes the paradigm for the Buddhist path and a model for responding to the meaning crisis.

## Role in the argument
Vervaeke reads Siddhartha's biography as a myth in the technical sense — not a false story about the past but a symbolic story about perennial patterns operative now. The palace represents life locked in the having mode; the Four Signs (sickness, old age, death, the peaceful mendicant) shatter that modal enclosure irreversibly. Siddhartha's attempt at radical asceticism is read as still operating within the having mode — just negating rather than transcending self-indulgence. The discovery of the Middle Path, triggered by hearing a musician tell an apprentice that strings must be "not too tight, not too loose," represents the insight that awakening requires optimization rather than maximization or minimization. Sati — the practice of remembering the being mode — is his core psycho-technology. His enlightenment (the non-dual state of prajña) is the systematic insight that transforms the whole agent-arena relationship. His title "Buddha" means "the awakened one" — and "awakening" becomes the master metaphor for the series. His refusal to answer metaphysical questions about Nirvana encapsulates Vervaeke's key point: the Buddhist path is about gaining wisdom (transforming the salience landscape), not acquiring new propositional knowledge (Ep 07, Ep 08, Ep 09, Ep 10).
