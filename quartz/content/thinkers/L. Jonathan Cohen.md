# L. Jonathan Cohen

> British philosopher (1923–2006); argued against the heuristics-and-biases school's claim that human reasoning is systematically irrational; claimed that ordinary human reasoning is normatively valid by definition — people cannot be systematically irrational in their native cognitive mode; Stanovich and Cherniak responded to this position in developing their accounts of bounded rationality.

## Role in the argument
Cohen enters AMC as a foil and target in the rationality debates (Ep 40, Ep 41). His position — roughly, that apparent cognitive biases are actually rational by the standards appropriate to ordinary human reasoning, and that we should revise our normative standards rather than condemn ordinary cognition — is the extreme rationality-charity position. Both Stanovich (who argues for the reality of dysrationalia) and Cherniak (who argues for "minimal rationality" given computational constraints) respond to this charitable view (Ep 41).

**The rationality debate**: The debate is about how to interpret the findings of Kahneman and Tversky: people systematically deviate from statistical norms. Cohen argued: either (a) our cognitive systems are the standard for rationality (we can't be systematically irrational), or (b) we need to revise the normative standard to fit actual cognitive performance. Stanovich and others argued: the evidence for real departures from good reasoning is strong; the answer is not to revise the standard but to explain why our systems sometimes fail to meet it — which is where bounded rationality and dysrationalia come in (Ep 41).
