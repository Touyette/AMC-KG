# Charles Stang

> American scholar of early Christianity and mysticism; professor at Harvard Divinity School; author of *Our Divine Double* (2016); traced the "divine double" motif across early Jewish, Christian, and Platonic sources; for Vervaeke, the historical scholar who grounds the divine double as a cross-traditional structure for understanding the aspiration toward one's ideal self.

## Role in the argument
Stang enters AMC as the scholarly source for the [[Divine Double]] concept. His research demonstrates that across early Jewish, Christian (especially Gnostic), Platonic, and Persian sources there is a recurring motif of the divine or sacred alter-ego: a second self, higher self, or angelic companion that represents the person's ideal or transcendent counterpart. Vervaeke uses this material to ground his account of the aspiration structure and the divine double as a psychotechnological tool for self-transformation (Ep 48).

**Divine double in historical sources**: Stang traces the divine double across: (1) early Jewish apocalyptic texts (the heavenly self in Sefer Hekhalot); (2) Platonic daimon (Socrates' daimonion, the divine companion that guides one's philosophical life, and Plato's account in the Timaeus of the divine part of the soul that aspires upward); (3) Gnostic heavenly counterpart (the "angel" or "twin" that the self must recognize and unite with for salvation — famously in the Acts of Thomas and the "Hymn of the Pearl"); (4) Syriac Christianity's concept of the "angelic image" each person has in heaven (Ep 48).

**The divine double and aspiration**: Vervaeke appropriates Stang's scholarship to develop the psychological-cognitive account of the divine double as the image of one's aspired-to self — the S2 in Callard's aspiration schema. The divine double is not a supernatural being but the participatory-perspectival projection of what one is becoming: the normatively prior self that guides and motivates the aspiration process before the transformation is complete (Ep 48, Ep 49).
