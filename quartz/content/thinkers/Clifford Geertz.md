# Clifford Geertz

> American cultural anthropologist (1926–2006); author of *The Interpretation of Cultures* (1973); developed "thick description" as the methodology of interpretive anthropology; argued that culture is a system of symbols and meanings that humans both produce and inhabit; proposed that religion is a cultural system producing moods and motivations that feel uniquely realistic.

## Role in the argument
Geertz enters AMC primarily through his concept of "meta-meaning" — the way religion functions as a cultural system that does not merely transmit beliefs but sets up an interpretive frame within which all of life acquires meaning. Vervaeke uses this to show that the meaning crisis is not just a crisis of individual belief but a collapse of the cultural systems that previously generated the framework of frameworks — the system that made individual meanings possible (Ep 39, Ep 40).

**Religion as cultural system**: Geertz defined religion as "a system of symbols which acts to establish powerful, pervasive, and long-lasting moods and motivations in men by formulating conceptions of a general order of existence and clothing these conceptions with such an aura of factuality that the moods and motivations seem uniquely realistic." This definition captures what Vervaeke calls religio — not a set of doctrines but a transformative engagement with a symbolic system that restructures one's participatory knowing (Ep 39).

**Thick description**: Geertz's methodology requires attending to the web of meanings in which human action is embedded — not just noting what happens (thin description) but understanding what it means within the cultural system (thick description). For Vervaeke, this is the difference between propositional knowledge about a culture and the interpretive knowledge required to genuinely understand it. Thick description requires all four kinds of knowing, not just propositional (Ep 40).

**Meta-meaning**: The key concept Vervaeke extracts from Geertz is that religious systems provide "meta-meanings" — not just answers to specific questions but the framework within which specific questions become meaningful. Religion tells you what kind of thing questions about death, suffering, and purpose are — how to approach them, what vocabulary to use, what would count as an answer. When this meta-meaning system collapses, individual questions become unanswerable not because the answers are missing but because the framework for questions is gone (Ep 39).
