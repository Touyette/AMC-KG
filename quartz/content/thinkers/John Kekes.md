# John Kekes

> American moral philosopher (b. 1936); author of *Moral Wisdom and Good Lives* (1995) and *The Art of Life* (2002); developed the concept of interpretive knowledge as a form of understanding distinct from factual (propositional) knowing; argued that wisdom requires the ability to see the significance of one's situation, not just know facts about it.

## Role in the argument
Kekes enters AMC through his concept of interpretive knowledge — the capacity to grasp what one's factual knowledge means, the significance and relevance it has for one's life and situation. This is precisely the dimension of knowing that Vervaeke argues is missing from accounts of rationality and intelligence that focus only on propositional knowledge: you can know all the facts about your situation and still not understand what they mean for how to live. Interpretive knowledge is close to what Vervaeke calls perspectival knowing applied to one's own life (Ep 40).

**Interpretive knowledge**: Kekes distinguishes descriptive knowledge (knowing that something is the case) from interpretive knowledge (knowing the significance of what you know). A person can know all the relevant facts about a life choice — the probabilities, the consequences, the values at stake — and still not understand what it means for them, what matters about it, how to weigh it. Interpretive knowledge is the capacity to read one's situation — to see it for what it is, in all its significance and connection to what genuinely matters. This is a form of knowing that cannot be fully articulated propositionally (Ep 40).

**Wisdom as moral imagination**: For Kekes, wisdom requires not just factual and procedural knowledge but the capacity to imaginatively engage with the significance of one's situation — to see how different possibilities would play out, how they connect to what one cares about, what they would mean for one's character and relationships. This "moral imagination" is a kind of participatory engagement with one's possibilities, not a computation over facts. Vervaeke uses Kekes to argue that wisdom is the integration of all four kinds of knowing, not reducible to propositional competence (Ep 40).

**Deep continuity with Vervaeke's framework**: Kekes's interpretive knowledge maps closely onto Vervaeke's account of expanded rationality: rationality that goes beyond logical inference to include the cultivation of the faculties (attention, framing, self-understanding) that allow one to grasp what matters in a situation. The interpretive dimension is what turns propositional knowledge into wisdom — the ability to see the relevance of what one knows to what actually matters (Ep 40).
