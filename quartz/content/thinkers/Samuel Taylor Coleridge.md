# Samuel Taylor Coleridge

> English Romantic poet and philosopher (1772–1834); introduced German Romantic and Idealist philosophy (especially Schelling) to the English-speaking world; developed the distinction between primary and secondary imagination; key conduit for the transmission of Naturphilosophie and participatory philosophy to Owen Barfield.

## Role in the argument
Coleridge is primarily important in AMC as the intellectual bridge between German Romanticism and Barfield. Barfield studied Coleridge deeply and drew directly on his philosophy of imagination. Coleridge himself attempted to recover a participatory understanding of nature and mind against the dominant Lockean empiricism and Newtonian mechanicism of his time — attempting to see nature as living and mind-like rather than dead and mechanical (Ep 23, Ep 50).

**Primary and secondary imagination**: Coleridge's distinction between primary imagination (the "living power and prime agent of all human perception" — the unconscious co-participation of mind and world that constitutes ordinary perception) and secondary imagination (the "echo of the former" — the conscious, voluntary, creative imagination that "dissolves, diffuses, dissipates, in order to recreate") maps onto Vervaeke's account of original vs. final participation. The primary imagination is what Barfield calls original participation; the secondary imagination is the beginning of the recovery of participation in a conscious mode (Ep 50).

**Coleridge and German Idealism**: Coleridge absorbed Kant, Fichte, and especially Schelling during his time in Germany. He brought this philosophy into English poetry and criticism, arguing that nature is not a mechanical system but an organic whole in which mind participates. This organicism and its critique of mechanism informed Barfield's own philosophy directly (Ep 23).

**Limitation**: Like the broader Romantic tradition, Coleridge's recovery of participatory philosophy remained largely aesthetic and speculative rather than scientifically grounded. Barfield attempted to complete what Coleridge started: developing the philosophy of imagination into a fuller account of how consciousness evolves and how final participation might be achieved (Ep 50).
