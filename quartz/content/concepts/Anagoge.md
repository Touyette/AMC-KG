# Anagoge

> The upward transformative movement — through contact with increasingly real patterns — in which the self and its grasp of reality mutually transform each other; Plato's term for the process represented in the Myth of the Cave.

## Vervaeke's framing
Anagoge (Greek: "leading up") names the process the Cave myth enacts: as the freed prisoner moves from shadows to fire to sunlight, they are not simply acquiring new information but undergoing participatory transformation. Each step forward brings blindness — a disorientation as the old frame dissolves — followed by adjustment, in which the self catches up to the new level of reality disclosed. Then the process can continue. Crucially, both sides of this are active: the self transforms, and in transforming, gains the ability to perceive patterns it could not before; those new patterns disclose still more reality, which calls for further transformation. This is participatory knowing at its most radical: the knower and the known are not independent but mutually constitutive in an ascending feedback cycle. Vervaeke traces anagoge from Plato through Neoplatonism, medieval mysticism, and Corbin's imaginal world, and treats it as the key to understanding how wisdom traditions use symbols, practices, and texts to afford transformation (Ep 05).
