# Active Open-Mindedness

> Jonathan Baron's cognitive style: trained, dispositional sensitivity to one's own cognitive biases (confirmation bias, essentialism bias, availability bias) plus the active will to counteract them, applied to the right degree — the key missing piece between intelligence and rationality.

## Vervaeke's framing
The empirical gap: intelligence (g) predicts reasoning performance (gr) at only r≈0.3. Something beyond intelligence must account for who is rational. Stanovich identifies this missing piece partly as *mindware* (the psycho-technologies one has internalized — which theories and norms one applies) and primarily as a *cognitive style*: active open-mindedness (AOM), developed from Baron's work on good thinking as a dispositional virtue (Ep 41).

AOM has two components:
1. **Sensitization**: learning the science of cognitive biases — confirmation bias (only seeking confirming evidence), essentialism bias (treating category members as sharing an essence), availability bias (judging probability by ease of recall), myside bias (reasoning from one's own standpoint without seeking alternative views), and many others. The first step is simply knowing these patterns exist and can affect you.
2. **Active counteraction**: deliberately seeking out disconfirming evidence, consulting others to help overcome one's own confirmation bias (they can easily see what you cannot), and internalizing those corrective moves so they become habitual. Not overdone — going too far triggers combinatorial explosion. The right degree is itself a matter of judgment that overlaps with wisdom (Ep 41).

What predicts how well someone cultivates AOM? Not intelligence. Rather: **need for cognition** — the intrinsic drive to seek out and solve problems (curiosity about problems, not just facts) combined with *wonder* (openness to having one's entire worldview questioned, not just local beliefs). Wonder is precisely what makes need for cognition existential rather than merely instrumental (Ep 41).

Vervaeke notes convergence: AOM independently parallels Stoic prosoche (self-examination of one's inner states) and cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) derived from Stoicism. This convergence is not accidental — it marks a genuine insight about the structure of self-corrective rationality and what it demands.
