# Tripartite Psyche

> Plato's three-part model of the psyche: reason (the man), thymos/spiritedness (the lion), and appetite (the monster); wisdom consists in their right ordering so that inner conflict and self-deception are minimized.

## Vervaeke's framing
Plato proposes that inner conflict — the central symptom of foolishness and self-deception — arises because three distinct centers in the psyche operate according to incompatible principles. Reason (located metaphorically in the head) operates by truth and falsity, grasps abstract and long-term goals, but is weak on its own. Appetite (in the gut and genitals) operates by pleasure and pain, prioritizes the immediate and the superficial, and is disproportionately powerful due to hyperbolic discounting. Thymos (in the chest) operates by honor and shame, tracks social and cultural norms, and can pursue medium-term socially agreed goals. Plato sees Socrates' method — taking rational reflection into the social arena — as the way the man trains the lion, and the man and lion together can tame the monster. Wisdom is an internal justice: each part is allowed to function optimally without suppressing the others, producing the fullness of being. Vervaeke notes that Freud's id/ego/superego and the neuroscientific reptilian/mammalian/neocortical division both rediscover this Platonic structure (Ep 05).
