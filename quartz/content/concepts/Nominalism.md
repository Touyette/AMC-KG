# Nominalism

> Ockham: only particulars exist in reality; universals are names (nomina) imposed by language; the world has no inherent rational order — whatever patterns we find in it, we are creating through how we speak.

## Vervaeke's framing
Ockham's starting point is theological: God's primary faculty is will, not reason. God does not create the world according to eternal rational principles — he imposes order by arbitrary fiat. This means any rational order we find in the universe is contingent on God's will, not a feature of reality's inherent structure. We human beings, made in God's image, are also primarily will-beings: we impose patterns rather than discover them (Ep 20).

The philosophical result is Nominalism. Look at three books on a table: they all belong to the category "book." In the Aristotelian/Platonic view, they really share a form — there is something real about book-ness that groups them together in nature. Ockham denies this: there is nothing in reality that groups these three things together. What groups them is only the name — the word "book" — and the way I use language. The patterns I find in the world are patterns I create by speaking. Only raw individuals exist; universals are mere names (Ep 20).

For Vervaeke, Nominalism is catastrophic for the Augustinian synthesis. It severs the **conformity theory of knowing**: there is nothing inherently rational or intelligible about the world for the mind to conform to. Knowing can no longer be a process of becoming more real by connecting with what is more real. Instead, knowing becomes inner coherence of propositions — achieving logical consistency within one's inner language. This feeds directly into the experience-as-veil problem that Copernicus makes acute: if the world has no inherent intelligible structure, then our perceptual experience is not contact with reality but an overlay of our meaning-making machinery onto fundamentally meaningless raw individuals (Ep 20).
