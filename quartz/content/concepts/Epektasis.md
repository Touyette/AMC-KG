# Epektasis

> Gregory of Nyssa/Eriugena: the structure of infinite self-transcendence within an inexhaustible God — no final rest, no achieved terminus, but ongoing disclosure of the inexhaustibleness of the ground of being.

## Vervaeke's framing
Epektasis (from Gregory of Nyssa, taken up by John Scotus Eriugena in the Eastern Orthodox Neoplatonic tradition) contrasts with the standard teleological model of salvation: in that standard model, the spiritual journey ends at a promised land, a rest in God, the achieved vision of God in which the pursuit self-dissolves. Epektasis denies this structure entirely (Ep 50).

For Gregory and Eriugena, God is inexhaustible — the ground of being cannot be fully had, not even in mystical rest. What the human being is engaged in is therefore not movement toward a terminus but constant disclosure of the inexhaustibleness of the ground of being itself. The transjectivity and transframing never stop. There is no resting — only the ongoing ecstatic unfolding of what the ground of being is (Ep 50).

**Why Tillich appropriates it**: Tillich's method of correlation and his notion of faith as ultimate concern both require epektasis. If the ground of being could be "arrived at" and "possessed," it would become an idol — an object in the having mode. Epektasis is the structural guarantee against idolatry: the ground of being always exceeds any frame one brings to it, perpetually drawing one into deeper ekstasis and further transframing. The method of correlation is therefore not a finite anagogic loop but an infinite epektatic spiral (Ep 50).

**Connection to infinity of reality**: Epektasis is the existential-theological equivalent of what Barfield and the early Romantics call the infinity of reality (inexhaustible moreness) — the combinatorially explosive surplus that draws finite beings into ongoing self-transcendence. Both are ways of saying: reality's inexhaustibleness means that finitude can never rest in a completed frame; the finite always longs for and is drawn toward what continually exceeds it (Ep 50).

**Connection to aspiration**: Epektasis is also the theological elaboration of what Callard describes as proleptic rationality and non-logical identity: because S2 is always normatively ahead, the trajectory of aspiration is itself without a final terminus. The sacred second self is not a destination to arrive at and possess but an anagogic pole that draws one through successive non-logical identities indefinitely.
