# Symbol

> A participatory, transjective representation that not only refers to something but *exemplifies* it through participation — getting the agent to activate the underlying cognitive machinery that constitutes the capacity being symbolized; distinct from a sign, which merely points.

## Vervaeke's framing
Vervaeke distinguishes symbol from sign (Ep 34). A **sign** refers: a heart makes you think of love. A **symbol** does more — it refers AND exemplifies by getting you to *participate* in what it refers to. Kissing someone is closer to a symbol than a sign: it does not merely represent intimacy, it activates the machinery of the agent-arena relationship that constitutes intimate reciprocal connection. The symbol draws you into participatory and perspectival knowing, not just propositional knowing.

Symbols achieve this through **profound metaphor** — not ornamental metaphors of speech (which are already pervasive and structure cognition), but the deeper metaphors that organize the way we make sense of the world. Vervaeke draws on the justice/balance-scale example: justice is abstract and hard to hold in mind. The symbol of the blindfolded woman holding scales does not merely remind us of justice — it activates the path back through the **exaptation chain** (Michael Anderson's neural reuse / circuit reuse). The cerebellum originally evolved for physical balance — coordinating multiple sensory modalities into smooth integrated movement. Through exaptation, it has been recruited to handle complex coordination of any kind, including the weighing and balancing of competing considerations in justice. By invoking the balance symbol, one traces back down this chain, activating the cerebellum's integration machinery, and brings that machinery up to give participatory-perspectival insight into what it is to be just (Ep 34).

This process is **bidirectional** — not merely Lakoff's "bottom-up projection" (embodied practice projected up into abstract thought). There is also a top-down selection and activation: the abstract domain (justice) selects which embodied machinery to reactivate (balance, not attack). Both directions interact: the symbol creates a living feedback loop between the abstract domain and its embodied grounding, making participatory knowing possible rather than merely propositional reflection. This is why symbols can "hold in mind" what cannot be held through propositions alone.

Symbols are therefore central to sacredness and religio: they allow one to seriously play with the machinery of Relevance Realization, activating participatory and perspectival knowing of what is typically background (Religio's pre-egoic, pre-normative machinery). Sacred symbols — rituals, myths, icons — are not merely cultural decorations; they are psycho-technologies for navigating the encounter with the numinous (Ep 34, Ep 35).

Ep 35 synthesizes the full structure of the symbol into the **EPIC** framework — four defining properties:

- **Ek-stasis** (*ek-stasis* = to stand outside oneself): symbols help one stand beyond one's current worldview/framing; they are transgressive, insinuating themselves into the current frame but destabilizing it, driving the agent toward a more expansive one. They are *ecstatic* in the literal Greek sense.
- **Participatory**: worked out in full detail in Ep 34–35; symbols work by getting you to participate in what they refer to, not merely represent it.
- **Integrative-anagogic**: not merely part-to-whole integration, but the coupled co-optimization of self and world in Anagoge; the symbol integrates the agent *and* discloses a new arena, simultaneously and mutually.
- **Complex**: the reality a symbol discloses is multifaceted, not a single static thing (Athena = wisdom, weaving, warfare; all drawn together). The symbol affords a radical reconfiguration of the salience landscape, connecting normally disconnected things.

The operative dynamic of a symbol at work is **symbolic resonance** (Ep 35). Drawing on Michael Polanyi's subsidiary/focal awareness (SA/FA) model: in ordinary perception, subsidiary processes are integrated into focal awareness of the object one is interested in. In symbolic engagement, the symbol is the focal object but the real interest is in the *integration of subsidiary processes themselves* — this is how one becomes more integrated, more patriotic, more just, through contemplating a symbol (flag, scales). Then what can happen is that through this powerful integration, the symbol *discloses* reality — the breath reveals impermanence and interconnectedness; the scales reveal the process-nature of justice. This disclosure draws one in further, powering more integration, which powers more disclosure: Anagoge. Symbolic resonance is the self-amplifying cycle of integration ↔ disclosure that symbols uniquely afford (Ep 35).

The breadth of human symbolic capacity is illustrated by Ramachandran's Buba/Kiki experiment: when shown a spiky star-shape and a rounded blob-shape and asked which is "Buba" and which is "Kiki," virtually everyone answers the same way. This cross-modal integration (between sound-shape and visual-shape) happens instantaneously and without prior learning — the cerebellum is doing multi-modal symbolic binding before any deliberate processing. We are symbolic animals at the most basic levels of cognition.
