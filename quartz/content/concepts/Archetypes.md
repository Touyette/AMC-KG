# Archetypes

> Jung's psychological analog of Platonic forms: structural-functional organizing patterns that regulate how the psyche self-organizes; virtual engines of salience landscaping; imaginal (not merely imaginary); the formative founding patterns by which the psyche makes itself.

## Vervaeke's framing
Jung's archetypes (*arche* = foundational/originary; *typos* = patterns) are the structural-functional organizations by which the psyche self-organizes. They are explicitly NOT images in the mind — that is the imaginary misreading that Vervaeke (following Jung) rejects. Archetypes are imaginal: they are virtual engines, systems of constraints that shape how the psyche self-organizes by regulating salience landscaping (Ep 49).

**What archetypes do (not what they are)**: The hero archetype being active in someone does not mean they carry hero-images in their conscious mind. It means their salience landscape is being reorganized toward heroic patterns — they perceive, respond to, and engage with the world in increasingly heroic ways. The archetype is best understood adverbially (a way of anagogically coming-to-be) rather than adjectivally (an inner possession). This is the imaginal mode: the archetype mediates between the abstract form (the structural-functional pattern of heroism) and the concrete sensory world, transforming the agent in the process (Ep 49).

**Archetypes as virtual engines**: Like Juarrero's virtual engines and Aristotle's character virtues, archetypes are systems of enabling constraints that open up rather than foreclose possibilities. They have a life of their own — they are autopoietic in the sense that they self-organize within the psyche as the psyche self-organizes. They are the way the psyche makes itself (Ep 49).

**The archetype–Platonic form connection**: Jung is much more willing than Freud to acknowledge Plato's influence. Archetypes are the psychological versions of Platonic forms: structural-functional patterns that organize experience. But unlike Platonic forms (which are abstract objects in a transcendent realm), archetypes are understood by Jung as part of the psyche's self-organizing structure — they are immanent within the process of individuation (Ep 49).

**Ego and Self as archetypes**: The ego is the archetype of the conscious mind — the virtual engine regulating self-organization of conscious experience. The Self is the archetype of the archetypes: the form of forms, the principle of autopoiesis itself — the ultimate virtual engine that constellates all other virtual engines so the psyche can continue its self-organizing process. The Self is what Vervaeke calls the sacred second self. The relationship between ego and Self, mediated through the imaginal archetypes, is the process of individuation (Ep 49).

**Why Jung criticizes fundamentalism/literalism**: Literalism reduces the imaginal archetypes to imaginary images — to possessible inner objects rather than transformative virtual engines. This causes psychic *inflation*: the ego pretends to be complete in itself, takes on the role of the Self, and loses the aspirational dynamic of individuation. This is deeply analogous to onto-theology's error of treating Being as a supreme being (Ep 49).
