# Reflectiveness Gap

> Velleman's name for the fundamental trade-off between immersive involvement (wantonness) and reflective self-monitoring: too little reflection undermines agency through impulse-conflict; too much reflection (Hamlet) paralyzes action by disconnecting from the motivational machinery; the right balance cannot be fixed and must be dynamically calibrated — this is one of the Perennial Problems arising from the self-reflective nature of RR.

## Vervaeke's framing
Vervaeke draws on Frankfurt and Velleman to articulate this perennial problem (Ep 36). Frankfurt's *wanton* is a being who acts with no reflection at all — completely impulsively. The problem: impulses conflict with each other, undermine each other, and the agent loses genuine agency to the chaos of competing drives (Plato's inner conflict). So reflection helps: by stepping back from a perspective, one gains awareness of that perspective rather than being swept along by it — a transparency/opacity shift. One can be in the thirsty perspective (swept along) or curious about it (observing from outside).

But Velleman's insight: this reflective stepping-back cannot simply be maximized. Hamlet demonstrates the failure mode. Hamlet has extraordinary powers of self-reflection; he is always stepping back to look at his looking. The result: he is completely disconnected from the motivational machinery that is required for decisive action. He cannot act. His reflectiveness has opened a gap so large that the bridge between cognition and motivation is lost. This is the **Reflectiveness Gap** — opening it is necessary for agency (against wantonness), but opening it too wide destroys agency (produces Hamlet's paralysis).

Crucially, there is no stable midpoint solution. How much reflection is needed is *contextually sensitive and dynamically varying*. This makes the reflectiveness gap an optimization problem that is intrinsically part of RR — it is the same family as the exploit/explore trade-off and the specialize/generalize trade-off. The self-reflection component of RR makes this trade-off unavoidable (Ep 36).

This connects directly to other perennial problems: the wanton end overlaps with parasitic processing (addictive cycles that are unreflected); the Hamlet end overlaps with existential inertia (unable to commit to action/transformation). The reflectiveness gap is, structurally, the self-reflection dimension of what it means to be an RR system that must also act.
