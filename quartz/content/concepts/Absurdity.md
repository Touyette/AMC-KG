# Absurdity

> Nagel: the experience of absurdity is NOT generated by inferential arguments (all such arguments are logically invalid by symmetry) but is a **clash of perspectives** — a perspectival-participatory phenomenon in which the meaning constituted within one frame is simultaneously undermined by another incommensurable frame; one of the perennial problems arising from the structural (meta-meaning) aspect of RR.

## Vervaeke's framing
Vervaeke draws on Thomas Nagel's article "The Absurd" (Ep 36) for a key anti-propositional insight. The common experience: we feel our lives to be absurd, and we reach for arguments to justify that feeling ("nothing I do matters to people a million years from now," "I'm too small in the vast universe," "I'm going to die"). But Nagel shows all such arguments are invalid by symmetry: if what I do now makes no difference to people a million years in the future, then their opinion of me from a million years in the future also makes no difference to me. The reasoning is symmetric — you can't use distance in time or space to generate asymmetric insignificance without undermining the evaluative framework you're using.

What this means: the arguments are **after-the-fact expressions** of absurdity, not before-the-fact generators. The absurdity is already there at the level of perspectival and participatory knowing; the propositional arguments are attempts to articulate what is already felt at a deeper level. Dropping to the propositional level to explain absurdity is the wrong level.

Absurdity is properly understood as a **clash of perspectives**. The key example: Tom works himself into an emotional state to confess love to Susan, calls, and delivers his declaration passionately — to an answering machine. From Tom's internal perspective, his action is deeply meaningful, emotionally weighty, charged with significance. From the impersonal perspective disclosed by the machine, his action is random noise directed at a device with no meaning whatsoever. The two perspectives are incommensurable and simultaneously present — and this produces the absurd. The same structure generates humor (when the perspective-clash is resolvable into a punchline) or horror (when the clash is so deep that intelligibility itself collapses). All three — absurdity, humor, and horror — involve this perspectival-participatory structure (Ep 36).

Absurdity is one of the structural/meta-meaning perennial problems: it arises when the connectedness between self and world is disrupted. When the agent-arena relationship no longer sustains a coherent perspectival ground, perspectives clash rather than integrate. Susan Wolf's insight connects here: the feeling of meaninglessness ("I'm too small, too insignificant") is a metaphor for being disconnected from something with value independent of one's valuing it — which is ultimately what Religio and Sacredness provide.
