# Three Dimensions of Meaning

> Heintzelman's empirical finding that meaning in life requires coherence (intelligibility), significance (mattering), and purpose (direction) — which Vervaeke shows map exactly onto the Three Orders that Augustine integrated into the medieval synthesis.

## Vervaeke's framing
Vervaeke cites Samantha Heintzelman's psychological research identifying three components of meaning in life: **coherence** (things are intelligible, they make sense and fit together), **significance** (things matter, have value and depth), and **purpose** (life has direction, is moving toward something). The empirical finding is that the more a person has all three, the more meaningful they experience their life to be (Ep 19).

Vervaeke's key theoretical move is to show these three dimensions map exactly onto the Three Orders that Augustine unified in the medieval synthesis. Coherence corresponds to the Nomological Order: the Aristotelian account of how the cosmos is structured and how the mind conforms to it — things fit together intelligibly. Significance corresponds to the Normative Order: the Neoplatonic ladder of being from evil (pure chaos, disintegration) to good (integration, oneness) — things have depth and genuine worth. Purpose corresponds to the Narrative Order: the Christian story of history moving under divine Agape toward a consummation — life has direction. Augustine's synthesis provided all three simultaneously and in mutual support, which is why it sustained civilization for a thousand years and why its collapse is experienced as a crisis of meaning across all three dimensions (Ep 19).
