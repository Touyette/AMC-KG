# Salience Tagging

> The cognitive act of making something stand out as "a this" — singling it out from an undifferentiated background — without yet categorizing what it is; the bridge between pre-categorical FINSTING and the full salience landscape.

## Vervaeke's framing
Salience tagging is the cognitive operation that converts pre-categorical tracking ([[FINSTING]]) into a tagged object of attention. When the mind's enactive demonstrative reference picks out "this one," it thereby tags it as salient — worthy of further processing. The tag is not yet a category (it does not yet classify what the thing is) but it is more than pure tracking (it marks the thing as a focus) (Ep 29).

**The relationship to the salience landscape**: The [[Salience Landscape]] (Vervaeke's four-layer model: salience, presence, depth, significance) has salience as its first and most basic layer. Salience tagging is the process that populates this layer: objects are tagged as salient before they are present, deep, or significant. The tagging happens at the participatory-enactive level and then feeds into the higher levels of the salience landscape (Ep 29).

**Salience tagging and relevance realization**: Salience tagging is one of the earliest expressions of RR: what gets tagged is already an RR output — the machinery has already made a relevance determination at the sub-semantic level. Since the tag precedes categorization, the RR machinery is operating on the world directly, not on representations of the world. This is part of the evidence that RR cannot be reduced to computation over representations (Ep 29).

**From tagging to framing**: Once tagged, an object enters the agent's cognitive workspace and begins to receive more elaborate processing: categorization, affordance detection, inferential elaboration. But the tagging decision — why this object was singled out in the first place — is already a relevance decision that cannot itself be explained by the downstream processing it enables (Ep 29).
