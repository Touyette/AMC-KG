# Akrasia

> Aristotle's term for acting against one's better judgment — the failure of character integration, not willpower, that makes one do the wrong thing even while knowing it is wrong.

## Vervaeke's framing
Akrasia is often translated "weakness of will," but Vervaeke argues this is misleading — it imports Protestant notions of willpower that modern science does not support. What Aristotle actually identifies is a failure of character: you do the wrong thing not because your beliefs are wrong (you know you shouldn't eat the cake) but because you have not sufficiently cultivated the character — the virtual engine — that would make acting rightly natural and automatic. Plato's structure explains why: the monster (appetite) races ahead of the man (reason) because of hyperbolic discounting, and if the lion (thymos) has not been trained to side with reason against appetite, the monster wins. Aristotle's corrective is not to try harder but to cultivate virtues — enabling and selective constraints — through sustained practice so that the right action flows from character rather than requiring constant effortful override. This distinguishes Aristotelian ethics from mere "knowing what's right" (Ep 06).
