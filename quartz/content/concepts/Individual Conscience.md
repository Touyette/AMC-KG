# Individual Conscience

> Luther's elevation of the private inner authority — the self knowing itself — to the sole arbiter of spiritual truth, displacing church, tradition, and institution.

## Vervaeke's framing
The word "conscience" literally means knowing oneself (con-science). Luther's central insight — or error, in Vervaeke's analysis — is that since salvation cannot come through participation in tradition, history, or institution, what remains is the individual's direct relationship to God mediated only by their own inner knowing. This is Luther's "Priesthood of all Believers": no priest, no church, nothing between individual and God. The radical consequence is that individual conscience becomes the final authority in matters of faith. Vervaeke traces this as a double bind: Luther gives people individual conscience as their sole resource while simultaneously insisting that the inner life is wracked with self-deception and self-loathing. The positive legacy includes seeds of democracy within the church; the negative legacy is cultural narcissism and endless fragmentation of Protestantism into denominations (Ep 21). This grammar persists secularly as the "cult of authenticity" — being true to yourself as the ultimate moral authority.
