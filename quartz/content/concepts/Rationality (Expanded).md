# Rationality (Expanded)

> Vervaeke's expanded definition: rationality = **reliably and systematically overcoming self-deception, and affording flourishing through optimization of cognitive processing**; domain-general (not reducible to any domain-specific expertise), and characterized by attending to *how* one reasons (process) rather than fixating on the products of cognition; deeply connected to wisdom.

## Vervaeke's framing
Vervaeke argues (Ep 40) that rationality has been misunderstood — both too narrowly (equated with syllogistic logic) and, on the other side, dismissed as unachievable given the cognitive bias literature. His expanded definition restores and deepens what rationality means.

**Reliably**: not a standard of perfection, certainty, or completeness — but a high probability of functioning well across repeated encounters with relevant problems. The rational person succeeds at this most of the time, not always (Ep 40).

**Systematically**: rationality is domain-general, not domain-specific. This distinguishes it sharply from **expertise**. Expertise in tennis does not transfer to squash (may even interfere). Expertise in physics does not transfer to philosophy. Rationality, by contrast, is supposed to apply across domains — in professional life, friendships, romantic relationships, civic life, existential questions. The systematic character means rational principles are applied not just in one protected domain but wherever they are relevant. The more domains in which a person reliably overcomes self-deception, the more rational (Ep 40).

**Overcoming self-deception**: the core of rationality (following Stanovich) is the capacity to recognize and correct one's own systematic cognitive distortions — failures of critical detachment, belief perseverance, misleaping, confirmation bias. The Rationality Debate experiments all show the same pattern: people acknowledge the correct principle but reliably fail to apply it. This is systematic self-deception — which is exactly what rationality, properly understood, targets. The rational person not only knows the correct principles but has *internalized* them as cognitive habits (Ep 40).

**Critical detachment**: a key capacity for rationality — the ability to evaluate an argument's quality *independently* of whether you agree with its conclusion. If you cannot detach from the product (conclusion) to evaluate the process (argument structure), you cannot reason to conclusions that might be uncomfortable or that revise your prior beliefs. This requires paying attention to *how* you are reasoning, not just what you conclude — a process orientation over a product orientation (Ep 40).

**Goals come under revision**: rationality is not just instrumental (optimizing the pursuit of fixed goals). As one reliably overcomes self-deception, the goals themselves come under review. The aspiring-rational person is open to discovering that their goals were mistaken, parochial, or self-deceptive — and revising them. This connects to Agnes Callard's proleptic/aspirational rationality: you reason toward a self you are not yet (Ep 40).

**Connection to wisdom**: the systematic seeing-through-illusion and into-reality that constitutes wisdom is the same thing as rational self-correction at the highest level. Rationality is a component of wisdom, or more precisely, wisdom is rationality extended to its fullest — operating at the level of existential identity, worldview, and perspectival-participatory knowing, not just propositional reasoning (Ep 40).

**Ep 41 — Intelligence ≠ rationality**: Stanovich and West demonstrate that reasoning errors form a strong positive manifold (gr) just as cognitive tasks form g. This proves errors are at the competence level (they are systematic, not circumstantially driven), defeating Cohen's performance-error response. Cohen's hidden premises — that competence is single and static — are both wrong: we have multiple competencies (dual processing) that can conflict, and those competencies are developmental (Ep 41).

Intelligence (g) and reasoning performance (gr) correlate at best r≈0.3. Intelligence is necessary but nowhere near sufficient for rationality. The classic reductions — rationality = logicality (Descartes), rationality = intelligence (folk psychology) — are both empirically falsified (Ep 41).

Cherniak's finitary predicament argues that formal logic/probability theory demand cognitive suicide, so they cannot be the right normative standard. Stanovich redirects this: the finitary predicament describes *intelligence* (dealing with computational limitations = relevance realization = g), not rationality per se (Ep 41).

The missing pieces beyond intelligence: (a) **normativity on construal** — an independent standard for how problems are *formulated*, not just how inferences are drawn; studied in insight problem-solving; connects to insight as the non-inferential component of rationality; (b) **active open-mindedness** (Baron) — trained dispositional sensitivity to cognitive biases plus active counteraction; predicted by need for cognition (curiosity + wonder), not by g (Ep 41).

Rationality therefore = insight + inference, both operating within a cultivated disposition of self-corrective attention. This is where rationality shades into wisdom.
