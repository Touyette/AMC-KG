# Eros

> In Vervaeke's use: the drive that keeps caring (what one finds salient/relevant) tightly coupled to reality (truth) — Socrates' claim to know "ta erotika" means knowing how to love well, i.e., knowing what genuinely deserves one's care.

## Vervaeke's framing
Eros in ancient Greek is far broader than romantic or sexual love. Vervaeke follows Plato and Socrates in treating it as the fundamental drive toward wisdom — toward knowing what is truly worth caring about. Socrates famously claimed to know only ta erotika ("the erotic things"), which meant he knew how to love well: how to keep what he found salient and relevant in right alignment with what is real. This is wisdom in its most personal form — not accumulating knowledge but caring about the right things in the right way. Eros, for Plato (developed most fully in the Symposium and Phaedrus), is the drive that draws the soul upward through the anagogic ascent: it is attracted by beauty, and beauty is what appears when the logos of something is properly disclosed. Frankfurt's "Reasons of Love" is cited as a contemporary version of the same insight: reason and love need each other, not as antagonists, but as mutually grounding (Ep 04).
