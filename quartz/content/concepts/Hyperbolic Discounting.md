# Hyperbolic Discounting

> The cross-species tendency to reduce the salience of stimuli as they become more distant in time — adaptive because it screens out low-probability futures, but maladaptive when it blinds one to abstract long-term consequences.

## Vervaeke's framing
Vervaeke introduces hyperbolic discounting (via Ainslie and others) to explain why Plato's "monster" (appetite) so reliably dominates the "man" (reason): the future is simply less salient than the present, making the cake now feel much more urgent than the abstract health consequences months away. This is adaptive: the probability of any specific far-future event is genuinely low, and screening them out prevents cognitive overload. But it is maladaptive when the low probability of each specific bad outcome blinds one to the common abstract consequence shared by all of them — like how the low probability of any specific cancer death makes smoking seem safe, even though the aggregate probability of premature death from smoking is very high. The adaptive machine that protects us from anxiety is the same machine that makes us self-destructively procrastinate and give in to temptation. This is what Plato's account of the Tripartite Psyche and the need for wisdom is responding to (Ep 05).
