# Finitary Predicament

> Cherniak's term: finite cognitive systems cannot derive all implications, check all contradictions, or review all assumptions without combinatorial explosion — therefore formal logic and probability theory cannot be the normative standard for human rationality.

## Vervaeke's framing
Cherniak's argument (Scandinavian Journal of Psychology, 1970) is that the normative standards invoked in rationality experiments — formal deductive logic, formal probability theory — are purely algorithmic: they demand certainty and completeness across the entire implication space. But given the combinatorial explosion problem (Ep 28–29), trying to apply these standards comprehensively within any real cognitive system leads to what Cherniak calls "cognitive suicide" — the system collapses under the weight of computation before it can achieve any cognitive goal at all. Therefore: if the normative theory demands something that, if followed, would undermine all cognition, it is the wrong normative theory (Ep 41).

What finite systems actually do — and must do — is relevance realization: they zero in on the implications, contradictions, and assumptions that are relevant to the current situation rather than pursuing exhaustive logical coverage. This is not arbitrary (one does not just choose any implication randomly) — it is governed by the organism's relevance realization capacity. Cherniak's finitary predicament is essentially a negative characterization of what Vervaeke calls the frame problem, and the finitary predicament is resolved (in part) by relevance realization (Ep 28, 41).

Stanovich and West accept Cherniak's argument but redirect it: the finitary predicament describes *intelligence* (the capacity to deal with computational limitations = relevance realization = g), not *rationality*. The correlation between g and reasoning performance (gr) is at best 0.3 — which means intelligence and rationality are empirically distinct. Cherniak correctly identifies a real limit on human cognition, but that limit belongs under the concept of intelligence, and intelligence alone does not explain the systematic biases that constitute irrationality (Ep 41).

Herbert Simon's related concept of **bounded rationality** covers the same terrain: rationality is constrained by limited information, time, and computation, leading to satisficing strategies rather than maximizing ones (Ep 41).
