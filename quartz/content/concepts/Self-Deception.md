# Self-Deception

> Systematic distortion of self-representation — not lying to oneself (which is impossible, since lying depends on you already knowing the truth) but bullshitting oneself by directing and capturing attention in ways that make salience swamp truth-tracking.

## Vervaeke's framing
Vervaeke distinguishes self-deception from lying-to-oneself: you cannot lie to yourself because you already possess the truth, so simply asserting "P" to yourself when you know "not-P" does nothing. But you can bullshit yourself. Attention has two directions: you can direct it voluntarily (making things more salient) and it can be captured (salience draws attention). These two mutually amplify each other: paying more attention to something makes it more salient, which captures more attention, which makes it more salient — a self-organizing cycle. When this cycle dominates, what you find salient overtakes any concern for whether it is true or real. This is self-deception: not lying but a failure of the salience-truth coupling that Socratic wisdom seeks to repair. The Socratic method is designed precisely to interrupt this cycle by forcing the interlocutor to notice that they have been pursuing something (happiness, fame) without understanding it. Inner conflict (Plato's Tripartite Psyche) also drives self-deception by generating the threat signal that triggers egocentrism (Ep 04, Ep 05).
