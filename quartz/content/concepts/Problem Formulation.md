# Problem Formulation

> The process of constructing a representation of a problem — deciding what the constraints are, what counts as a solution, which features are relevant — as distinct from problem-solving; Vervaeke argues that getting the formulation right is the hard part, and insight is always a change in formulation.

## Vervaeke's framing
There is a standard model of problem-solving: you have a problem, you apply strategies, you find a solution. But this model assumes the problem is already correctly formulated — that you already know what you are trying to do, what the constraints are, what counts as progress. Problem formulation is the prior process of constructing that representation. It is not given; it has to be built, and it can be built badly (Ep 27).

**Why formulation is harder than solving**: Given a well-formulated problem, search strategies are well-defined (even if computationally expensive). Given a badly formulated problem, the best solver in the world will find the wrong answer. Most of what we call "stupidity" or "poor judgment" is not failure of inference given a good representation but failure to achieve an adequate representation in the first place. Getting the formulation right is the cognitively demanding part (Ep 27).

**The impasse-insight cycle**: Vervaeke uses insight research to illustrate this: in classic insight problems (the nine-dot problem, the candle problem), subjects become impassed not because they can't solve the problem but because they have formulated it incorrectly — they have imported constraints that the problem statement doesn't actually contain (you *can* go outside the dots; you *can* use the box as a candleholder). The solution requires not more effort within the current formulation but a change of formulation — a restructuring of the problem representation. This restructuring is insight (Ep 27).

**Attentional scaling and formulation change**: Vervaeke's model of insight involves two processes: (1) breaking frame — withdrawing attention from the features that are defining the current (bad) formulation; (2) moving through to a new formulation — allowing a reorganized representation to emerge. The phenomenology is the aha experience: the sudden feeling of the new formulation as obvious and the old one as absurd. Both stages require suspending the tendency to exploit the current frame (Ep 27).

**Multi-aptness**: A well-formulated problem is one where the representation simultaneously satisfies multiple constraints — what Vervaeke calls [[Multi-Aptness]]. Insight solutions feel right partly because the new formulation is multi-apt: it resolves the impasse, explains what was wrong with the old framing, and opens new solution paths all at once. This multi-aptness is why insight solutions feel not just correct but revelatory (Ep 27).

**Connection to wisdom and the meaning crisis**: The meaning crisis is, in part, a problem formulation failure at the cultural scale: modern culture has formulated the questions of meaning, value, and self in ways (Cartesian, propositional, individualist) that make adequate answers impossible. What is needed is not better arguments within the current formulation but a change of formulation — which is why wisdom traditions, not more philosophy, are the relevant resource (Ep 27).
