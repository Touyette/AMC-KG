# Virtue

> Aristotle's term for an excellence of character — a stable disposition to respond appropriately — understood by Vervaeke as a cultivated set of enabling and selective constraints forming part of one's virtual engine.

## Vervaeke's framing
For Aristotle, virtues are not rules or feelings but stable dispositions acquired through practice. They function as the specific constraints that constitute character — the enabling and selective constraints that together form the virtual engine regulating one's development. Courage, for example, is not a rule ("always act thus") nor a feeling, but a cultivated disposition that opens up appropriate responses (enabling) while ruling out excessive ones like recklessness or cowardice (selective). Virtues are acquired dynamically: you become courageous by practicing courageous actions in the right contexts, with the right tuning over time, until the disposition is stable. The word "virtue" itself shares a root with "virtual" (in "virtual engine") — both refer to conditions that shape what is possible, not events that make things happen. Wisdom is the capacity to cultivate the right set of virtues — the right virtual engine — for realizing one's potential as a rational, reflective human being (Ep 06).
