# Gelassenheit

> Eckhart's term for spiritual releasement — the act by which the will ceases its self-assertion and makes an interior space for God to flow in; the inverse of self-will; later taken up by Heidegger as a mode of openness to Being.

## Vervaeke's framing
For Eckhart and the Rhineland Mystics, the problem of spiritual life is that the self's will presses outward — asserts itself, claims its projects, imposes patterns. This self-assertion is the obstacle to Agape. **Gelassenheit** (German: letting-go, releasement) is the act of releasing this self-assertion — negating the will's outward press — so that divine love can flow in rather than being resisted. The complementary movement is **Durchbruch** (breakthrough): the eruption of divine life through the self that has been emptied by Gelassenheit (Ep 20).

Vervaeke situates Gelassenheit within the broader transformation of the normative order after Aquinas. Where Augustine's synthesis involved an anagogic ascent through love-and-reason together, the Rhineland Mystics reconceive the spiritual life as primarily a descent: God flows into the self that has made space through will-negation. This makes inner conflict central to spirituality — the ongoing battle between self-assertion and releasement — rather than the cultivated wisdom of self-transcendence.

Heidegger recovers Gelassenheit in a philosophical register: as a mode of openness and dwelling that contrasts with the will-to-mastery (Gestell) characteristic of modern technological thinking. For Heidegger, Gelassenheit toward things and toward Being is a way of responding to the forgetfulness of Being without reverting to metaphysics (Ep 47+). This shows the durability of Eckhart's conceptual contribution across radically different contexts.
