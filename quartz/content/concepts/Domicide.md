# Domicide

> The destruction of home — not merely as physical place but as the existential condition of being deeply embedded in a coherent agent-arena relationship (place, language, history, religion, community).

## Vervaeke's framing
Domicide (Porteous and Smith) refers to the destruction of home in two forms: (1) *physical destruction* of one's dwelling, and (2) *cultural domicide* — having a physical house but no longer inhabiting a meaningful home. The Hellenistic period produced cultural domicide on a civilizational scale: Alexander's conquests dissolved the Polis, scattering populations across far-flung empires, severing people from the tight Polis identity that had defined their cognition, politics, language, history, and religion. The result was an Age of Anxiety: a loss not of any particular thing but of the entire *constellation of belonging* that constituted a coherent agent-arena relationship.

Vervaeke uses this concept to illuminate both the Hellenistic meaning crisis and the modern one: we currently experience cosmic un-homing — a loss of the sense of being at home in the cosmos itself — because the sacred canopy that previously integrated self, community, nature, and meaning has dissolved. The Hellenistic philosophical schools (Epicureans, Cynics, Stoics) are all therapeutic responses to domicide. The concept returns in later episodes to frame the modern situation (Ep 14, Ep 25).
