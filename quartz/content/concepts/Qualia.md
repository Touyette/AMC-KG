# Qualia

> The purely subjective, secondary qualities of experience — how things taste, feel, look, and sound — that Galileo and Descartes place entirely within the mind, not in the world.

## Vervaeke's framing
Galileo distinguished primary qualities (mathematically measurable, objective, in the object) from secondary qualities (not mathematically describable, purely subjective, only in the mind). The sweetness of honey, the redness of red, the painfulness of pain — these are qualia. Philosophers name this the "secondary qualities" problem; Descartes uses it to argue against Hobbes: matter cannot possess qualia, and yet qualia are central to consciousness. Therefore, no material machine can possess consciousness. This argument is still live in cognitive science and philosophy of mind — it is Descartes's most powerful objection to strong AI (Ep 22). Vervaeke notes that Nagel, Chalmers, and others have formalised this as the "hard problem of consciousness." The existence of qualia is one reason why the Cartesian settlement — mind and matter are completely separate substances — seems to save the soul while simultaneously making its interaction with the body inexplicable.
