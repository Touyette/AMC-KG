# Conatus

> Spinoza's term for the intrinsic tendency of every thing to persevere in its own being — the self-sustaining drive that constitutes the essence of any entity; for Vervaeke, an early philosophical formulation of the deep biological self-organization that the continuity hypothesis grounds in relevance realization.

## Vervaeke's framing
Spinoza argues in the *Ethics* that each thing, insofar as it is in itself, strives to persevere in its own being (conatus suum esse conservandi). This is not an external force applied to things but the very essence of each thing — what it means for something to exist at all is for it to have this drive toward self-maintenance. Vervaeke connects this to the deep continuity hypothesis and to autopoiesis: the same self-sustaining dynamic that Spinoza identified philosophically, Varela's autopoiesis identifies biologically, and Vervaeke's account of relevance realization identifies cognitively. The conatus is the philosophical ancestor of the enactivist account of sense-making (Ep 23, Ep 38).

**Conatus and scientia intuitiva**: In Spinoza's system, conatus operates at three levels of knowing: at the level of imagination (first kind of knowledge — passive, confused) it appears as desire and affect; at the level of reason (second kind — adequate ideas of common notions) it becomes the pursuit of what is genuinely good; and at the level of scientia intuitiva (third kind — direct intuitive knowledge of the whole) it is identical with the intellectual love of God (amor intellectualis Dei). Vervaeke uses this to argue that the same cognitive dynamic — the organism striving to orient itself rightly — operates differently at different levels of knowing, and that wisdom involves integrating all levels (Ep 23).

**Conatus as agent-arena relationship**: For Vervaeke, the philosophical significance of conatus is that it already refuses the Cartesian separation of subject from world: the conatus of a thing is not a property of a subject standing over against an object but the dynamic tendency of the entity to maintain its form-in-relation-to-its-environment. Spinoza's monism (mind and body as two attributes of one substance) makes the conatus a bio-cognitive dynamic, not a mental fact grafted onto a material body (Ep 38).
