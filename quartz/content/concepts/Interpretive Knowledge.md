# Interpretive Knowledge

> Kekes's distinction: descriptive knowledge = knowing that p (grasping the fact); interpretive knowledge = grasping the *significance* of p (understanding what it means, implies, and demands of you); wisdom requires the latter.

## Vervaeke's framing
McKee & Barber invoke Kekes's distinction as one of two key features of wisdom: wisdom is about *how* you know, not what you know, and specifically it is about *interpretive knowing* rather than merely descriptive knowing. Ardelt develops this further (Ep 44): the mistake in product theories of wisdom (like Baltes & Staudinger) is treating wisdom as a matter of having descriptive knowledge — knowing the facts about life, wisdom, virtue. The wise person doesn't just *have* this knowledge; they *realize* it: they grasp its significance in a way that transforms how they perceive, act, and care (Ep 40, 44).

Descriptive knowledge is propositional: the cat is a mammal; one should be honest. It's knowledge that a fact is so. Interpretive knowledge grasps what that fact *means* — its connections, implications, significance for one's own development and action. Vervaeke argues that grasping significance involves *construal* (how one formulates and sizes up the situation) and *relevance realization* (what features are relevant, to whom, in what context). Interpretive knowledge is therefore not a different data structure than descriptive knowledge but a higher-order cognitive achievement: the same content organized around what matters (Ep 44).

This connects directly to understanding. Understanding = interpretive knowledge = grasping the significance, implications, and connections of what one knows. It is pre-propositional in the sense that it involves salience and perspectival knowing that ground and contextualize propositional content. This is why wisdom — which centrally involves understanding — cannot be identified with having correct beliefs or a large store of knowledge (Ep 44).
