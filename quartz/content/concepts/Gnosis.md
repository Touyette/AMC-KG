# Gnosis

> Transformative, salvific, participatory knowing — knowing by being conformed to and thereby transformed by what one loves; identity-level change rather than propositional knowledge.

## Vervaeke's framing
Gnosis is introduced through Paul's 1 Corinthians 13 framework: "then I shall know fully, even as I am fully known." This is not a claim about acquiring more information; it is a claim about achieving full participatory conformity — knowing someone the way one knows a language, a culture, or a deeply intimate partner: by having one's identity shaped through sustained loving engagement. Gnosis is knowing by loving. In Paul's usage, Gnosis Agape (knowing-through-Agape) is the participatory mode that all the other forms of knowledge — prophecy, tongues, faith to move mountains, even martyrdom — are worthless without. The knowing is inseparable from the being-transformed (Ep 16).

Vervaeke situates Gnosis within his broader epistemological framework: it is a species of participatory knowledge (the fourth of the four kinds of knowing), but at its most intensive form — involving identity transformation, not merely skillful engagement. This is also why Gnosis carries the projection danger: the degree to which the knower has unacknowledged self-ignorance, that darkness is poured into the participatory bond and distorts it. The Gnostic movements (Ep 17) will push this idea further, sometimes in productive, sometimes in distorted directions.

Ep 17 gives the fullest definition: Gnosis is the integration of psychotechnologies — ritual context, altered states of consciousness, enacted anagoge — that frees one from existential entrapment (being stuck and stupefied). It activates and transforms perspectival and participatory knowing; it affords sensibility transcendence; it heals fractured agency and broken worldview. This is what the Gnostic movements were pursuing: the Axial Revolution taken to its logical culmination in a practice of radical self-transformation (Ep 17).

**Ep 47 — Corbin's gnosis as dynamic coupling (Avens / Heidegger connection)**: Henry Corbin (drawing on Heidegger) makes explicit what the AMC framework has been building toward: gnosis is participatory knowing understood as a "dynamic coupling" — self-knowing and world-knowing interpenetrating and mutually transforming. Corbin (via Robert Avens, *The New Gnosis*): gnosis is "a salvational redemptive knowledge, because it has the virtue of bringing about the inner transformation of man." Knowing by being transformed; being transformed by knowing — the two sides are inseparable. This is the appropriate response to Dasein (the being whose being is in question): to respond to one's own ontological questioning by engaging in a knowing that is simultaneously self- and world-transformative. The ancient Neoplatonic principle "like can only be known by like" is the deep structure: to know being, you must become attuned to being, which means being changed by it (Ep 47).

**Ep 48 — Gnosis requires the imaginal**: Corbin makes explicit that gnosis is inseparable from the *imaginal* capacity. The imaginal (transjective mediation between intelligible and sensible, subjective and objective, in constant transformative transframing) is the mode in which gnosis operates. Without the imaginal, gnosis becomes impossible — the symbol loses its translucency and trajectory; everything collapses into either subjective fantasy (imaginary) or objective fact. This is why fundamentalism and literalism are anti-gnostic: they freeze the imaginal by refusing the transjective, transformative movement. The imaginal symbol (e.g., Corbin's angel / the divine double) is the concrete cognitive tool for gnosis — it works as gnosis-as-serious-play, enabling the non-logical identity between current self (S1) and aspirational self (S2 / divine double) to be navigated without inference. Liberal education, properly understood, is this kind of gnosis: salvational, identity-transforming, imaginally mediated (Ep 48).

**Ep 50 — Tillich's gnosis and Barfield's poiesis**: Vervaeke makes explicit that Tillich's method of correlation has the full structure of gnosis: it is transformative (not propositional), participatory (faith as ultimate concern, not belief), salvational (the no-thingness of God overcomes the nothingness of despair), and requires an imaginal symbol (God as icon, not idol). Tillich explicitly acknowledges the connection to gnosticism in his "God beyond the God of theism" — the critique of the demiurge-God of classical theism is a Gnostic move. Barfield's poiesis and the recovery of dual-meaning language (pneuma/spiritus; see/understand) similarly function as a form of gnosis: the re-exaptation of sensory-motor machinery through imaginal symbols to enact new forms of self-transcendence is what salvational participatory knowing looks like in the domain of creativity and language (Ep 50).
