# Dysrationalia

> Stanovich's term for the condition of high intelligence paired with poor rationality; the dissociation of IQ from rational processing demonstrates that intelligence and rationality are distinct cognitive capacities, with profound implications for why education and self-improvement cannot just "add more information."

## Vervaeke's framing
Dysrationalia (from Keith Stanovich) names the common but underappreciated condition of being highly intelligent — scoring well on IQ tests, succeeding in technical domains, reasoning fluently within specified problems — while exhibiting systematic failures of rationality: active closed-mindedness, susceptibility to [[Confirmation Bias]], poor probabilistic reasoning in everyday contexts, and inability to separate what one believes from what the evidence supports (Ep 42).

**The intelligence-rationality dissociation**: IQ tests measure a cluster of skills (processing speed, pattern recognition, working memory capacity in defined tasks) that do not automatically translate into the broader rational capacities needed for good judgment: intellectual humility, calibrated uncertainty, active consideration of alternatives, awareness of one's own cognitive biases. High IQ can coexist with severe dysrationalia. In fact, high intelligence can make dysrationalia *worse* by providing more computational power to rationalize pre-existing beliefs and defeat challengers (Ep 42).

**Why this matters for the meaning crisis**: If intelligence and rationality are dissociated, then the tools that most intellectual culture prizes — argument, evidence-gathering, philosophical reasoning — can all be captured by dysrationalic processing. One can be a sophisticated philosopher or scientist and simultaneously be running parasitic processing, self-deception, and rationalization at the base level. This means that proposing better arguments or more evidence is insufficient to address the meaning crisis: the underlying RR machinery must be recalibrated, not just the explicit belief system (Ep 42).

**Stanovich's taxonomy**: Stanovich distinguishes among: (1) algorithmic mind (processing efficiency, what IQ measures); (2) reflective mind (metacognitive, overrides default processing when appropriate — what rationality requires); (3) autonomous mind (fast, automatic processing). Dysrationalia occurs when the reflective mind has insufficient governance over the autonomous mind despite a high-functioning algorithmic mind. Developing the reflective mind is a matter of psychotechnological cultivation, not cognitive endowment (Ep 42).

**Connection to active open-mindedness**: The primary corrective for dysrationalia is [[Active Open-Mindedness]] — systematically counteracting myside bias and confirmation bias through practices of genuine engagement with disconfirming evidence. This is why Vervaeke treats Socratic dialectic as the psychotechnology of rational self-cultivation: it is specifically designed to break dysrationalic processing by making one's own reasoning into an object of scrutiny (Ep 42).
