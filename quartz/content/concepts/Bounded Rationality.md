# Bounded Rationality

> Herbert Simon's concept: human rationality is bounded by limited information, time, and computational capacity; therefore agents *satisfice* (find good-enough solutions) rather than maximize, and rational behavior must be evaluated relative to these ecological constraints.

## Vervaeke's framing
Simon's bounded rationality challenges the classical economic model of the perfectly rational agent who surveys all options and maximizes expected utility. In reality: information is incomplete, computational power is finite, and time pressure is ever-present. The rational response under these conditions is *satisficing* — searching until a good-enough option is found and stopping there — not maximizing, which would require exhaustive search (Ep 41).

Vervaeke invokes bounded rationality as convergent with Cherniak's finitary predicament: both identify the same structural feature of finite cognitive systems (they cannot be purely algorithmic or complete). The deeper positive characterization of what bounded rationality *is* — how good-enough is determined, how the search is directed — is relevance realization. Satisficing is not arbitrary stopping; it is stopping when the solution is relevant enough to the organism's goals and situation (Ep 41).

Simon's work (with Newell) on heuristics in problem-solving (General Problem Solver) points toward the same conclusion: effective real-world cognition relies on domain-general heuristics that are fallible but ecologically rational — appropriate to the constraints of the finitary predicament (Ep 41).
