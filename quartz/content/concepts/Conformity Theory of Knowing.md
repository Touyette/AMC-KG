# Conformity Theory of Knowing

> Aristotle's account of knowing as the mind taking on the same structural-functional organization as what is known — a contact or participatory epistemology rather than a descriptive one.

## Vervaeke's framing
On the conformity theory, to know something is not to stand apart from it and generate accurate descriptions; it is to actually share its form — its structural-functional organization. The analogy Aristotle uses is an architect who has in their mind the idos of the building: they don't have the physical building in their mind, but they share its organizational pattern in such a way that they could cause the building to come into being. To know is thus to be in contact with reality, to participate in it, not to point at it with propositions. This makes knowing a change in being, not just a change in beliefs. Vervaeke notes how much we still rely on this model in everyday life — checking our sobriety, optimal environmental conditions, and inter-subjective agreement before trusting a perception. The conformity theory lends plausibility to Aristotle's geocentric cosmos (which embodies meaningful natural motion) and makes knowing inherently connected to the shape of the world. Its eventual undermining by nominalism and the scientific revolution is a key step toward the meaning crisis (Ep 07).
