# Logos

> In Vervaeke's use (following Plato and Aristotle): the structural-functional organization of a thing — the real pattern that both makes something be what it is and makes it knowable.

## Vervaeke's framing
Vervaeke introduces logos as a corrective to the feature-list model of knowledge. A bird is not simply a collection of features (wings, beak, feathers, flight) — it is their structural-functional integration, the way they hang together so that the whole is greater than the sum of its parts. This is what the Germans call Gestalt. The logos (or idos — Plato's "form") is not a shape but a formula: the organizing pattern. Crucially, this pattern does two things simultaneously: it is what makes a thing be what it is, and it is the access-point by which we come to know it. When we truly know something, our mind conforms to its logos — this is what Aristotle will develop as the Conformity Theory of Knowing. Logos is the rational principle of intelligibility that structures reality and makes it available to understanding. In later episodes it figures in Stoic cosmology (logos as divine reason pervading the cosmos) and in Neoplatonic and Christian theology (the Word/Logos as the pattern of creation) (Ep 05).
