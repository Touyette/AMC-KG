# Existentialism

> The philosophical movement (Kierkegaard, Heidegger, Sartre, Camus, Merleau-Ponty) that takes lived existence — with its finitude, freedom, anxiety, and unchosen thrownness — as the primary subject of philosophy; Vervaeke treats existentialism as a genuine but incomplete response to the meaning crisis that diagnoses the problem without recovering the participatory knowing needed to address it.

## Vervaeke's framing
Existentialism arises as a counter-movement to idealist and rationalist philosophy: instead of beginning with abstract essences, universal reason, or speculative metaphysics, existentialists begin with the concrete situation of a finite, mortal, free being thrown into a world without pre-given purpose. Existence (the concrete, situated being-here) precedes essence (any fixed definition or purpose) — the human being is what it makes of itself (Ep 36, Ep 46).

**The key thinkers in AMC**:
- **[[Søren Kierkegaard]]**: the original proto-existentialist; the inwardness of truth; the paradox of faith; the stages of existence (aesthetic, ethical, religious); anxiety as the dizziness of freedom.
- **[[Martin Heidegger]]**: [[Dasein]] (being-there) as the mode of existence that is always already in-the-world; thrownness, fallenness, authenticity; Being-toward-death; care (Sorge) as the structure of Dasein; the forgetfulness of Being as the deep diagnosis of modernity.
- **Jean-Paul Sartre**: radical freedom as condemnation; bad faith (self-deception about one's own freedom); existence precedes essence as the humanist manifesto.
- **[[Albert Camus]]**: the absurd (the confrontation between human meaning-demand and the world's silence); the question of whether suicide is the honest response or whether revolt is possible without self-deception.

**What existentialism correctly sees**: Existentialism correctly diagnoses the situation of modern human beings after the collapse of the cosmological order. Without a pre-given teleological structure (the Christian narrative, the rational cosmos), the human being is thrown back on its own finitude and freedom. Anxiety, absurdity, and alienation are not symptoms of neurosis but accurate responses to the real situation. Existentialism is philosophically honest about the problem (Ep 36).

**Why it falls short**: Vervaeke's critique (often implicit) is that existentialism's strength — the honest confrontation with the groundlessness of modern existence — is also its limit. Existentialism offers authenticity (Heidegger), revolt (Camus), or radical self-creation (Sartre) as responses, but none of these recover the participatory, relational, and transpersonal knowing that existentialism itself shows we have lost. Existentialism is a philosophy of heroic coping, not transformation. It does not recover the world as a home but insists on living in the homelessness with dignity. What is missing is the anagoge — the movement of participation that reconnects self and world at a deeper level than existentialism accesses (Ep 36, Ep 46).

**Existentialism and Heidegger's later work**: Heidegger's own trajectory from early existentialist analysis (Being and Time) toward the later thinking of Being, Gelassenheit, and poiesis is, in Vervaeke's reading, a movement from existentialist diagnosis toward something resembling the participatory recovery — incomplete and distorted (by Heidegger's politics and his onto-theological residues), but pointing in the right direction (Ep 46).
