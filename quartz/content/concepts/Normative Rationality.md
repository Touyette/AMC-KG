# Normative Rationality

> Rationality as conformity to formal logical and statistical norms — Bayesian probability, deductive validity, expected utility maximization; the standard against which human reasoning is measured in cognitive bias research; Vervaeke contrasts this with ecological rationality and bounded rationality to show that "deviating from norms" is not the same as being irrational.

## Vervaeke's framing
Normative rationality is the view that rational thinking consists in conforming to explicit logical and statistical rules: valid deductive inference, Bayesian belief updating, probability calculus, expected utility theory. On this view, cognitive biases are deviations from normative standards — errors. The heuristics-and-biases program (Kahneman/Tversky) measures human reasoning against normative standards and finds it systematically defective (Ep 40).

**Why normative rationality is incomplete as a standard**: Vervaeke (following Simon, Gigerenzen, and others) argues that normative rationality provides the wrong benchmark for real cognition. Normative standards are defined for frictionless, fully-specified environments with complete information. Real agents face combinatorially explosive, open-ended environments with incomplete information and finite resources. A system that achieved normative rationality in such environments would never complete a computation; it would be paralyzed by the demand for completeness. Normative rationality is ecologically irrational as a cognitive standard (Ep 40, Ep 41).

**Normative vs. ecological rationality**: Ecological rationality (Gigerenzen) asks not whether a strategy conforms to logical norms but whether it works — whether it produces good outcomes in the environment where it actually operates. Fast-and-frugal heuristics often outperform normatively "correct" strategies in realistic environments because they use less information and avoid overfitting. This reframing is crucial for Vervaeke: the goal of rational cultivation is ecological fit, not formal norm-conformity (Ep 40, Ep 41).

**Normative rationality and the meaning crisis**: Part of the Enlightenment's contribution to the meaning crisis is the equation of rationality with normative-formal-explicit processing, and the resulting demotion of non-propositional knowing (perspectival, participatory, procedural) as "merely subjective" or "irrational." Restoring the full picture of rationality — as including embodied, enactive, relevance-realizing intelligence — is part of what Vervaeke's cognitive-scientific account accomplishes (Ep 40, Ep 41).
