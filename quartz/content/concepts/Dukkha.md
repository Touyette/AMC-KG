# Dukkha

> Buddhist: the pervasive threat to agency inherent in all existence — entrapment and loss of freedom, not mere pain; etymologically, a wheel off its axle, destroying itself as it turns.

## Vervaeke's framing
Dukkha is systematically mis-translated as "suffering" in the sense of pain. The etymology corrects this: a wheel whose axle is not at center, so it destroys itself in motion; a joint out of socket. The experience is not primarily pain but *loss of agency* — being fettered, trapped, losing freedom. The Buddha's own metaphors for enlightenment are freedom metaphors ("the taste of salt"), not relief-from-pain metaphors. And his parables (the monkey grabbing pitch until entirely trapped) are entrapment parables, not pain parables (Ep 08, Ep 13).

Ep 13 gives the cognitive-science account: the First Ennobling Provocation is to *realize that all of life is threatened by Dukkha* — not that all life is painful (a meaningless claim), but that the very machinery that makes the agent adaptively intelligent (heuristics, self-organization, salience, pattern-detection) is simultaneously the machinery by which the agent can become trapped in **Parasitic Processing** (internal spiral) and **Reciprocal Narrowing** (external agent-arena contraction). This threat is endemic — it cannot be escaped by denial or self-denial, because it lives inside the adaptive machinery itself. The appropriate response is the Eightfold Path as counteractive dynamical system (Ep 13).
