# Golden Mean

> Aristotle's method for cultivating virtues: each virtue is the dynamically calibrated appropriate response between deficiency (too little) and excess (too much) — not a fixed midpoint but an ongoing optimization.

## Vervaeke's framing
The Golden Mean is Aristotle's practical method for setting up the conditions that cultivate character and virtues. Courage, for example, is not cowardice (deficiency) and not recklessness (excess) — it is the dynamically calibrated response that is appropriate to the situation. Crucially, Vervaeke stresses that the mean is "golden," not arithmetic: it is not simply the average between extremes but the right response for the particular context, which requires judgment (phronesis). This is the same optimization logic as the Middle Path and Optimal Grip: not maximizing or minimizing but finding the right tuning for what the situation calls for. The method of the Golden Mean is a way of training attention to notice when one lacks sufficient enabling constraints (not courageous enough) and when one has too many options that all seem like courage (reckless rather than truly courageous), and then adjusting accordingly over time through practice (Ep 06).
