# Ground of Being

> Tillich's non-onto-theological name for God: the simultaneous ground of meaning, reality, and the relationship between them — not any kind of being, but the inexhaustible depth from which being, meaning, and their interrelation arise.

## Vervaeke's framing
Tillich represents God as the simultaneous grounding of meaning, of reality, and of their relationship to each other. This is a deliberate departure from onto-theology: God is not located *within* this triad as a special instance of it (e.g., as the most meaningful being, or as reality itself, or as the principle of their correspondence). Any attempt to limit God to just one of these three — just meaning, just reality, or just their relationship — is, for Tillich, a profound form of idolatry (Ep 50).

**Connection to Heidegger**: The ground of being inherits from Heidegger's notion of Being itself — not any particular being but the condition of possibility for beings to be and to show themselves. Tillich theologizes this: the ground of being is what Heidegger's analysis of Being points toward but resists naming theologically. The forgetfulness of Being (Heidegger) and the idolatrous objectification of God (Tillich) are the same error seen from philosophy and theology respectively (Ep 50).

**No-thingness**: God is no kind of thing. The ground of being is a *no-thingness* — not nothingness in the sense of absence or void, but the inexhaustible fount of meaning cultivation that cannot be framed as an entity. As an inexhaustible source, it parallels what Heidegger calls the moreness of the thing beyond itself and what the early Romantics call the infinity of reality. When Tillich says the no-thingness of God takes into itself and overcomes the nothingness of despair, he means that an authentic encounter with this inexhaustibleness reveals that the ground cannot be drained by existential meaninglessness (Ep 50).

**God beyond the God of theism**: This phrase is Tillich's most transgressive formulation. Classical theism presents God as a supreme being — the highest entity. Tillich argues this is onto-theology and therefore idolatry. The ground of being is beyond that God: beyond personal/impersonal, beyond theism/atheism, beyond the Cartesian grammar that makes those dichotomies feel inevitable. This is the non-theism of Tillich, continuous with the non-theism appropriate to Buddhism and Taoism (Ep 50).

**Epektasis**: Because the ground of being is inexhaustible, the human relationship to it is necessarily one of infinite self-transcendence (epektasis). There is no resting in the ground of being as a final terminus — only the ongoing disclosure of its inexhaustibleness, in which transjectivity and transframing never stop (Ep 50).
