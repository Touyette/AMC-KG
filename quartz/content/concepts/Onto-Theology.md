# Onto-Theology

> Heidegger: the philosophical error of understanding Being (the ground of reality and disclosure) in terms of a supreme being (God as the highest, most powerful entity) — a having-mode misunderstanding of Being that simultaneously grounds classical theism and produces nihilism.

## Vervaeke's framing
Onto-theology is the ultimate expression of the forgetfulness of Being in the philosophical-theological tradition. The structure of the error: when we forget attunement (the being mode's openness to Being), we slip into the having mode and begin to treat beings as objects to be grasped. But something registers as missing — Being itself (the ground of realness, disclosure, inexhaustibleness) escapes the propositional net. The response, rather than recovering the being mode, is to posit Being as a limit-case being: the supreme being, God, the highest entity that somehow grounds all other entities (Ep 47).

This is a modal confusion: it tries to resolve the question of Being within the having mode, by conceptually defining and possessing a supreme object. Classical theism's traditional presentation of God as "the highest being, the ultimate cause, the supreme person" is, for Heidegger, the philosophical crystallization of this mistake. (Note: Heidegger is not denying the possibility of genuine religious experience of the divine; he is critiquing a specific philosophical framework for speaking about God — which Tillich and others will take up and redirect) (Ep 47).

**Onto-theology → nihilism**: This is the connection Heidegger makes via Nietzsche. If Being (the ground of realness) has been misidentified as a supreme being, then when that supreme being is undermined — scientifically, philosophically, culturally — it appears as if Being itself has vanished. The "death of God" (Nietzsche) produces nihilism because the onto-theological framework had conflated Being with the supreme being. Remove the supreme being and Being goes with it. But this only happens because Being was never properly understood in the first place (Ep 47).

**Tillich's development (Ep 47 anticipation, fully developed Ep 50)**: Tillich takes Heidegger's critique of onto-theology and develops it theologically: the critique of onto-theology = the critique of *idolatry*. Idolatry is treating anything finite — including the theological concept of God as supreme being — as ultimate. Any reification of God is idolatry because it captures the ground of being in a having-mode frame. Tillich responds with the "God beyond the God of theism" and the "ground of being" — neither a supreme being nor an absence of being, but the inexhaustible depth that simultaneously grounds meaning, reality, and their relationship. This is accessible not through belief or propositional theology but through faith-as-ultimate-concern and the anagogic method of correlation (Ep 47, Ep 50).

**Idolatry and the meaning crisis**: For Tillich, literalism and fundamentalism are the most pernicious forms of idolatry in contemporary religion: they attempt to *have* the symbol rather than *become* through it; they freeze the imaginal into the imaginary. This is why the meaning crisis and the failure of religious structures are, at root, a problem of idolatry — the modal confusion (having mode) applied to the most fundamental level of symbolic life (Ep 50).

**Vervaeke's framing**: Onto-theology is a specific form of modal confusion where we try to capture the mystery of Being (which requires the being mode, aletheia, and openness to the thing beyond itself) within a having-mode conceptual scheme. The result is the destruction of the very mystery one was trying to honor (Ep 47).
