# General Intelligence

> Spearman's empirically robust finding that all cognitive task performances inter-predict each other (the Strong Positive Manifold), pointing to a single underlying capacity — which Vervaeke argues is best understood as Relevance Realization.

## Vervaeke's framing
Spearman discovered in the 1920s that how well someone performs on any one cognitive task (math, English, even sports) is strongly predictive of how they perform on any other cognitive task. This cross-domain inter-predictability is the **Strong Positive Manifold** — and it has replicated consistently for a century. It points to a single underlying capacity: general intelligence (g), the factor that makes someone a general problem solver across diverse domains (Ep 31).

Vervaeke (with Leo Ferraro) argues that when you look at what psychometric tests probe for g — problem formulation, similarity judgment, pattern finding, adapting to ill-defined dynamic situations, dealing with complex environments — these are exactly the tasks where relevance realization is required. You cannot do these things without continuously and adaptively zeroing in on what is relevant in an open-ended environment. Therefore RR is the best scientific candidate for the underlying capacity that is g (Ep 31).

This convergence is significant for the series: it means the same virtual-engine machinery (efficiency-resiliency opponent processing, compression/particularization, temporal exploitation/exploration) that explains cognition at the bioeconomic level also explains what psychometrics has been tracking all along. It is also a check on hubris: g is one of the most robust findings in psychology, and RR's identification with g is evidence for RR's reality and importance. Vervaeke separates general intelligence from rationality and wisdom — having high g does not guarantee good rationality or wisdom; all three are distinct but related (see later episodes).
