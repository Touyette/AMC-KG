# Autopoiesis

> Varela/Thompson's term for self-making systems that not only self-organize but actively seek out and maintain the conditions for their own continued self-organization; contrasted with mere self-organization (e.g., a tornado).

## Vervaeke's framing
Vervaeke introduces autopoiesis while distinguishing the human case from mere self-organization. A tornado is self-organizing: it creates a structure through the interplay of constraints and events. But it is not autopoietic — it does not seek out conditions that will protect and promote its own self-organization; in fact it will move into conditions that destroy it. A living organism, by contrast, is self-making: it has a structural-functional organization that actively seeks out the conditions necessary for its continuation. Applied to persons, this means you are not just self-organizing in response to your environment but actively shaping your environment and yourself in ways that realize your developmental potential. This is the Aristotelian insight that Varela and Thompson capture in contemporary terms: the purpose of a living thing is to enhance its structural-functional organization — its self-making. For humans, this means the cultivation of character and virtue that realizes rational, reflective capacities (Ep 06).
