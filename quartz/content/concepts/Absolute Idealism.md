# Absolute Idealism

> Hegel's position that reality just is the self-developing system of rational patterns of intelligibility — "the real is the rational, and the rational is the real" — with no unknowable Thing in Itself beyond.

## Vervaeke's framing
Absolute Idealism is Hegel's response to Kant's concession that the Thing in Itself — reality as it is in itself, apart from our framing — is unknowable. Hegel argues that if something is completely unknowable, it is indistinguishable from non-existent, and should be dropped. What remains is just the self-organizing, historically developing system of patterns of intelligibility: Geist. These patterns are not merely "in our heads" — they structure reality itself, since without the distinction between Geist and the Thing in Itself, mind and reality become identical (Ep 24). Vervaeke reads this as a deeply plausible but ultimately too neat move: Absolute Idealism's grand synthesis provides the cultural grammar for totalitarian ideologies that claim their system of ideas is identical to the course of history. Kierkegaard's critique is that this leaves no room for personal transformation or the leap of faith that wisdom requires.
