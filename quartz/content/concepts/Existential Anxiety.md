# Existential Anxiety

> Tillich's distinction: anxiety is a nebulous, non-specific threat where one does not know what to do — contrasted with fear (determinate, observable threat with a known response); the Hellenistic and modern meaning crises are fundamentally anxiety crises.

## Vervaeke's framing
Fear and anxiety are commonly conflated, but they differ in a functionally important way: *fear* has an identifiable object and a known range of responses (a tiger enters the room — you know what to do, even if you fail to do it). *Anxiety* has no identifiable object; the threat is nebulous, diffuse, and the appropriate response unclear. This is why Kierkegaard and Heidegger prefer "anxiety" when discussing existential conditions — they are precisely conditions in which the agent faces a threat that cannot be localized or resolved by any determinate action. Vervaeke uses this distinction (drawing on Tillich's *Courage to Be*) to reframe the Epicurean diagnosis: what the Hellenistic person suffers from is not fear of death per se but existential anxiety — a pervasive sense that the ground of one's being is threatened without knowing how to respond. The same applies to the modern meaning crisis: it is not a specific fear (of disease, poverty, etc.) but anxiety about whether anything matters at all (Ep 14, extended throughout series).
