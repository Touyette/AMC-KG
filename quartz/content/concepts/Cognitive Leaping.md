# Cognitive Leaping

> Baker-Sennett & Ceci: the capacity to leap from minimal cues to an accurate Gestalt of a pattern's final form — measured by how few cues a person needs to predict the whole correctly; directly predictive of insight problem-solving.

## Vervaeke's framing
Baker-Sennett and Ceci (1996) studied what they called "inductive leaping" — Vervaeke rejects this label because what they are describing is non-inferential, not inductive. Cognitive leaping is the more neutral term. The experimental paradigm: present partially-revealed patterns (e.g., dot patterns) across time and at various points ask participants to predict the final Gestalt. A good cognitive leaper uses *fewer cues* and *more accurately* identifies the final pattern — moving rapidly from features to Gestalt, from looking *at* the dots to looking *through* them. This requires an opacity-transparency shift: the local details become transparent to the global structure (Ep 42).

Cognitive leaping is directly predictive of insight problem-solving. The reason: insight *is* a kind of leap — from a locally-constrained framing that cannot solve the problem to a new Gestalt that renders it tractable. People who are good at cognitive leaping are good at the kind of rapid restructuring that constitutes insight (Ep 42).

This creates a tension with the AOM account of rationality. S1 processing produces "leaping to conclusions" — which AOM is designed to moderate. But if we try to suppress *all* S1 leaping, we also suppress the cognitive leaping machinery that underlies insight. The challenge for a full theory of rationality is not to eliminate S1 leaping but to distinguish its destructive form (premature conclusions based on heuristic bias) from its productive form (rapid Gestalt completion that enables insight). Leaping and coping are deeply interdependent — to be fast and cope effectively, you must leap; and leaping is also what makes insight possible (Ep 42).
