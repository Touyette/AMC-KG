# Second-Order Thinking

> The capacity to think about one's own thinking — to reflect, correct, and transcend one's first-order cognition — produced by internalizing alphabetic literacy into metacognition.

## Vervaeke's framing

All humans have metacognition (awareness of one's own mental states), but Vervaeke, following Robert Bellah, argues that second-order thinking is a qualitatively different enhancement: it arises when a psycho-technology is internalized into metacognition, enabling one to *systematically* examine, correct, and extend one's own reasoning. Alphabetic literacy provided this: once you can write your thoughts down, compare them, notice contradictions, and revise them, you have a feedback loop for correcting your own mind. Combined with the abstract symbolic reasoning trained by coinage, this produces a new epistemic situation: people simultaneously discover (1) how much they can self-transcend — cognition is not fixed, it can be improved; and (2) how much they are self-deceived — errors that were previously invisible are now detectable. This double discovery is the cognitive foundation of the Axial Age: Socrates' mission to expose self-deception through elenchus, the Buddha's diagnosis of suffering as rooted in cognitive distortion, and the Hebrew prophets' demand for moral self-examination are all expressions of it (Ep 02, 04).
