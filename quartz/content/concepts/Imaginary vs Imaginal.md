# Imaginary vs Imaginal

> Corbin's foundational distinction: the imaginary is purely subjective inner imagery (controllable, private, known to be "not real"); the imaginal is transjective — mediating between the abstract intelligible and concrete sensible worlds, between the subjective and objective — and is always in transformative transframing.

## Vervaeke's framing
Corbin invokes imagination not in its ordinary sense but introduces a technical distinction that is foundational to everything he says about gnosis, the angel, and the divine double (Ep 48).

**The imaginary**: What we normally mean by imagination. Purely subjective mental imagery that we know is not real, that is entirely under our control, and that we can play with at will. This is NOT what Corbin is interested in.

**The imaginal** (*mundus imaginalis*, the imaginal world): Something fundamentally different from the imaginary. Its key features:

1. **Transjective**: Neither purely subjective nor purely objective. It operates in the transjective relation between agent and arena, self and world, knower and known.

2. **Mediates between intelligible and sensible**: There is the abstract intelligible world (pure mind, formal/mathematical structure) and the concrete sensible world (matter, sensory experience). The Cartesian grammar severs these, leaving only inner mental representations vs. outer physical facts. The imaginal bridges them — it is the meaningfully structured interface through which intelligible order and sensory experience interpenetrate.

3. **Mediates between subjective and objective**: The Cartesian grammar also severs subjective and objective. The imaginal is what allows them to co-constitute experience in a non-Cartesian, participatory way.

4. **Always transformatively transframing**: The imaginal is not a static bridge; it is always in motion, always mediating through a trajectory of transformation. This is why it enables gnosis (participatory, identity-transforming knowing) rather than just representation.

**What the loss of the imaginal means**: When the Cartesian grammar eliminates the imaginal (by forcing everything into either mind or matter, subjective or objective), gnosis becomes impossible. Fundamentalism and literalism (freezing symbols into one pole) destroy the imaginal by refusing the transjective, transformative movement (Ep 48).

**Symbol in the imaginal mode**: A symbol used imaginally (not imaginarily) is: (a) translucent — you look *at* and *through* it simultaneously; (b) transjective; (c) trajective — it puts you on a trajectory of transformation; (d) transformative — it changes your identity, not just your beliefs; (e) transtemporal/transspatial — the movement it enacts is ontological rather than narrative. Such a symbol enables gnosis by making the non-logical identity between current self and aspirational self workable (Ep 48).

**Connection to Heidegger**: The imaginal is Corbin's explicit development of what was implicit but underdeveloped in Heidegger: the medium through which aletheia operates and through which the being-mode engagement with being-as-simultaneously-shining-and-withdrawing becomes practically navigable (Ep 48).
