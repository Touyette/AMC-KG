# Intentionality

> Husserl's foundational phenomenological term: any mental directedness — consciousness is always *of* or *toward* something; the double structure of noesis (the intending act) and noema (the object-as-intended).

## Vervaeke's framing
Husserl's key observation is that all consciousness is intentional — i.e., directed. This is not the everyday sense of "doing something on purpose"; it is a broader technical claim: every mental act — perceiving, imagining, remembering, desiring, thinking — is always *of* something. There is no bare awareness floating in a void; awareness is always awareness *of* (Ep 46).

Husserl analyzes the intentional structure into two poles:
- **Noesis**: the act of intending — the mental side of the relation (the act of perceiving, judging, desiring)
- **Noema**: the object-as-intended — not the physical thing itself, but the thing *as it shows up* in the act; the object's mode of givenness to consciousness

The debate about what the noema ultimately is (purely mental content, or the real thing itself as disclosed?) generates Heidegger's critique and later speculative realism's response (Ep 46).

In Vervaeke's terminology: intentionality maps onto the perspectival knowing pole of the transjective relationship. Phenomenology investigates this perspectival-knowing-transjective-relationship structure through a disciplined, reflective, experimental, probative attention — distinct from ordinary introspection. The point is to discover how world is disclosed through consciousness and how consciousness is always world-engaged, not self-enclosed (Ep 46).

**Why Husserl introduced it**: As part of the contact epistemology project — to show that consciousness is not a sealed subjective sphere (the Cartesian picture) but is always already reaching out to the world through its intentional structure. The hope was that mapping intentionality rigorously would give philosophy back its contact with reality. Heidegger's critique: intentionality, even properly mapped, floats without a ground in participatory knowing — it still doesn't tell you what mode of being underlies the intentional relationship (Ep 46).
