# Intensive Self

> Kranz's term for the emergent late-medieval model of selfhood in which the self is conceived primarily as inner coherent language — beliefs and propositions inside the head — in contrast to the extensive self that understood itself through trans-projective conformity with the world.

## Vervaeke's framing
Vervaeke draws on Kranz to identify a pivotal historical shift in the implicit model of the self. The earlier model — which Kranz calls the **extensive self** — understood the self primarily in terms of its connections to reality. The self was what it conformed to, participated in, and was structured by in the world. Knowing was conformity: the mind literally taking on the form of what is known. The self extended outward trans-projectively into the world through its agent-arena relationship (Ep 19).

The shift produces the **intensive self**: the self is now primarily what is inside my head. What I essentially am is my inner language — my beliefs, my propositions, and the way they cohere with each other. Knowing is no longer about conforming to the external world but about achieving consistency and logical coherence within an inner propositional space. This shift is driven by the change in reading practices after the Great Schism — from oral/communal Lectio Divina (which cultivated perspectival and participatory knowing through transformation) to silent propositional reading aimed at inner logical coherence — and by the rise of Averroes's purely Aristotelian emphasis on definitions and syllogisms. The intensive self is the forerunner of Descartes' cogito ("I think therefore I am") and the modern subjective turn in which the primary locus of certainty and identity is inner mental life rather than worldly participation (Ep 19).
