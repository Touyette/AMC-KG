# Salience Landscape

> Vervaeke's four-layer model of how consciousness structures experience by foregrounding what matters: featurization (selecting), foregrounding (prioritizing), figuration/framing (configuring), and significance (the fully cultivated landscape that affords wisdom).

## Vervaeke's framing
The salience landscape is the structured field of what stands out to consciousness at any given moment — not a flat array but a textured, dynamically shifting landscape in which some things are figure and others ground, some are causally transparent and others are depth-laden. Vervaeke distinguishes four nested layers. The salience landscape proper: consciousness performs featurization (selecting out relevant features from the vast array), foregrounding (prioritizing some over others), and figuration/framing (configuring features into gestalts and framing problems around them). The presence landscape: through optimal gripping and sizing up, affordances are opened between the agent and salient features, co-identifying the agent and arena. The depth landscape: through interaction, the brain tracks causal patterns rather than merely correlational ones, gaining deeper contact with reality. When salience consistently tracks presence and depth, you have a significance landscape — the fully trained, wisely calibrated landscape that protects against bullshit and self-deception. Higher states of consciousness are "higher" because they transform this entire landscape systematically (Ep 10).

In Ep 32, Vervaeke adds a second way of describing the salience landscape: three dimensions that give consciousness its phenomenological signature, abbreviated **ACT**. **Aspectuality**: everything is always experienced as an aspect — not the thing itself but a perspectival view of it. **Centrality**: experience is always indexed on and centred around the subject; "hereness" — what matters is always relative to me and my body. **Temporality**: timing is constitutive; things matter in terms of *when*, not just what; "nowness" — consciousness is always unfolding in dynamic time. Together these three dimensions explain the hereness, nowness, and togetherness of conscious experience. This ACT structure is enacted — it is produced by the dynamic embodied-embedded relevance-realizing process, not a static container. It connects the four-layer functional model (featurization → foregrounding → figuration → framing) to the phenomenology of conscious experience (Ep 32).
