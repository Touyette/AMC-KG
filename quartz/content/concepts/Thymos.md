# Thymos

> Plato's "spirited" part of the psyche — the social motivational center, operating by honor and shame, capable of intermediate-range goals and cultural-level cooperation.

## Vervaeke's framing
Thymos occupies the middle position in Plato's Tripartite Psyche, between the abstract long-range rationality of reason and the immediate pleasure-seeking of appetite. It is the part of us motivated by how we stand in relation to our peers — seeking honor and avoiding shame — and it operates within the socio-cultural domain of shared meanings rather than either abstract truths or superficial pleasures. Because lions are social animals, Plato uses the lion as the symbol for thymos. Thymos can be trained by reason (through Socratic dialogue and social practices) and then helps tame the appetite. It is not identical to what we now call "emotion" and not quite what we mean by "spirit" — Vervaeke leaves it in the Greek to avoid misleading connotations. Thymos explains why joining groups (Weight Watchers, study groups) improves performance: it harnesses social motivation in service of rational goals (Ep 05).
