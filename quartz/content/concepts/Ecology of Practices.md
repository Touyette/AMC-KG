# Ecology of Practices

> A set of mutually supporting and mutually constraining practices that together constitute the cognitive-transformative environment for a practitioner; the practices form a system, not a list — each shapes what the others can do, and the whole is more than the sum of its parts.

## Vervaeke's framing
A single practice in isolation — one session of meditation, one philosophical dialogue, one ritual — has limited transformative reach. The wisdom traditions did not provide single practices but ecologies: interconnected sets of practices (contemplation, intellectual inquiry, bodily discipline, communal ritual, ethical cultivation, service) that work together synergistically. Each practice opens the practitioner to what the others require; each guards against the pathologies of the others (Ep 31).

**Why ecology rather than list**: A list of practices is additive; an ecology is dynamical. In an ecology, practices can reinforce or undermine each other depending on sequencing, emphasis, and the practitioner's current state. A contemplative practice without intellectual grounding can produce dissociation; intellectual cultivation without contemplative grounding can produce rationalization. The skill of [[Meta-Psycho-Technology]] is precisely the management of this ecological interaction (Ep 31).

**Connection to the wisdom traditions**: Traditional wisdom schools — the Platonic Academy, Buddhist monasteries, Stoic communities, Neoplatonic circles — were institutionalized ecologies of practices. They embedded practices in community, curriculum, and graduated stages. The loss of these institutions (part of the collapse of the three orders) is partly the loss of the ecological scaffolding for practice. Part of what [[Wise Cultivation of Enlightenment]] aims to reconstruct is such an ecology, informed by cognitive science (Ep 31).

**Ecology and diversity**: A healthy ecology has diversity — different practices addressing different dimensions of the human being (somatic, attentional, relational, intellectual, aesthetic). Monocultures of practice (only meditation, only philosophy, only therapy) produce imbalanced practitioners who are over-developed in one dimension and under-developed in others. The meta-skill is calibrating diversity to the individual's needs and developmental stage (Ep 31).
