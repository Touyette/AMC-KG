# Alphabetic Literacy

> The psycho-technology of phonetic writing that made literacy broadly accessible and exapted the capacity for second-order thinking.

## Vervaeke's framing

Earlier writing systems (Egyptian hieroglyphics, Sumerian cuneiform) were ideographic and required years of specialist training — literacy was a full-time profession (the scribe). The alphabetic system, invented in Canaan and spread by the Phoenicians, reduced the cognitive load of literacy dramatically by encoding phonemes rather than ideas, making it learnable by far more people. But the key insight Vervaeke develops is *what literacy does to the mind*: when you can write your thoughts down and return to them later, you can reflect on your own thinking, correct it, share it, and store it independently of memory. This is the origin of second-order thinking — the internalization of literacy into metacognition. You do not just think; you can now think about your thinking. The two civilizations most shaped by early alphabetic literacy — the Greeks (via Phoenician transmission) and the Hebrews (whose alphabet developed directly from Canaanite) — are the two traditions that drive the Axial Revolution most powerfully (Ep 02).
