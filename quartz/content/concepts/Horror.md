# Horror

> Mary Douglas: the encounter with what is inter-categorical — things that violate core categorical distinctions — producing a loss of grip on reality and intelligibility, associated with madness rather than fear; the negative face of the numinous and the extreme of the continuum from wonder through awe.

## Vervaeke's framing
Vervaeke carefully distinguishes horror from startle-and-fear — which is what most horror movies actually produce (a monster jumping from shadows, preying on victims). True horror has nothing to do with being startled or fearing a predator. Horror is losing one's grip on reality and intelligibility — the experience associated with *madness*, not with danger (Ep 34).

Drawing on Mary Douglas's work on purity codes, Vervaeke grounds horror in the **inter-categorical**: things that violate the categorical distinctions by which we organize and make sense of the world. Douglas showed that "unclean" creatures in Leviticus are those that violate the expected correlation of shape, locomotion, and habitat (e.g., shellfish in the sea but not swimming in a fish-like way). More broadly: we maintain purity codes — systems of categorical organization — and anything that violates them registers as eerie, yucky, uncanny, or (at the extreme) horrifying. The violation spectrum: yucky (minor categorical breach) → uncanny/eerie (significant breach) → horror (violation of core categorical distinctions on which intelligibility depends). Monsters are prototypically inter-categorical: the werewolf (person/animal), the ghost (living/dead), the vampire (consuming life without generating it). Inter-categoricality in these core domains threatens our ability to maintain an optimal grip on reality itself (Ep 34).

Horror is the *negative* face of the numinous: the numinous involves both super-salient fascination (drawing one in, flow-like) and horror (the sense of losing grip, of something independent and overwhelming). Where awe takes us to the *precipice* of intelligibility — the edge of horror — horror is falling over that edge: the framing process cannot stabilize on any purchase, the trajectory of transframing accelerates without resolution, and what results is madness (Ep 34).

The functional role of horror within the sacred: by confronting us with the ultimate limits of our intelligibility, horror enforces humility — the deep recognition that we are finite meaning-making beings who can never fully encompass the mysteries we confront. This is why sacredness is not just worldview attunement (meta-assimilation) but also numinous disruption (meta-accommodation): the two together prevent both domicide (too little grounding) and inflation (too little humility).
