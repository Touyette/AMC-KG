# Nirvana

> The Buddhist goal: the cessation of tanha (craving) and thereby of dukkha (suffering); not annihilation but the extinguishing of the grasping self-structure; Vervaeke frames it as a radical reconfiguration of relevance realization rather than a metaphysical exit from existence.

## Vervaeke's framing
Nirvana is easily misunderstood as annihilation — the extinction of self and world. Vervaeke's framing, following careful readings of the Pali canon, is that nirvana is the cessation of a particular cognitive-affective structure: the grasping, craving mode of selfhood (tanha) that generates dukkha. What ceases is not experience or consciousness but the clinging structure that produces suffering (Ep 08).

**What is extinguished**: The Sanskrit root is "nir-vana" — blowing out, as of a flame. What is "blown out" is not the self as such but the fire of craving: the relentless, grasping attempt to possess, secure, and stabilize what is inherently impermanent. This is a specific cognitive-affective orientation — the having mode applied to existence itself — not a metaphysical entity. Its cessation leaves something, not nothing (Ep 08).

**Nirvana as cognitive recalibration**: In Vervaeke's interpretation, nirvana is best understood as a radical restructuring of the relevance realization machinery: the elimination of the craving-structure as the dominant organizing principle of the salience landscape. Without tanha as the attractor, the RR machinery becomes free to register reality as it is rather than through the distorting lens of what I want to permanently possess. This makes nirvana continuous with (rather than discontinuous from) insight, flow, and higher states of consciousness — the extreme end of the [[Continuity Hypothesis]] (Ep 08).

**Nirvana and enlightenment**: Nirvana is one Buddhist framing of what Vervaeke calls [[Enlightenment (Reverse-Engineered)]]: the stable reconfiguration of the RR machinery such that parasitic processing is extinguished and genuine contact with reality is sustained. Vervaeke finds convergent descriptions in Neoplatonism (return to the One), Daoism (wu-wei), and Christian mysticism (union with God), all pointing to something structurally similar — the transformation that is the end-point of the [[Continuity Hypothesis]] (Ep 08).
