# Communitas

> Victor Turner's term for the mode of collective being in which social roles dissolve and participants experience real communion, shared identity, and collective flow — the direct antidote to alienation (the perennial problem of lost connectedness with others); grounded in Durkheim's collective effervescence.

## Vervaeke's framing
Vervaeke draws on Victor Turner and Émile Durkheim (Ep 37) to articulate communitas as the practice-response to the perennial problem of **alienation** (lost self-other connectedness). Communitas is more than group proximity or social networking — it is a mode of collective being characterized by:

1. **Shared attention**: participants are genuinely co-attending to something — an event, a dialogue, a practice — not merely co-present
2. **Getting in sync**: becoming rhythmically and cognitively entrained with one another
3. **Collective flow**: the emergence of a shared dynamical system that takes on a life beyond individual participants; real communication rather than propositional exchange
4. **Communion**: participants feel they are genuinely participating in a shared identity — not performing social roles but touching something underneath them (this is the liminal dimension Turner developed from ritual studies)

Communitas is described as collective flow with the additional quality of real communication — of feeling that something real and deep is being exchanged. Contrast with the illusion of connection via social media (modal confusion: having many connections does not overcome alienation; it often deepens it by substituting a having-mode proxy for genuine being-mode contact).

Contemporary practices Vervaeke identifies as cultivating communitas: **circling practices** (a form of mindfulness applied collectively, creating a shared attentional field); the **authentic discourse movement** (Peter Limberg; anti-debate; authentic relating); **Jordan Hall's "coherence"** (marshaling distributed cognition through collective flow to both free participants from limiting cultural grammar and access non-propositional knowing together). These practices are attempts to create, without a traditional religious mythos, the conditions for communitas that wisdom traditions historically embedded in ritual and sacred practice (Ep 37).

Communitas is not therapy (though it overlaps with gnosis in therapeutic contexts). The key difference: in communitas, participants are not passive recipients but active co-creators of the collective dynamical system. It is a "participating in" rather than a "being done to." Like flow, it cannot be forced directly but must be set up by creating the right conditions and then entering it together (Ep 37).
