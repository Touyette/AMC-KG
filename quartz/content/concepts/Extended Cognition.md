# Extended Cognition

> The hypothesis (Clark and Chalmers, 1998) that cognitive processes can and regularly do extend beyond the boundary of brain and skull to include external tools, environments, and other agents; part of the 4E cognitive science framework.

## Vervaeke's framing
The extended mind / extended cognition thesis challenges the Cartesian assumption that mind is located inside the skull. Clark and Chalmers argued through the "parity principle": if an external process plays the same functional role that an internal cognitive process would play, it should count as part of cognition. The paradigm case is Otto, who uses a notebook as external memory — the notebook functions as his hippocampus (Ep 38).

**Connection to 4E cognition**: Extended cognition is one of the four Es of 4E cognitive science: **E**mbodied, **E**nactive, **E**xtended, **E**motional (or Ecological). Vervaeke uses the 4E framework to argue that cognition is not computation inside a box but a dynamical coupling between an organism, its body, and its environment — of which extension is one dimension (Ep 38).

**Extended cognition and relevance realization**: If cognition is extended, then RR is also extended — the relevance landscape is not set by the brain alone but co-constituted by the agent's extended cognitive system: the tools they use, the social practices they participate in, the cultural psychotechnologies they have internalized. Writing is not merely an output of cognition but reorganizes cognition; likewise, contemplative practices, philosophical dialogue, and ritual are cognitive extensions, not optional add-ons (Ep 38).

**Extended cognition and psychotechnologies**: Psychotechnologies (literacy, numeracy, dialectic, meditation) extend and reorganize cognitive capacities. The graph itself is an example: externalizing the web of relationships among ideas allows cognitive operations that would be impossible purely "in the head." This is why Vervaeke takes psychotechnology so seriously — they are not tools for already-formed thinkers to use but technologies that reshape what kind of thinker one becomes (Ep 38).

**Limits and debates**: Critics note that extension risks making "cognition" too diffuse. Vervaeke is less interested in the metaphysical boundary dispute and more in the pragmatic implication: designing environments, practices, and social structures that support rather than undermine good RR is a cognitive-scientific and ethical task, not merely an architectural or pedagogical one.
