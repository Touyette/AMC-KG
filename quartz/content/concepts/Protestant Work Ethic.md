# Protestant Work Ethic

> The drive to demonstrate elected status through worldly socioeconomic success, born from Lutheran anxiety about not knowing if one is saved.

## Vervaeke's framing
Because Luther's doctrine of salvation by faith alone removes any causal role for the individual in earning grace, there is no official way to know whether one is saved. The result is profound existential anxiety. The informal, semi-unconscious solution is to treat worldly success as an indirect sign of divine favour — if God has blessed your enterprises, perhaps that shows you are among the elect. This leads to intense labour and the reinvestment of profits back into business (to avoid the pride of conspicuous consumption), producing the cultural grammar of hard work and material success. Max Weber argued in *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism* that this alignment of the work ethic with emerging corporate capitalism was foundational to modernity (Ep 21). Vervaeke traces how this grammar persists even after secularisation: the anxiety of worthlessness and the hunger for unearned positive regard remain culturally active.
