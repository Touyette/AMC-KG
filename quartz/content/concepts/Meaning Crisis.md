# Meaning Crisis

> The civilizational condition in which the structures that previously made life feel meaningful — the three orders, the continuous cosmos, the participatory practices — have collapsed, leaving widespread disconnection, anxiety, and the proliferation of "bullshit."

## Vervaeke's framing

Vervaeke argues the meaning crisis is not a surface problem (social media, political dysfunction, economic precarity) but a deep historical-cognitive one rooted in the progressive fragmentation of the three orders — nomological, normative, and narrative — that had constituted a coherent worldview for most of Western history. The crisis expresses itself empirically as rising rates of anxiety, depression, despair, and suicide, and culturally as an increasing sense of disconnection from self, others, world, and any viable future (Ep 00).

The historical spine of AMC is the story of how this fragmentation happened: the Axial Revolution created the capacity for second-order thinking that made self-transcendence possible, but also made self-deception visible; the great religious and philosophical traditions were responses to this new condition; the scientific revolution, nominalism, and the Protestant Reformation progressively severed the nomological, normative, and narrative orders from each other; and the result is the contemporary West's inability to find a coherent framework that integrates scientific understanding with the human need for meaning (Eps 20–25).

The crisis is also self-reinforcing: because the meaning-making machinery itself has been damaged — people no longer have reliable access to the transformative practices (psycho-technologies) that would allow them to address parasitic processing, modal confusion, and existential entrapment — the conditions for recovering meaning are themselves hard to establish (Ep 36).
