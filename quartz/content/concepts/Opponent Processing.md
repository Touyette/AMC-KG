# Opponent Processing

> A dynamical architecture in which opposing processes — scaling down and scaling up in the attentional case — check and balance each other, producing a constantly self-adjusting optimum rather than a fixed midpoint.

## Vervaeke's framing
The image for opponent processing is the musician's principle: strings must be not too tight and not too loose, but the correct tension is never a static midpoint — it is the ongoing product of the strings' opposition. Applied to attentional scaling, mindfulness training teaches both scaling down (Vipassana: step back, break up gestalt, de-automatize) and scaling up (Metta, contemplation: reach for larger patterns, deeper contact) and then, through practices like Tai Chi, trains the capacity to flow dynamically between them. The reason for this integration is that each direction alone tends to overshoot: purely scaling down causes choking; purely scaling up causes fixation. Opponent processing keeps the system fluid. Vervaeke generalizes this architecture beyond attention to suggest that many of the most important self-organizing systems in cognition, biology, and the psyche operate through opponent processing — including the three-part soul (Plato), the virtual engine (Aristotle/Juarrero), and the non-dual state (Buddhism) (Ep 09).
