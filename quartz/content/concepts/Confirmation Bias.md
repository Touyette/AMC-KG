# Confirmation Bias

> The tendency to preferentially seek, interpret, and remember information that confirms pre-existing beliefs; the central cognitive obstacle to critical reflection and self-knowledge; one of Stanovich's key examples of dysrationalia independent of IQ.

## Vervaeke's framing
Confirmation bias is the tendency to search for and weight evidence that confirms rather than disconfirms what one already believes. It is not a mere statistical error but a systematic distortion of the relevance-assignment process: the very mechanism that decides what to look for and what counts as significant is biased by prior commitment. Vervaeke introduces it in the context of Socratic epistemology: the examined life requires active counter-pressure against confirmation bias, which is why Socrates' questioning is not just information-seeking but a psychotechnological intervention against it (Ep 04).

**Why IQ doesn't fix it**: One of Vervaeke's key points (following Stanovich) is that confirmation bias is largely independent of intelligence. Intelligent people are often *better* at generating confirming arguments — they use their reasoning capacity to rationalize rather than to genuinely examine. This is part of the argument for dysrationalia: the dissociation between intelligence and rationality means that higher IQ can actually produce more elaborate self-deception, not less (Ep 42).

**Myside bias**: Stanovich's term for the self-referential version: generating and evaluating arguments from one's own position as though that position were obviously correct. In active open-mindedness, the goal is to genuinely expose oneself to disconfirming evidence and to make the disconfirmation feel as urgent as the confirmation — but this requires overriding a deep automatic tendency (Ep 42).

**Connection to relevance realization and self-deception**: Confirmation bias is a form of relevance misalignment: one's RR machinery has been captured by a prior belief such that it makes that belief look more supported than it is. This is why Vervaeke calls it a form of self-deception — the machinery that was supposed to check the belief has been co-opted by the belief. The Socratic method, contemplative practices, and scientific peer review are all psychotechnological responses to confirmation bias operating at different scales (Ep 04, Ep 42).

**Connection to implicit learning**: Confirmation bias can operate at the level of implicit pattern detection — we don't just rationalize explicitly but automatically perceive situations in ways that confirm our expectations. This makes it harder to overcome because much of the biasing happens before conscious reflection (Ep 26).
