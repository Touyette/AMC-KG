# Dual Processing Theory

> The proposal that human cognition involves (at least) two distinct processing systems — fast/automatic/heuristic (System 1) and slow/deliberate/rule-governed (System 2) — that constitute separate competencies and can conflict with each other.

## Vervaeke's framing
Vervaeke invokes dual processing (following Stanovich) primarily to explain how systematic errors can exist at the level of competence while still being consistent with Cohen's correct point that we are the source of our standards. If we had only one competence, systematic errors would falsify the claim that we are rational at the competence level. But with two (or more) competencies that can generate conflicting outputs, we can be the source of all standards and yet produce systematic error when the two systems conflict in a particular direction (Ep 41).

System 1 is fast, automatic, associative, pattern-matching, heuristic — the system that does relevance realization below the threshold of explicit attention. System 2 is slow, rule-following, deliberate, effortful — the system that can explicitly apply logical or probabilistic standards. Neither system alone is sufficient for rational cognition. System 1 alone produces heuristic biases; System 2 alone runs into the finitary predicament. The two must work together — a relationship Vervaeke frames as opponent processing rather than strict dichotomy (analogous to Plato's reason and appetite: both real, both necessary, capable of conflict but best in dynamic balance) (Ep 41).

The positive manifold of reasoning errors (gr) demonstrates that when people fail at one reasoning task (lily pads, argument evaluation, belief perseverance), they tend to fail at the others — a systematic pattern that reflects a competence-level tendency for System 1 to override System 2 in ways the individual does not catch. This is what Stanovich calls *dysrationalia*: high intelligence (System 1 relevance realization = g) combined with poor rationality (failure of System 2 oversight) (Ep 41).

**Ep 42 — full S1/S2 characterization and AOM vs. Mindfulness**: S1 is intuitive, associational, implicit, fast — the mode operative in coping and caring. S2 is deliberate, inferential, explicit, slow — relies heavily on working memory; the mode of theorizing and planning. S2 has a corrective function for S1 (the cashier example: S1 flags "this seems wrong"; S2 systematically verifies). But S1 also does valuable work via cognitive leaping — arriving at an accurate Gestalt from minimal cues, directly predictive of insight. Suppressing all S1 leaping would undermine insight (Ep 42).

Two opponent cognitive styles: **active open-mindedness** (Baron) foregrounds S2 and protects it from S1 interference — appropriate for *theorizing* contexts where explicit inferential rigor is needed. **Mindfulness** foregrounds S1 and constrains S2 (inner speech, deliberate inference) — appropriate for *therapeutic/insight* contexts where transformative reconstrual and qualitative development are needed (Teasdale's metacognitive insight). Both share the core of training attention but apply it in opposite directions. Wisdom requires knowing which to foreground and coordinating them (Ep 42).
