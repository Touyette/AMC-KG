# Kant's Copernican Revolution

> Kant's self-described "Copernican revolution" in philosophy: just as Copernicus moved the earth (observer) around the sun rather than the sun around the earth, Kant moved the object of knowledge around the subject — arguing that the form of experience is constituted by the mind's categories, not derived from an independent reality; the knowing subject is no longer passive but active in structuring what can be experienced.

## Vervaeke's framing
Kant's Copernican revolution is the pivot on which much subsequent philosophy turns. Before Kant, the standard epistemological view was roughly: reality has a structure; the mind perceives that structure and represents it. Kant reversed this: the mind imposes structure on experience through its a priori categories (causality, substance, unity, etc.) and forms of intuition (space, time). We can never know the "thing in itself" (Ding an sich) — reality as it is independently of any observer — but only phenomena: things as constituted by the subject's cognitive apparatus (Ep 24).

**The Copernican analogy**: Copernicus moved the center of the solar system from the earth to the sun — rethinking which thing moves and which stays still. Kant analogously moved the center of epistemology from the object to the subject — rethinking which thing is structuring and which is structured. Objects of knowledge do not impose their structure on the passive mind; the active mind imposes its structure on the raw material of experience (Ep 24).

**Consequences for the meaning crisis**: Kant's revolution is part of the trajectory Vervaeke traces from Descartes to the meaning crisis. Descartes localized meaning and mind in the subject; Kant showed that the subject constitutively structures reality-as-experienced. This makes the subject even more central to the epistemic process — and simultaneously makes objective reality (the thing in itself) permanently inaccessible. The tension between the subject as constitution-source and the thing in itself as permanently withdrawn is what subsequent philosophers (Fichte, Schelling, Hegel, and later Heidegger) attempt to resolve, each leading to different positions on the meaning crisis (Ep 24).

**Hegel's response**: Hegel removes the thing in itself entirely: if we can never access mind-independent reality, there is no reason to posit it. Reality just is the self-developing system of intelligibility (Geist). This is the most important response to Kant's revolution in AMC — it produces both the Absolute Idealism that grounds Marxism and Nazism as pseudo-religious ideologies, and the dialectical insight that genuine development involves genuine self-transcendence (Ep 24).

**Speculative Realism's response**: Contemporary Speculative Realism (Meillassoux, Harman) calls Kant's revolution "correlationism" — the view that we can only access reality as it is correlated to a thinking subject — and attempts to break out of it. Vervaeke's transjectivity is a different response: neither pure subjectivism (the subject constitutes reality) nor pure objectivism (reality is mind-independent) but a real relational property of organism-environment coupling (Ep 47).
