# Middle Path

> The Buddhist path that transcends both self-indulgence and self-denial by rejecting the having-mode framing of both and pursuing an optimization of cognition that recovers the being mode.

## Vervaeke's framing
Siddhartha's discovery of the Middle Path is triggered by hearing a musician tell an apprentice that the strings of an instrument must be neither too tight nor too loose — an image for optimization rather than maximization. The Middle Path is not a compromise or an average between two extremes; it is a radical reformation that transcends the shared frame of both extremes. Both self-indulgence (the palace) and self-denial (extreme asceticism) remain within the having mode: both are attempts to control and have (or not have) the self. The Middle Path abandons this framing entirely and asks instead: what mode of cognition and being leads to genuine awakening? Vervaeke consistently returns to this image across the series as a model for cognitive optimization — the "not too tight, not too loose" principle applies to attentional scaling, to the relationship between the pure consciousness event and resonant at-onement, and to the balance between scaling up and scaling down in mindfulness (Ep 08, Ep 09).
