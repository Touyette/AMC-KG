# The Sacred

> The metaphysical question: what grounds the experience of sacredness? — the standard Western answer has been something supernatural; Vervaeke's project is to offer a non-supernatural alternative grounded in the transjective nature of Religio.

## Vervaeke's framing
Vervaeke distinguishes two questions (Ep 33): the **psycho-existential** question (what is it like to experience sacredness? — the territory of Sacredness) and the **metaphysical** question (what grounds that experience? what is it that is being encountered? — the territory of the Sacred). Historical Western religion typically answers the metaphysical question by positing something supernatural: God, the divine, a reality above and beyond the natural order (in the Thomistic sense established by the natural/supernatural distinction).

Vervaeke begins from the psycho-existential side and argues that the experience of sacredness is grounded in Religio — the transjective machinery of RR that is pre-egoic, pre-normative, and phenomenologically mysterious. His project is then to ask: can we offer a metaphysical proposal that grounds this experience without invoking the supernatural? This is the constructive project that will be developed through Eps 33–50, drawing on thinkers like Tillich, Corbin, Barfield, and Jung.

The distinction between the Sacred and Sacredness maps onto the classic theological divide between those who emphasize the metaphysical referent (what God is) and those who emphasize the experiential reality (what the experience of God discloses). Schleiermacher's move — away from metaphysical claims and toward the experience of "absolute dependence" — is the historical precedent for Vervaeke's approach (Ep 33).

**Ep 35 makes the central metaphysical move.** The standard account of The Sacred is the supernatural proposal: there exists something that is *above* nature (*super-naturalis*), and its ontological superiority means it is absolutely, essentially, always relevant — it has an essence of relevance. When one comes into relationship with it, one encounters what is inherently sacred. Vervaeke argues this commits a **category mistake**: it supposes there is an *essence to relevance* — some thing that has permanent, absolute, intrinsic relevance. But relevance realization has no essence; it is an ongoing process, not a product. Nothing is absolutely interesting to RR — not even RR itself (the salience machinery can be hijacked such that one no longer finds one's own existence salient). To claim there is something with an essence of relevance is to confuse *products* of RR (things relevance realization has latched onto in the past) with the *process* of RR (the ongoing self-organizing, self-correcting evolution). Fixating on products over process is a hallmark cognitive error (Ep 35).

**The alternative proposal**: The Sacred is the **transjective relationship between two inexhaustiblenesses**. First: the combinatorially explosive nature of Reality — reality always transcends any framing; it is never fully captureable in a representation; it is, in that sense, a *no-thing-ness* (not nothing, but not any particular frameable thing). Second: the inexhaustible no-thing-ness of the self and the RR process — the 'I' can never be fully captured in any frame; RR is constituted by its own ongoing evolution and never arrives at completion or final form. These two inexhaustiblenesses are deeply, primordially coupled at the level of Religio: the combinatorially explosive depth of Reality and the inexhaustible self-transcendence of RR resonate with each other at the foundational level of agent-arena co-identification. The Sacred is this transjective coupling; it is what powers the experience of Sacredness without requiring the supernatural category. This is, Vervaeke suggests, what the mystics were pointing toward when they spoke of the soul's union with God or the infinite — not a supernatural object, but this deep non-logical resonance between two inexhaustiblenesses (Ep 35).
