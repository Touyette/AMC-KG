# Ontonormativity

> The property of higher states of consciousness whereby they present reality as more real and simultaneously demand — normatively require — a transformation of one's everyday existence.

## Vervaeke's framing
Vervaeke coins this term to capture the puzzle that makes higher states of consciousness philosophically and existentially distinctive. Most altered states (dreaming, intoxication) are rejected as less real when one returns to ordinary consciousness. But higher states — mystical experiences, certain psychedelic experiences within a therapeutic frame — produce the opposite: one returns convinced that "that was more real and this is less real," and this conviction motivates and justifies radical life transformation. The "ontological" component is the enhanced sense of reality — the agent and arena both feel more real, more themselves; the "normative" component is the prescriptive demand this creates to change one's life to align with that more-real contact. Vervaeke notes the philosophical difficulty: these experiences are temporary, ineffable, and do not cohere with everyday experience (like dreams don't). Yet they are taken as more real, not less. Resolving this requires both a descriptive account (why do people experience them as more real?) and a prescriptive account (are they actually more real, and if so in what sense?) (Ep 10).
