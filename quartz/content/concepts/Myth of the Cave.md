# Myth of the Cave

> Plato's mythological account of anagogic ascent from the shadows of self-deception to the light of the Good — a participatory transformation in which self and world are mutually disclosed.

## Vervaeke's framing
The Cave is a myth in Vervaeke's technical sense: a symbolic story about perennial patterns, not a literal description. Prisoners chained in the cave see only shadows and echoes, which they take to be real; when one prisoner is freed, they first see the fire that casts shadows, then journey toward the opening, suffering recurring blindness as their eyes must adjust to progressively more real light. Eventually they glimpse the sun — the source of both intelligibility and life — and return to tell the others, who mock and threaten them (an allusion to Socrates). The structure is anagogic: each step forward discloses a realer pattern, which simultaneously transforms the knower. Vervaeke stresses that this is not a passive acquisition of new beliefs but a participatory transformation of the whole self. The Matrix (1999) is offered as a modern version of this myth, showing its perennial power. The Cave is also an account of what wisdom does: it reduces inner conflict, increases contact with reality, and feeds on itself progressively (Ep 05).
