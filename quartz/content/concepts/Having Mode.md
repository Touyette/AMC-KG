# Having Mode

> Fromm's term for the existential mode organized around acquiring, categorizing, and controlling — meeting needs by having things rather than by becoming something; characterized by I-It relations.

## Vervaeke's framing
In the having mode, one relates to the world categorically: things are instances of types, replaceable and controllable, valued for their utility in solving problems. This mode is coordinated with what Buber calls I-It relations — one treats everything as an object. There is nothing wrong with the having mode in itself: you need water, you need food, and the having mode enables you to acquire them efficiently. The problem arises when the having mode is the only mode operative — particularly when it is used to try to meet being needs (maturity, love, wisdom, meaning) that cannot be met by having something. Vervaeke links the having mode to market-economy dynamics: commercial culture profits by inducing modal confusion, getting people to pursue being needs (identity, love, maturity) through commodity acquisition. The palace in the Siddhartha myth represents a life locked entirely in the having mode (Ep 07).
