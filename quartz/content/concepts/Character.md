# Character

> The cultivated aspect of a person — the set of constraints one has internalized and identified with — that regulates self-development; the virtual engine of a person's growth.

## Vervaeke's framing
Aristotle distinguishes character from personality: personality is given by biology and unchosen environment, while character is what you can cultivate — consciously or unconsciously. To say someone is "acting out of character" is to make an existential or moral criticism, pointing to a departure from their cultivated dispositions. Vervaeke treats character as the virtual engine of a person's development: it is the systematic set of constraints you have internalized that determines which possibilities get actualized as you self-organize across time. The cultivation of character is thus the cultivation of virtues understood as enabling and selective constraints. Aristotle's diagnostic question — "How much time have you spent on your character today?" — is meant to be shocking, since most people spend enormous time on appearance and status but almost none on the constraints that regulate their development toward wisdom (Ep 06).
