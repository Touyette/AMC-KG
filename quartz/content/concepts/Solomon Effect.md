# Solomon Effect

> Grossman's finding that people reason more wisely about others' interpersonal problems than their own, because describing from a third-person perspective triggers de-centering and breaks egocentric lock-in.

## Vervaeke's framing
Igor Grossman asked participants to describe a messy, personally relevant interpersonal problem in the first person, then to re-describe the same problem from the third-person perspective. Egocentric first-person description keeps people stuck — mesmerized by the super-salience of their own standpoint. The third-person re-description triggers de-centering: participants break frame, identify the systematic structure of their error, and often achieve a resolution. Vervaeke reads this as experimental evidence that the de-centering central to higher states of consciousness is directly wisdom-productive — it interrupts not just a local error but the systematic bias of egocentrism. The name invokes Solomon: when we advise others, we are naturally in a de-centered position and reason more wisely. The practical implication is that belonging to a wisdom community provides the outside de-centering perspective that is dangerous to pursue alone (Ep 12).

**Ep 43 — Baltes & Staudinger experimental grounding**: Baltes & Staudinger found that discussing a dilemma with a significant other, or merely *simulating* such a dialogue internally (imagining talking to someone), both significantly outperform having additional thinking time alone. Vervaeke connects this to the Solomon effect: first-person framing keeps one locked inside one's own perspective; dialogue (real or simulated) breaks the lock by providing a structurally third-person vantage on one's situation. The practical implication: the Stoic practice of internalizing the sage (simulating a wise interlocutor) and the Socratic practice of dialogue are not culturally arbitrary — they exploit a real cognitive mechanism for wisdom-enhancement (Ep 43).
