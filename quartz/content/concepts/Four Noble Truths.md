# Four Noble Truths

> The foundational framework of Buddhist teaching: (1) life is suffering (dukkha); (2) suffering has a cause (craving/tanha); (3) there is a cessation of suffering (nirvana); (4) there is a path to that cessation (the Eightfold Path). Vervaeke reframes these as "four ennobling provocations" — not a pessimistic doctrine but an invitation to transformative inquiry.

## Vervaeke's framing
The standard translation "four noble truths" carries an unfortunate connotation of propositional claims one must accept. Vervaeke's preferred framing is **four ennobling provocations**: the Buddha is not delivering a set of beliefs but issuing four invitations to a process of transformation. Each "truth" is a call to see something and let that seeing change how you live (Ep 08).

**The four provocations**:
1. **Dukkha** (suffering/unsatisfactoriness): Not "life is full of pain" but something subtler: an existential misalignment, a pervasive sense that things are not quite right even when nothing specific is wrong — the grinding friction of being a self that craves permanence in an impermanent world. This is not pessimism but diagnostic accuracy.
2. **Tanha** (craving): The cause of dukkha is craving — not desire in general but the grasping, clinging mode of wanting. Tanha is the having-mode applied to experience itself: trying to possess, preserve, and control what is inherently flowing and unstable. This misidentification of the object of desire (the flow of experience treated as a fixable possession) generates the suffering.
3. **Nirodha** (cessation): There is a possibility of ceasing — not of all desire or experience but of the craving mode. This is the ennobling claim: the condition you are in is not the only possible condition. The transformation is possible.
4. **Magga** (the path): The Eightfold Path — right view, right intention, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, right concentration — is the method. It is a psychotechnology, not a doctrine.

**Provocations, not propositions**: Vervaeke emphasizes that the Four Noble Truths are designed to be *worked*, not believed. Believing "life is suffering" is not what the Buddha intended; cultivating the kind of mindful attention that actually reveals the dukkha-structure of one's own craving is what is intended. This is why the translation "truths" is misleading — they are transformative diagnoses, not credal assertions (Ep 08).

**Connection to AMC's broader argument**: The Buddhist analysis of suffering as structurally caused by a cognitive-affective misalignment (craving impermanence in an impermanent world) parallels Vervaeke's account of the meaning crisis: a structural mismatch between the way the RR machinery is trained/organized and what it is being asked to track. Both diagnoses point to the same type of solution: not more information but transformation of the underlying machinery (Ep 08).
