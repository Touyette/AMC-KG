# Small World Networks

> Network topology that optimizes both efficiency (low mean path distance) and resiliency (robustness to damage), via high local clustering plus long-range connections; correlates with general intelligence and consciousness, and is what the brain generates through self-organizing criticality.

## Vervaeke's framing
Vervaeke introduces three network topologies (Ep 32) to show how the efficiency-resiliency trade-off is structurally implemented at the level of neural wiring. **Regular networks** have all short-distance connections — highly redundant, very resilient (damage never isolates a node), but very inefficient (high mean path distance, slow information transfer). **Random/chaotic networks** have a mix of short and long-distance connections — very efficient (short mean path distances, rapid information transfer) but poor resilience (damage easily isolates nodes). **Small world networks** — combining high local clustering with some long-distance connections — optimize both: more efficient than regular, more resilient than random. They are at the sweet spot.

The prediction: if the brain is doing RR by trading between efficiency and resiliency, it should tend to generate small world networks. And the more it does so, the more functional it should be. Converging evidence (Ep 32): Langer et al. (2012, 2013) found that SWN structure in the brain correlates with higher general intelligence, and that working memory becomes more SWN-organized under greater task demands; Hilger et al. (2016) linked efficient SWN hubs in the salience network specifically to g; Monti et al. (2013) found that loss of consciousness is correlated with breakdown from SWN to local regular networks, and recovery of consciousness with SWN reformation.

Insight (Schilling 2005): insight occurs when two domains of information that were each organized as regular networks suddenly form a long-distance connection, converting the joint structure into a small world network — a sudden optimization of RR. This explains why insight produces a flash of salience, a sense of expanded intelligence, and altered consciousness simultaneously (Ep 32).

SOC (firing) and SWN (wiring) mutually reinforce each other: the more the brain fires in SOC patterns, the more it wires as a SWN; the more it wires as a SWN, the more it fires in SOC patterns.
