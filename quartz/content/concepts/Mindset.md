# Mindset

> Dweck: the dispositional orientation toward one's own traits as fixed (static, innate) vs. malleable (developable through effort and skills); shapes error-attribution, challenge-seeking, self-deception, and cognitive performance.

## Vervaeke's framing
Dweck's research (Ep 42) shows that the *way one identifies with one's cognitive processes* — not merely the processes themselves — has profound effects on reasoning and self-deception. This is Vervaeke's key exhibit for the claim that rationality is an existential issue, not just an information-processing one.

**Fixed mindset**: intelligence (or ability) is a static trait, essentially fixed at birth or early development. Under this orientation, error becomes threatening: it permanently reveals that one has low intelligence, a defect that cannot be changed. Consequences: (a) avoid challenges (hard problems increase error risk); (b) attribute error to permanent trait-defects rather than improvable processes; (c) produce self-deception — in Dweck's experiments, 40% of fixed-mindset children lied to a student in Germany about their performance. (d) Performance declines after exposure to hard problems even when subsequent problems are equivalent to earlier ones.

**Malleable (growth) mindset**: intelligence is developable through effort and skill-cultivation. Under this orientation, error becomes informative rather than threatening: it signals that more effort or better skills are needed. Consequences: (a) seek challenge (hard problems are learning opportunities); (b) focus on the process rather than the product (Ep 42).

**Triggering mechanism**: How one is praised determines which mindset is activated. Trait praise ("you're so smart") → fixed mindset. Process praise ("you worked really hard; you used a great strategy") → malleable mindset. The mode is context-sensitive and trainable, not a fixed personality variable.

The connection to rationality: fixed mindset = product orientation (fixated on error as outcome) = the same failure Stanovich diagnoses as dysrationalia (poor S2 self-monitoring). Malleable mindset = process orientation (attending to how one is reasoning) = the attitudinal prerequisite for active open-mindedness. Rationality requires identifying with one's processes, not one's products (Ep 42).
