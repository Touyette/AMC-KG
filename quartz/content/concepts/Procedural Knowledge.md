# Procedural Knowledge

> The second of the four kinds of knowing: embodied skill and competence — knowing-how — which is not fully articulable in propositions and requires practice to acquire.

## Vervaeke's framing
Procedural knowledge is knowing-how: the skill of the chair-maker, the fluency of the proficient reader, the martial arts practitioner's capacity for right timing. It cannot be fully reduced to propositional knowledge — you cannot learn to ride a bike simply by reading instructions. This is why Aristotle's example of the person who can make a chair is held to understand the chair more deeply than someone who can merely describe it accurately. Procedural knowledge requires practice, feedback, and embodied engagement; it is what Aristotle is pointing to when he says you acquire virtues by performing virtuous acts. It is also the form of knowledge that psycho-technologies transmit through practice rather than through reading (Ep 05).
