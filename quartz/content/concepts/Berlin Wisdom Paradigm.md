# Berlin Wisdom Paradigm

> Baltes & Staudinger: wisdom as a "metaheuristic (pragmatic) to orchestrate mind and virtue toward excellence" — the first major empirical theory of wisdom, specifying five criteria for wisdom-competence and developing experimental methods for measuring it.

## Vervaeke's framing
Baltes & Staudinger define wisdom as a metaheuristic — a heuristic for managing one's heuristics — that coordinates mind and virtue toward excellence. Vervaeke notes that this theory is "dripping with the machinery of relevance realization": the terms "metaheuristic," "pragmatic," "orchestrating," "managing uncertainty" all presuppose RR even if they don't name it (Ep 43).

**Five criteria for wisdom**:
1. *Rich factual knowledge about fundamental pragmatics of life* — deep grasp of the facts, principles, and conditions of human existence (sophia-like, but not merely academic).
2. *Rich procedural knowledge about fundamental pragmatics of life* — knowing how to apply those principles in practice (phronesis-like; connects to McKee & Barber's "wisdom is about how, not what").
3. *Lifespan contextualism* — perspectival knowing: the capacity to zoom out to the big picture of a life across time and zoom in to the specific situation; self-regulatory and integrative.
4. *Relativism of values and priorities* — Vervaeke's critique: this conflates wisdom with liberal-democratic values; Socrates, the Buddha, Jesus were not moral relativists. Better replacement: **fallibilism** (never assert certainty) + **humility** (appropriate awareness of one's limits).
5. *Recognition and management of uncertainty* — the wise person operates effectively within the finitary predicament; doesn't demand algorithmic certainty; calibrates action to irreducible uncertainty.

**Empirical method**: Present participants with challenging real-life dilemmas; have trained independent judges score responses against the five criteria. This was one of the first attempts to generate quantitative measures of wisdom analogous to intelligence testing.

**Vervaeke's criticisms**:
- The paradigm treats the metaheuristic as a form of expertise — Vervaeke rejects this; wisdom must be domain-general, expertise is domain-specific (Ep 43).
- Missing perspectival and participatory knowing — the paradigm handles propositional and procedural but doesn't explicitly theorize how participatory knowing (identity-level transformation) is involved (Ep 43).

**Solomon effect finding**: Baltes & Staudinger found that discussing a dilemma with a significant other, or merely *simulating* such a dialogue internally, both significantly outperform additional thinking time. First-person framing locks one inside one's own perspective; third-person or dialogical perspective breaks the lock. This grounds the Solomon effect and vindicates the Socratic and Stoic tradition of internalizing wise interlocutors (Ep 43).
