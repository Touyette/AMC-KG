# Scientific Naturalism

> The worldview that the natural world as described by science exhausts what is real; only physical processes and their mathematical laws exist; meaning, value, purpose, and consciousness are either reducible to physical facts or are merely apparent, subjective additions to a fundamentally meaningless universe.

## Vervaeke's framing
Scientific naturalism is the dominant worldview of educated modern culture: the universe is the physical universe as described by physics, chemistry, and biology; there is nothing supernatural; meaning and value are human projections onto a value-neutral cosmos. While this view correctly respects scientific findings, Vervaeke argues that it generates a specific philosophical-existential problem: it cannot account for the very things that make human life worth living — meaning, purpose, consciousness, beauty, the felt quality of genuine contact with reality (Ep 01).

**The explanatory gap**: Scientific naturalism faces what philosophers call the "hard problem" of consciousness and the even harder problem of meaning. If the universe is only matter and energy arranged by mathematical laws, then: (1) why is there any subjective experience at all? (consciousness is not reducible to physical processes, despite multiple attempts); (2) where do meaning and value come from? The scientific worldview brackets these questions but cannot answer them (Ep 22).

**Scientific naturalism and disenchantment**: Max Weber described the scientific worldview's effect on the world as Entzauberung — disenchantment: the world as described by science is "dis-ench-anted," stripped of magic, soul, purpose, and inherent meaning. A disenchanted world is one in which the categories of human meaning (sacred/profane, beautiful/ugly, good/evil) have no purchase — they are merely how things appear to human minds, not features of reality. This is the condition that Vervaeke calls the modern meaning crisis (Ep 01).

**Vervaeke's response**: Vervaeke's project is not to abandon scientific naturalism but to complete it. His claim: scientific naturalism as currently practiced operates with an impoverished picture of nature — one that systematically excludes the phenomena that matter most to human beings. A completed scientific naturalism would need to account for consciousness, meaning, and relevance as real features of the natural world, not as illusions or projections. The 4E cognitive science tradition, the science of relevance realization, and the deep continuity hypothesis are all attempts to develop such a completed naturalism (Ep 38, Ep 50).

**The relationship to the Cartesian grammar**: Scientific naturalism is closely related to, but not identical with, the [[Cartesian Cultural Grammar]]. The Cartesian grammar provides the implicit epistemological framework; scientific naturalism is the ontological conclusion that follows from it when the grammar is fully applied: only what can be described mathematically is real, everything else is secondary quality or subjective projection (Ep 22).
