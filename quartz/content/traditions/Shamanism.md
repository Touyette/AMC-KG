# Shamanism

> The oldest attested psycho-technology tradition: a set of ritual practices for inducing altered states of consciousness to enhance cognition, healing, and communal meaning-making.

## Role in the argument

Vervaeke treats shamanism as the foundational psycho-technology tradition — the first systematic attempt by human cultures to deliberately induce and exploit the flow state. Shamanic practices (sleep deprivation, fasting, psychedelics, extended chanting and dancing, social isolation) are disruptive strategies that alter brain states in ways that enhance insight, implicit learning, and metaphorical cognition. This is why shamanism is universal across cultures: it exapts cognitive machinery that is universal, and the results — better pattern recognition, healing efficacy, group cohesion, metaphorical world-making — are universally adaptive. For Vervaeke the shaman is the prototype of the wisdom figure: someone who has trained themselves to reliably access states that enhance relevance realization, and who uses this to serve the community. The Axial Revolution does not abandon shamanism but transforms it: Socrates' dialectic, the Buddha's meditation, and the Hebrew prophets' moral critique are all, in a sense, refined and socially generalized versions of what the shaman was doing (Ep 02, 03).
