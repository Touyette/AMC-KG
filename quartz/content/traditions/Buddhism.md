# Buddhism

> The tradition originating with Siddhartha Gautama (5th c. BCE), centered on the diagnosis of dukkha (unsatisfactoriness/suffering), its cause in craving (tanha), and the path of practice (mindfulness, insight, ethical conduct) to its cessation (nirvana); Vervaeke treats it as one of the most sophisticated wisdom traditions for addressing the meaning crisis because it provides both a precise diagnosis and a rich ecology of practices.

## Role in the argument
Buddhism enters AMC most explicitly in Episodes 8–9 and recurs throughout. Vervaeke treats it as a paradigm case of a wisdom tradition that: (a) correctly diagnoses the fundamental cognitive-affective problem (tanha/craving as a RR pathology); (b) provides a rigorous path of practice for transforming that machinery; (c) has been remarkably successful cross-culturally; and (d) is amenable to cognitive-scientific interpretation without being reduced to it (Ep 08).

**The core diagnosis**: The [[Four Noble Truths]] frame Buddhism's diagnostic move: existence has a pervasive quality of unsatisfactoriness (dukkha) arising from a specific cognitive-affective structure (craving: the having-mode applied to experience). This maps cleanly onto Vervaeke's account of [[Parasitic Processing]]: a cognitive pattern that hijacks the RR machinery and generates suffering without genuine contact with reality (Ep 08).

**Mindfulness as psychotechnology**: The Buddhist practice of sati (mindfulness) is for Vervaeke a paradigm psychotechnology: it develops meta-awareness of one's own cognitive and emotional processing, interrupting automatic patterns, deautomatizing the salience landscape, and enabling a more accurate relevance realization. It is not primarily a relaxation technique but a cognitive training in second-order attention (Ep 08).

**Insight practices (vipassana)**: The insight practices aim at direct perception of the three marks of existence (impermanence, no-fixed-self, unsatisfactoriness), not as propositions to believe but as participatory-perspectival seeing that restructures how one relates to experience. This corresponds to Vervaeke's account of transformative experience: not learning new facts but changing the machinery of salience (Ep 08).

**Nirvana and the Continuity Hypothesis**: The Buddhist goal of nirvana — the cessation of tanha and the release from the cycle of craving — is located by Vervaeke at the extreme end of the Continuity Hypothesis: the most radical and stable reconfiguration of the RR machinery, analogous to what Neoplatonism calls return to the One and Christian mysticism calls union with God (Ep 08, Ep 11).

**Limitations in Vervaeke's reading**: Buddhism, for Vervaeke, shares with the other prophets a partial response to the meaning crisis. It provides a powerful diagnosis and ecology of practices but lacks the scientific vocabulary to communicate across cultural contexts or to integrate with the cognitive science that could help reconstruct its insights for a post-Enlightenment audience.
