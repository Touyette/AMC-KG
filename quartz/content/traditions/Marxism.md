# Marxism

> The philosophical, economic, and political tradition deriving from Karl Marx (and Friedrich Engels); centered on dialectical materialism, historical analysis of class struggle, and the critique of ideology; Vervaeke treats Marxism as one of the paradigm pseudo-religious ideologies: a distorted Hegelianism that provides salvific narrative and in-group/out-group structure without genuine wisdom-cultivation.

## Role in the argument
Marxism enters AMC primarily as an example of what Vervaeke calls [[Pseudo-Religious Ideology]]: a belief system that appropriates the structure of religious meaning-making (sacred narrative, eschatology, martyrdom, in-group solidarity, the promise of transformation) without the participatory knowing that makes religion genuinely wisdom-bearing (Ep 23, Ep 25).

**Marxism as distorted Hegelianism**: Marx famously "turned Hegel on his head" — taking Hegel's dialectic of Geist (Spirit) unfolding through history and replacing Spirit with the material forces of production. The triadic Hegelian dialectic becomes the narrative of class conflict: thesis (bourgeoisie), antithesis (proletariat), synthesis (communist society without classes). The teleological structure is preserved — history has a destination, an eschatological end point — but the quasi-spiritual content is replaced with economic categories (Ep 24, Ep 25).

**The pseudo-religious structure**: The features that gave Hegelian idealism its appeal — the sense that one is participating in something larger than oneself, the narrative of struggle and redemption, the certainty that history is on one's side, the moral urgency — are all preserved in Marxism. This is what makes it function as a pseudo-religion: it provides the psychological-existential goods (meaning, community, identity, purpose) that religion provides, through a secular narrative. But because it lacks genuine wisdom cultivation, it is susceptible to the pathologies of ideology: rigidity, persecution, the collapse of enemy into abstract evil (Ep 23, Ep 25).

**Marxism and alienation**: Marx's concept of alienation — workers alienated from their labor, product, species-being, and each other under capitalism — is a genuine diagnosis that connects to Vervaeke's account of alienation as a perennial problem. But Marx's solution (collective ownership of the means of production) addresses the economic structural level without addressing the deeper cognitive-existential RR failures that generate alienation (Ep 36).
