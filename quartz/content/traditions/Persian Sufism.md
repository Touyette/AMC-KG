# Persian Sufism

> The Islamic mystical tradition that flowered in Persian-speaking contexts (9th–15th centuries); emphasized the direct experiential encounter with God (fana — annihilation in God), the love-mysticism of figures like Rumi and Hafiz, the imaginal cosmology of Suhrawardi, and the gnostic-esoteric dimension of Islam that Corbin explored; for Vervaeke, a carrier of participatory-transformative knowing within the Islamic tradition.

## Role in the argument
Persian Sufism enters AMC primarily through Henry Corbin's scholarship on Islamic mysticism, especially the Ismaili and Suhrawardian traditions. Corbin's lifework was to show that within Islam there was a tradition — largely suppressed by the literalist-legalistic dominant strand — of participatory, transformative knowing: gnosis, the imaginal world (mundus imaginalis), and the divine double. Persian Sufism is the tradition that preserved and developed this dimension (Ep 48, Ep 49).

**Fana (annihilation) and baqa (subsistence)**: The Sufi path culminates in fana — the dissolution of the ego-self in the ocean of God — followed by baqa — the subsistence of the transformed self in God. This is the Islamic version of the anagogic ascent: not the Christian soul's return to God but the lover's annihilation in the Beloved and return as a transformed participant. For Vervaeke, this is the participatory-transformative structure: the identity-level change that characterizes genuine gnosis (Ep 48).

**Rumi and love mysticism**: Rumi (Jalal al-Din Rumi, 13th c.) is the paradigm figure of Persian love mysticism: his Masnavi and Divan-e Shams are explorations of the lover's journey toward the Beloved — using erotic metaphors to describe the soul's orientation toward God. Vervaeke treats this as participatory knowing in its most poetic form: eros directed at the ground of being (Ep 48).

**Suhrawardi's Illuminationism**: Yahya Suhrawardi (12th c.) developed an Islamic Neoplatonism (Ishraq — illuminationism) that integrated Zoroastrian light symbolism with Platonic forms and Islamic mysticism. Corbin's recovery of Suhrawardi was central to his account of the imaginal world: a real ontological realm (neither purely physical nor purely abstract) where spiritual realities are present in imaginal form (Ep 48).

**Corbin's Ismaili emphasis**: Among Persian Sufi traditions, Corbin paid particular attention to the Ismaili (Shia esoteric) tradition as the most fully developed system of participatory-gnostic knowing within Islam. The Ismaili system of batin (inner, esoteric) vs. zahir (outer, exoteric) interpretation, and its elaborate angelology and cosmology, provided Corbin with the material for his account of the imaginal and the divine double (Ep 48, Ep 49).
