# Rhineland Mysticism

> German mystical movement (13th–14th c.) led by Eckhart and others: responds to Aquinas's natural/supernatural split by replacing anagogic ascent with descent spirituality — God flowing into the self that has emptied itself through will-negation (Gelassenheit).

## Role in the argument
Vervaeke presents the Rhineland Mystics as the spiritual response within Christianity to the crisis created by Aquinas (Ep 20). Aquinas had made faith the act of the will asserting what reason cannot know; this opened up a question: if reason cannot reach God, what form does genuine spiritual transformation take? The Rhineland Mystics, drawing on Neoplatonic currents still alive within Christianity (including the legacy of Pseudo-Dionysius and the Gnostic underground of the Cathars), propose a radical answer.

The central move: spirituality is no longer an ascent through love-and-reason toward what is more real. Instead, love is how the will moves when it negates itself. The self normally presses outward — asserts itself, claims its projects and identity, imposes its will on the world. That self-assertion is the obstacle. To become a conduit for divine Agape (God's love), one must release one's self-assertion — Eckhart's **Gelassenheit** (releasement) — and make an interior space for God to descend. The complementary term is **Durchbruch** (breakthrough): the eruption of divine life through the emptied self.

The consequences for Vervaeke's narrative are multiple: (1) the connection between spirituality and wisdom (wisdom as cultivation of self-transcendence) is severed; (2) inner conflict and self-negation become valorized as essential to the spiritual life rather than biographical contingencies; (3) this feeds directly into the Protestant Reformation (Luther's inner struggle, individual conscience vs. external authority); (4) Heidegger recovers Gelassenheit as a philosophical category for a different mode of relationship to Being — openness rather than will-to-mastery (Ep 20, Ep 47).
