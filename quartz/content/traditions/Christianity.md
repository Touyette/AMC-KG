# Christianity

> The religious tradition centered on the life and teachings of Jesus of Nazareth, the theological interpretation of his death and resurrection, and the community of practice formed around them; for Vervaeke, Christianity is the dominant carrier of participatory-transformative knowing in Western culture — and also the tradition whose collapse most directly produces the meaning crisis.

## Role in the argument
Christianity is the tradition whose historical transformation and decline is the central plotline of AMC's account of the meaning crisis. Christianity was the integrating framework of Western civilization for over a millennium — providing the three orders (nomological: creation as God's rational order; normative: divine law and natural law as the basis of morality; narrative: the Christian salvation history from creation through redemption to eschatological fulfillment) that gave life meaning and structure. The collapse of this framework is the proximate cause of the modern meaning crisis (Ep 07, Ep 15, Ep 20).

**Christianity and the three orders**: As long as Christianity was culturally dominant, it provided: the nomological order (the world is God's creation, intelligibly structured by logos); the normative order (natural law, divine commandment, virtue tradition); and the narrative order (the story of creation, fall, redemption, and eschatological fulfillment that located individual lives in a larger meaningful trajectory). The scientific revolution, Protestant Reformation, and Enlightenment successively dismantled each of these (Ep 07, Ep 20, Ep 21).

**Agape and metanoia**: Christianity's positive contribution to the wisdom tradition is its articulation of agape (unconditional love as the form of genuine relation) and metanoia (radical transformation of the salience landscape). These are the Christian responses to the perennial problems: agape addresses alienation and relational failure; metanoia addresses parasitic processing and modal confusion. Vervaeke treats these as genuine psychotechnological innovations, not merely doctrines (Ep 15).

**The Neoplatonism synthesis**: The decisive intellectual event in early Christianity was the synthesis with Platonic philosophy (through figures like Augustine, Origen, Pseudo-Dionysius). The Platonic account of the soul's ascent to the Good was mapped onto the Christian narrative of redemption, producing a tradition in which rational dialectic and contemplative ascent were both paths toward union with God. This synthesis — Neoplatonism within Christianity — is what Vervaeke finds most valuable and what he draws on extensively (Ep 18, Ep 20).

**The historical distortion**: Over its history, Christianity underwent the transformations that eventually contributed to the meaning crisis: the onto-theological move (treating God as a supreme being rather than the ground of being), the credalization of religion (post-Reformation emphasis on propositional belief), and the displacement of religio by credo. The Reformation itself was a response to real failures but its unintended consequences were sapiential obsolescence and further credalization (Ep 20, Ep 21).
