# Cynicism

> Hellenistic philosophical movement founded by Antisthenes (~4th c. BCE); characterized by the rejection of social convention (nomos) in favor of natural virtue (physis); advocacy of radical self-sufficiency (autarkeia) as the only condition for happiness; and practice of enacted, embodied philosophy over propositional doctrine.

## Role in the argument
Cynicism is the most radical of the Hellenistic responses to domicide. Where the Stoics re-embedded the individual in the cosmic rational order, the Cynics went in the opposite direction: stripping everything down to the bare minimum and demonstrating through their lives that the conventions of the polis (wealth, reputation, social status, marriage) were neither necessary for nor conducive to genuine flourishing. Vervaeke uses Cynicism to illustrate the range of responses to perennial problems and to highlight the importance of enacted philosophy over propositional philosophy (Ep 14).

**Nomos vs physis**: The Cynics held that conventional social life (nomos — human law and custom) is not grounded in nature but is a web of agreed-upon fictions that distract from genuine virtue. The philosophical life strips away these fictions to live according to nature (physis) — which requires very little: a body, a mind, and the basic conditions for health. The Cynic insight is that the complexity and status-seeking of normal social life is the primary obstacle to wisdom (Ep 14).

**Enacted philosophy**: Diogenes of Sinope, the most famous Cynic, did not write philosophy — he performed it. His life was a sustained argument, enacted in public, that social conventions have no rational basis. This is an extreme form of participatory knowing as philosophical pedagogy: the philosopher's life is itself the demonstration of the position. Vervaeke uses this to distinguish propositional from participatory modes of philosophical communication (Ep 14).

**Legacy**: The Cynics' influence on Stoicism was acknowledged by the ancient sources, who called Cynicism "the short path to virtue." The Stoic Zeno had Cynic elements in his early training. The Cynic stress on self-sufficiency, training the will against discomfort, and cosmopolitanism all reappear in Stoicism in a more systematic form.
