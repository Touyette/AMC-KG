# Hebrew Prophetic Tradition

> The Axial tradition of the Hebrew prophets (Isaiah, Jeremiah, Amos, Hosea, Ezekiel, and others, c. 8th–5th c. BCE) who called Israel back from idolatry to covenant fidelity; Vervaeke treats it as a distinctive Axial contribution: the introduction of a radically transcendent God, the moral critique of religious formalism, and the participatory concept of covenantal knowing (da'at).

## Role in the argument
The Hebrew prophets enter AMC as one of the Axial traditions that exemplify the reflexive second-order turn: they subjected the existing religious practices and social structures to a searching moral critique, claiming to speak from a point of view beyond the immediate cultural consensus. Their contribution to Vervaeke's account lies in the concept of [[Da'ath]] (participatory knowing), the radical transcendence of God (contra idolatry), and the moral-existential demand of covenantal relationship (Ep 03, Ep 15).

**Radical transcendence and the iconoclastic critique**: The prophets' insistence on the uniqueness and radical transcendence of YHWH against the polytheistic environment was simultaneously a critique of idolatry (any finite thing treated as the ultimate ground of value) and an expression of the second-order thinking that the Axial Revolution required. The prohibition on images (no visual representation of God) enacts this: what is ultimate cannot be captured in any finite form. This is the origin of the apophatic tradition in Western religion (Ep 03).

**Da'at as participatory knowing**: The Hebrew prophets described the ideal relationship with God as da'at — literally knowing, but in the participatory, intimate sense that also describes sexual knowing (Adam "knew" Eve). Da'at is knowing-by, knowing-in-relationship, knowing that transforms the knower and the known. When the prophets accuse Israel of not "knowing" God (lo yada Adonai), they mean not that Israel has insufficient information but that Israel has lost participatory relationship. This is Vervaeke's key use of the prophetic tradition (Ep 03).

**The moral critique of formalism**: The prophets repeatedly criticize religious formalism — performing rituals while maintaining social injustice (Amos 5: "I hate, I despise your religious festivals"). The critique is that ritual without transformative relationship (da'at) is empty and worse than empty — it is a form of idolatry, treating God as a ritual machine rather than as the ground of moral-transformative relationship. This anticipates Vervaeke's distinction between religio (participatory cultivation) and credo (propositional assent) (Ep 03).

**Kairos**: The prophets' sense of time as qualitative — the appointed time, the moment of divine meeting — is what Greek would call kairos: the right, pregnant moment that demands response, as opposed to mere chronological time (chronos). The prophetic call is always a kairos: a moment when the ordinary structures are interrupted and a different mode of response becomes necessary (Ep 03).
