# Stoicism

> Hellenistic–Roman philosophical school founded by Zeno of Citium in Athens (~300 BCE); teaches that virtue (reason acting in accordance with nature) is the only genuine good; that externals are "indifferent" (adiaphora); that the cosmos is permeated by divine Logos; and that philosophy is practiced through spiritual exercises — especially prosoche (self-attention) — not merely as theoretical doctrine.

## Role in the argument
Stoicism is one of the three major Hellenistic responses to domicide — the loss of the polis as the source of meaning, identity, and social belonging. Where Epicureanism withdrew to the Garden and Cynicism stripped everything to radical austerity, Stoicism re-embedded the individual in a larger cosmic order: the Logos that pervades the universe is accessible to reason everywhere, making every person a citizen of the world. Vervaeke uses Stoicism as a paradigm case of a psycho-technology system — a full ecology of practices for transforming the self and addressing the perennial problems (Ep 14, Ep 43).

**Logos and cosmopolitanism**: The Stoics identified the individual human logos with a fragment of the cosmic Logos — the divine rational principle that orders nature. This re-embedding of the individual in the cosmic rational order is the Stoic response to the loss of polis identity: wherever you are, whatever your circumstances, you participate in the rational order of the whole. This is the earliest and most philosophically rigorous form of cosmopolitanism (Ep 14).

**Virtue as the only good**: The Stoic teaching that virtue is the only genuine good — and that health, wealth, reputation, and even life are "indifferent" — is a systematic recalibration of the relevance realization system. What matters is what the rational soul does with its judgments; externals are neither good nor bad. This produces the remarkable Stoic equanimity: genuine goodness is always available and can never be taken away by fortune (Ep 14).

**Spiritual exercises and prosoche**: Pierre Hadot demonstrated that Stoicism was practiced primarily as a set of spiritual exercises: prosoche (continuous self-attention), the view from above, premeditatio mortis (premeditation of death), objective seeing. These are psycho-technologies for recalibrating the agent-arena relationship — for attending appropriately to what genuinely matters. Marcus Aurelius's Meditations is the primary text demonstrating what prosoche looks like in practice across a lifetime (Ep 43).

**Major figures**: Zeno of Citium (founder), Cleanthes, Chrysippus (systematizer), Epictetus (most psychologically acute), Marcus Aurelius (philosopher-king exemplar).
