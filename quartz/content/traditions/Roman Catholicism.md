# Roman Catholicism

> The largest Christian denomination, centered on papal authority, the sacramental system, apostolic tradition, and the integration of Greek philosophy (especially Aristotle via Aquinas) with Christian theology; for Vervaeke, the primary institutional carrier of Western wisdom cultivation — and the institution whose loss (through the Reformation and scientific revolution) directly produces the sapiential vacuum of the modern meaning crisis.

## Role in the argument
Roman Catholicism enters AMC as the institution that — whatever its failures — had been the carrier of the wisdom-cultivation ecology in Western Christendom for over a millennium. Its sacramental system, monastic tradition, university system, and structure of spiritual direction constituted an institutionalized ecology of practices for meaning cultivation. The Reformation's attack on this institution, and the scientific revolution's challenge to its cosmological framework, dismantled this ecology without providing an adequate replacement (Ep 20, Ep 21).

**The sacramental system**: Catholic sacramentalism (the system of baptism, Eucharist, penance, confirmation, marriage, holy orders, anointing of the sick) was a psychotechnological ecology: each sacrament was a participatory enactment of a key transformation (initiation into community, regular renewal of communal participation, forgiveness and re-integration, strengthening of commitment, etc.). As participatory rites rather than propositional doctrines, they functioned at the level of perspectival and participatory knowing (Ep 20).

**Scholastic synthesis**: Thomas Aquinas's synthesis of Aristotelian philosophy and Christian theology created an integrated worldview in which the natural order (Aristotle's teleological nature) and the supernatural order (Christian salvation history) were mutually supporting. This synthesis provided the three orders (nomological, normative, narrative) with mutual reinforcement. The dissolution of the scholastic synthesis (through Nominalism, the scientific revolution, and the Reformation) unraveled this integration (Ep 20).

**The Reformation's challenge**: When Luther challenged the Church's authority, he did so in ways that had unintended consequences for the wisdom-cultivation function. The attack on indulgences led to the attack on purgatory, which led to the attack on prayers for the dead, which undermined the whole sacramental economy of spiritual practices. The credalization that followed (sola fide, sola scriptura) emptied the participatory dimension of religion in favor of propositional assent (Ep 21).
