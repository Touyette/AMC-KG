# Gnosticism

> A family of late antique religious-philosophical movements emphasizing salvific knowledge (gnosis) through direct transformative experience — as opposed to faith or ritual alone — typically involving a sharp dualism between the transcendent true God and the inferior demiurge who made the material world; Vervaeke treats Gnosticism as a crucial (if flawed) carrier of the participatory-transformative knowing structure.

## Role in the argument
Gnosticism enters AMC most explicitly through the analysis of the Neoplatonic tradition and through Corbin's retrieval of Islamic Gnosticism (Ismaili Sufism). Vervaeke treats Gnosticism as preserving a crucial insight — that the primary mode of religious life is transformative participatory knowing (gnosis), not propositional assent — while distorting it through extreme dualism: the material world becomes a prison from which the spirit escapes, rather than a participatory field in which it deepens (Ep 16, Ep 17, Ep 48).

**Gnosis as participatory knowing**: The Gnostic core is the concept of gnosis itself: salvific knowledge that is not about propositions but about an identity-level transformation of the self. The Gnostic "knows" not by believing doctrines but by undergoing an initiation that changes who one fundamentally is. This is what Vervaeke reclaims: the structure of knowing-by (participatory knowing) as essential to the wisdom project (Ep 16, Ep 17).

**The demiurge and the Fall**: In most Gnostic systems, the material world was created by an inferior or ignorant deity (the demiurge), and the divine spark (pneuma) in human beings is imprisoned in matter and seeking return to the true God above. This generates the extreme dualism (matter is evil, spirit is good; world is prison, pleroma is home) that Vervaeke diagnoses as a failure: it destroys the possibility of finding meaning in embodied, worldly, relational life (Ep 17, Ep 48).

**Corbin's retrieval**: Henry Corbin retrieves Ismaili Gnosticism (Islamic esoteric tradition) and argues that the Gnostic structure — the divine double, the ascent through imaginal worlds, the transformative encounter with one's higher self — can be understood without the extreme metaphysical dualism. The imaginal is real but not material, spiritual but not disembodied. This is Corbin's contribution to the participatory recovery (Ep 48, Ep 49).

**Barfield and Gnosticism**: Barfield's final participation is partly a response to Gnosticism's failure: the Gnostic move is to recover the original participation (before the fall into matter) by escaping the world. Barfield insists instead on final participation: recovering participating knowing within and through the world, not escaping it. This is the positive correction to Gnosticism's dualism (Ep 50).
