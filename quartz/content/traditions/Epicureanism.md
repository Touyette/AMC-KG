# Epicureanism

> Hellenistic philosophical school founded by Epicurus in Athens (~306 BCE) in his school "the Garden"; teaches that the highest good is ataraxia (tranquility, freedom from mental disturbance) and aponia (freedom from bodily pain); achieves this through the careful management of desire (eliminating "vain desires" and cultivating natural, necessary ones), the cultivation of friendship as the highest good, and withdrawal from public life into a community of like-minded companions.

## Role in the argument
Epicureanism is one of the three major Hellenistic responses to domicide. Where the Stoics remained engaged with the world and the Cynics enacted radical austerity in public, the Epicureans withdrew to the Garden: a counter-cultural community of friends living simply and philosophically, separated from the political arena that had ceased to provide meaning. Vervaeke uses Epicureanism to illustrate the withdrawal strategy — the attempt to find meaning and belonging in a deliberately cultivated micro-community when the larger culture has fragmented (Ep 14).

**Ataraxia**: Epicurus's primary goal — tranquility (ataraxia, literally "without disturbance") — is achieved not through passive withdrawal but through active philosophical therapy: identifying the sources of mental disturbance (fear of death, fear of the gods, unsatisfied unlimited desires) and eliminating them through understanding. The Epicurean philosophical life is therapeutic: philosophy is medicine for the soul. Fear of death is removed by the argument that where death is, we are not; fear of the gods by understanding that they are indifferent to human affairs (Ep 14).

**Friendship**: Epicurus argued that friendship (philia) is the greatest of all goods — greater than pleasure, greater than virtue in isolation. The Epicurean community was structured around friendship as its organizing value: a community of mutual care, shared philosophy, and simple pleasures. For Vervaeke, this is an anticipation of the communitas that the meaning crisis demands — a counter-cultural community of practice that provides genuine belonging and shared orientation (Ep 14).

**Withdrawal and the Garden**: The Epicurean withdrawal from politics ("live hidden") is both a response to domicide (the polis no longer provides meaning) and an assertion that meaning is found in the quality of one's inner life and relationships, not in status, power, or public achievement. This is the strategy of creating a micro-culture of meaning within a larger culture that has lost its meaning-structures (Ep 14).
