# The Sophists

> The group of Greek intellectual entrepreneurs (5th–4th c. BCE) who taught rhetoric, argumentation, and practical wisdom for pay; often associated with relativism ("man is the measure of all things" — Protagoras) and the prioritization of persuasion over truth; for Vervaeke, the foils against whom Socrates defines the philosophical project.

## Role in the argument
The Sophists enter AMC as Socrates' primary interlocutors and opponents. They represent the alternative to the philosophical project: the use of rhetorical skill to produce persuasion without concern for truth; the relativization of values to context, culture, or convention; and the commercialization of wisdom into a marketable skill. Socrates' entire project — the examined life, dialectic as psychotechnology, the distinction between knowledge and mere opinion — is defined in opposition to the Sophistic practice (Ep 04).

**Protagoras's relativism**: "Man is the measure of all things" — Protagoras's famous dictum — is the philosophical expression of Sophistic relativism: there is no truth independent of the perspective of the measurer; what appears true to each person is true for that person. This is a form of epistemic individualism that makes the Socratic project impossible from the start: if there is no truth beyond appearance, the examined life has no objective criterion to examine toward (Ep 04).

**Rhetoric vs. dialectic**: The Sophists taught rhetoric — the art of persuasion, especially in the law courts and political assemblies. Rhetoric aims at producing a desired effect in the audience regardless of whether the position argued for is true. Dialectic (Socrates' method) aims at truth — examining positions critically until what survives is what stands up to scrutiny. This is the founding distinction of Western philosophy: the pursuit of truth vs. the pursuit of persuasion (Ep 04).

**The marketplace of ideas**: The Sophists were the first intellectual professionals in Greece — they charged fees for their teaching (unlike Socrates, who claimed to charge nothing, though he also received support from wealthy Athenians). This commercialization of sophia (wisdom) into a marketable product is itself a form of the having-mode: wisdom as possession rather than as a way of being. Socrates' ironic claim that he knows nothing is a direct critique of this commercialization (Ep 04).

**Revisionary reading**: Some contemporary scholars argue that the Sophists were more sophisticated than Plato's portrayal suggests — that they were genuinely developing alternative epistemologies and pedagogies. Vervaeke acknowledges this but focuses on the Platonic-Socratic critique as historically significant: whatever the Sophists actually thought, the Socratic response to the image of them as relativist rhetoricians defined the philosophical tradition (Ep 04).
