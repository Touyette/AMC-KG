# Natural Philosophy

> The ancient Greek (and later early modern) tradition of explaining the natural world through rational principles rather than mythological narratives; includes the pre-Socratics (Thales, Heraclitus, Parmenides) through Aristotle's physics and into the early modern period (Galileo, Descartes, Newton); Vervaeke treats its Axial origin as the beginning of second-order thinking about nature.

## Role in the argument
Natural philosophy enters AMC as the tradition that initiated what Vervaeke calls "second-order thinking" about the natural world: instead of accepting mythological narratives about why things happen, the natural philosophers sought rational, general principles. Thales' claim that "all is water" is not primarily a scientific hypothesis but a declaration that the world has an underlying rational order accessible to reason — a watershed moment in the Axial Revolution (Ep 02, Ep 03).

**Pre-Socratic natural philosophy**: The pre-Socratics (Thales, Anaximander, Anaximenes, Heraclitus, Parmenides, Empedocles, Democritus) developed the first systematic accounts of natural change using immanent rational principles. Vervaeke emphasizes Heraclitus (logos as the principle of all change and unity of opposites), Parmenides (the argument for unchanging Being), and the tension between them as the beginning of the dialectical tradition. The pre-Socratics were developing [[Ontological Depth Perception]] — the capacity to see through appearances to underlying rational structure (Ep 03).

**Aristotelian natural philosophy**: Aristotle synthesized pre-Socratic speculation into a systematic natural philosophy: the four causes (material, formal, efficient, final), the concept of substance (form + matter), natural teleology (everything moves toward its natural end). This provided the metaphysical framework for medieval Christian scholasticism — when this framework collapsed (Galileo, Newton), it took the theological and moral framework with it (Ep 07).

**The Scientific Revolution as natural philosophy's transformation**: The early modern Scientific Revolution (Copernicus, Galileo, Newton) is both the culmination and the transformation of natural philosophy: mathematical physics replaced Aristotelian teleology, the quantitative replaced the qualitative, and the primary/secondary quality distinction relocated meaning from the world to the subject. This transformation is the proximate cause of the meaning crisis: natural philosophy became modern science, but in doing so lost the framework that had supported teleological and moral understanding of nature (Ep 20, Ep 22).
