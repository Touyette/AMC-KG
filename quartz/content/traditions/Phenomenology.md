# Phenomenology

> Husserl's movement: disciplined, reflective attention to the first-person structure of experience and how world is disclosed through consciousness; aimed at restoring direct contact with reality after Cartesian philosophy had severed mind from world.

## Vervaeke's framing
Vervaeke uses Husserl's phenomenology as a philosophical partner for AMC's cognitive science: both traditions model cognition as fundamentally relational and world-engaging, not as inner representation followed by inference. Phenomenology is a **contact epistemology** — it tries to get back to "the things themselves" by paying careful, disciplined attention to how experience is always already directed at a world (Ep 46).

**Phenomenological attitude** ≠ ordinary introspection (the everyday stance). Introspection is naive — it simply turns attention inward. Phenomenology is a disciplined practice in which one pays reflective, experimental, exploratory, probative attention to the *structure* of experience. In Vervaeke's terms: it is a careful attending to one's *perspectival knowing of the transjective relationship* — how things are disclosed, how world and knower are constitutively entangled (Ep 46).

**Intentionality** (noesis/noema): Husserl's central finding. Consciousness is always *of* something — all mental acts are directed at an object. The act of intending is called *noesis*; the object as intended (not the physical thing, but its mode of givenness) is the *noema*. There is a constant double structure: the intentional act and its correlate world-disclosure. Phenomenology investigates this double structure (Ep 46).

**Downstream influence**: Existentialism (Heidegger does something to phenomenology and generates existentialism); deconstruction/postmodernism; 4E cognitive science (Varela, Thompson, Dreyfus, Taylor). Dreyfus and Taylor's concept of a "contact epistemology" that is being lost in modernity comes directly from their phenomenological heritage. Merleau-Ponty extends Husserl toward embodied participatory knowing, partially repairing what Heidegger identified as the key gap (Ep 46).

**Heidegger's critique of Husserlian phenomenology**: Despite restoring intentional directedness, Husserl's framework (1) did not ground intentionality in participatory knowing (the ontological modes in which agent and arena are fundamentally co-constituted), and (2) remained inside the Cartesian grammar — still beginning from the subject's acts rather than from being-in-the-world. This made phenomenology's realism, in Heidegger's view (and later Sparrow's), ultimately a form of idealism (Ep 46).
