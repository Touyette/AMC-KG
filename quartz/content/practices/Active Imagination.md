# Active Imagination

> Jung's central psycho-technology for individuation: a waking practice in which the practitioner allows unconscious contents (images, figures, emotions) to emerge and develop autonomously — without directing or suppressing them — then enters into genuine dialogue with them; the imaginal complement to dream interpretation; the practice that makes Jungian psychology more than theory.

## Vervaeke's framing
Active imagination is Jung's most concrete practice and the one that makes him the most practically useful of the prophets. Where Heidegger has only Gelassenheit (a posture, not a technique) and Tillich has no equivalent at all, Jung developed an explicit method for engaging the imaginal in a transformative way. Vervaeke treats active imagination as the Jungian form of the practice ecology for engaging with the sacred second self (the Self archetype) that every prophet proposes but few specify (Ep 49).

**How it works**: The practitioner enters a relaxed but alert state and allows unconscious images or figures to arise — not directed as in daydreaming or fantasy, but allowed to develop on their own terms. The key move is to then *engage* these figures: speak to them, argue with them, listen to their response. The figures are not treated as mere psychological projections to be explained but as genuine interlocutors with their own perspectives. This is the imaginal (Corbin) not the imaginary (merely subjective) (Ep 49).

**Why this works (Vervaeke's reading)**: The archetypes that emerge in active imagination are virtual engines of salience landscaping — structural patterns in the psyche that organize attention, motivation, and meaning-making. By dialoguing with them rather than simply enacting them (unconscious) or suppressing them (ego control), the practitioner achieves a genuine non-logical identity: the ego and the Self come into anagogic relationship. The practice cultivates the perspectival and participatory dimensions of knowing that neither propositional knowledge nor procedural skill can reach (Ep 49).

**The Jungian advantage**: Among the five prophets, Jung provides the most developed psychotechnologies — active imagination and dream interpretation. This is why Vervaeke says Jung supplies the psychological implementation that Corbin lacks: both describe the imaginal transformation, but only Jung gives you an actual practice for achieving it (Ep 49).

**Limits**: The concern Buber and Corbin raise — that Jungian practice can become too intra-psychic, too focused on the inner theater of the psyche — applies to active imagination too. For the transformation to be genuinely participatory (not merely psychological), the dialogue with the imaginal figures must remain genuinely transjective: the figures must be encountered as disclosing something real about the world, not merely about one's own psychology (Ep 49).
