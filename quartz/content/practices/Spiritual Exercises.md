# Spiritual Exercises

> Pierre Hadot's term for the concrete, repeated, body-involving practices that ancient philosophers actually performed as part of their philosophical life — not ancillary to philosophy but its heart; the means by which philosophical doctrines were internalized and transformed into lived wisdom.

## Vervaeke's framing
Hadot's recovery of "spiritual exercises" (exercices spirituels) is a key resource for Vervaeke's project. Hadot demonstrated that ancient philosophical schools — Stoic, Platonic, Epicurean, even Skeptical — were primarily practice communities: they organized themselves around concrete exercises for transforming the agent's relationship to reality, not merely around doctrines to be believed. The "spiritual" in spiritual exercises does not mean supernatural but refers to exercises of the pneuma (spirit, breath, rational soul) — the cognitive-existential dimension of the person (Ep 43).

**The exercises**: Hadot identified a family of exercises that appeared across ancient schools in different forms: prosoche (self-attention), the view from above, premeditatio mortis (premeditation of death), objective seeing (seeing things as they are without projective overlays), philosophical dialogue, contemplation of the whole, examination of conscience, and sustained attention to the present moment. These are not techniques for achieving goals (though they have effects) but modes of engaging with reality that are themselves constitutive of wisdom (Ep 43).

**Practice over theory**: Hadot's central claim — that philosophical doctrine was secondary to philosophical practice — is the historical warrant for Vervaeke's account of wisdom as an ecology of practices rather than a body of knowledge. One cannot become wise by learning Stoic doctrine; one must practice the exercises through which Stoic wisdom is constituted. The doctrine articulates what the exercises are doing and why; the exercises are what actually transforms the agent (Ep 43).

**Relevance to WCE**: Vervaeke's Wise Cultivation of Enlightenment is precisely the attempt to construct an ecology of practices adequate to the contemporary meaning crisis — to design a "spiritual exercises" system that is scientifically informed, cross-traditional, and open to secular practitioners. Hadot's recovery of spiritual exercises provides the historical model for what such a system would look like (Ep 43, Ep 46).
