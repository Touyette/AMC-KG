# Vipassana

> Pali: "clear seeing" or insight meditation; the Buddhist practice of cultivating direct, non-conceptual perception of the three marks of existence (impermanence, no-fixed-self, unsatisfactoriness); paired with samatha (concentration) to constitute the core of Buddhist meditation practice.

## Role in the argument
Vipassana enters AMC as the paradigm Buddhist practice for transforming the most fundamental levels of the RR machinery. While samatha (concentration) stabilizes and clarifies attention, vipassana uses that stable attention to see directly — not propositionally but in a participatory way — how experience is actually structured: impermanent, without a fixed self at its core, fundamentally unsatisfactory when approached through craving (Ep 08, Ep 09).

**Seeing the three marks**: The three marks (tilakkhanā) are not doctrines to believe but features of experience to be directly perceived through sustained, refined attention. When attention is clear enough and non-reactive enough, the impermanence of experience is directly visible (not just intellectually understood), the absence of a fixed self becomes apparent, and the way craving generates dukkha can be seen moment by moment in its arising. This seeing restructures how one relates to experience — it is participatory-perspectival transformation, not propositional learning (Ep 08).

**Deautomatization and vipassana**: Vervaeke connects vipassana to the concept of [[Deautomatization]]: the practice systematically disrupts the automatic, habitual patterns of salience and response that constitute ordinary cognition. By looking directly at the arising and passing of mental events without immediately categorizing and reacting, the practitioner interrupts the default relevance assignments and makes space for a more accurate, less craving-driven RR (Ep 09).

**Vipassana and opponent processing**: The practice involves sustained attention to the arising of mental events alongside a cultivated non-reactivity — the capacity to observe without immediately approaching or avoiding. This is an instance of [[Opponent Processing]] in the meditating mind: the observing awareness and the observed content in a dynamical relation that gradually decouples the observer from the reactive habits, enabling genuine seeing (Ep 09).

**Pure consciousness events**: At the advanced end of vipassana practice, some traditions report "pure consciousness events" — moments when the observing awareness itself becomes the sole content, without any particular object. Vervaeke (following Robert Forman) treats these as evidence of a mode of awareness that is non-intentional, pointing to the deepest level of de-centering and de-automatization (Ep 09).
