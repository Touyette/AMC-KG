# Lectio Divina

> Monastic Christian reading practice: slow, aloud, communal engagement with sacred text in which the goal is to be transformed by the text rather than extract propositions from it — a psycho-technology targeting procedural, perspectival, and participatory knowing.

## Vervaeke's framing
Vervaeke discusses Lectio Divina as the dominant mode of textual engagement in the early medieval West and the Patristic period, drawing on Ivan Illich's work on Hugh of Saint Victor (Ep 19). Before the shift toward silent propositional reading, people read aloud — often communally, often reciting. The point was not to gather information but to open oneself to be spoken to by the text, to let it operate on one's perspectival and participatory modes. It is, as Vervaeke puts it, "ontologically remedial": designed to heal and transform, not merely to inform. The difference is like listening to music prepared to have a profound aesthetic experience rather than scanning a receipt.

Vervaeke treats Lectio Divina as the psycho-technology through which Augustine's synthesis was made liveable, not merely believable. One did not merely assent to propositions about the Narrative Order; one recited the Bible communally, allowed it to reorganize one's salience landscape, and participated in the transformation it enacted. This is why Vervaeke emphasizes that the Confessions — Augustine's own participatory text — is itself a form of Lectio Divina: the reader is invited to undergo the same transformation Augustine underwent.

The shift away from Lectio Divina toward silent propositional reading marks a pivotal cultural devaluation of perspectival and participatory knowing. Reading becomes the consumption of propositions; the self that reads becomes the intensive self — primarily structured by inner coherent language rather than by transformative encounter with reality (Ep 19).
