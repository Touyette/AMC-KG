# View from Above

> Stoic visualization practice in which the practitioner imaginatively ascends to a cosmic-level perspective, seeing their life, city, and circumstances from a vast remove — and thereby seeing the anxiety-producing concerns of daily life in their proper (small) proportion, while the genuinely important things (virtue, right action, one's inner faculty) retain or gain in significance.

## Vervaeke's framing
The view from above is one of Hadot's paradigmatic Stoic spiritual exercises — explicitly practiced by Marcus Aurelius in the *Meditations* and Epictetus in the *Discourses*. The practitioner deliberately adopts a perspective from which the local and the contingent appear properly small: reputation, wealth, political reversals, social slights. From this cosmic vantage point, the only thing that remains significant is the quality of one's own rational faculty and the actions it produces. For Vervaeke, the view from above is a perspectival exercise that recalibrates RR — it reorganizes what is salient by changing the perspective from which salience is assessed (Ep 43).

**The mechanism**: The practice works by changing the perspective from which one assesses what matters. From the scale of a human life in a city on a particular day, many things feel urgent and important. From the scale of geological time, the solar system, the cosmos — the same things appear correctly small. Marcus Aurelius uses this repeatedly: the whole sweep of history before Alexander, and everything since, shows that what appears great is local and transient. This is not cynicism (nothing matters) but proportionality: what remains significant from the cosmic perspective is what is genuinely significant (Ep 43).

**View from above and the Solomon effect**: There is a structural connection between the view from above and the Solomon effect (Grossman): both achieve wisdom by taking a third-person or distanced perspective on one's own situation. The view from above generalizes this spatial and temporal distancing — it is not just imagining how an outside observer would see your dilemma but imaginatively occupying the perspective of the whole. Vervaeke suggests that practices that achieve genuine third-person perspectives on the self are among the most reliable methods for wisdom cultivation (Ep 43).

**Connections to 4E cognition**: The view from above is a counterintuitive practice for embodied creatures who are naturally embedded in their immediate situation. It requires the capacity to imaginatively de-center — to take up a perspective radically different from one's natural first-person, embodied view. This is the perspectival dimension of wisdom: not escaping the body but using the cognitive capacity for perspective-taking to see one's embodied situation from without (Ep 43).
