# Internalizing the Sage

> Stoic (and wider wisdom-tradition) practice of taking on the normative perspective of a wise exemplar — Socrates, the idealized Stoic Sage, Marcus Aurelius — and using that perspective as one's internal metacognitive standard; the exemplar becomes an inner voice that asks, "What would the sage do here?" before each action and judgment.

## Vervaeke's framing
Internalizing the sage is a practice that addresses the reflectiveness gap and the problem of self-deception by providing a stable, external-yet-internalized standard for evaluating one's own judgments. The sage is not an external authority imposing rules but a constructed inner perspective — a metacognitive second voice that can evaluate one's first-order impulses and judgments from the outside. Epictetus's version involves asking, in any situation, what the wise and good person would do; Marcus Aurelius's version involves writing to himself as if to a student who needs instruction; the Socratic version involves imagining Socrates's cross-examination (Ep 43).

**The mechanism**: The practice works by creating a genuine second perspective within the agent's metacognitive system. The tendency of self-deception is to capture both first-order responses AND the metacognitive evaluation — to rationalize rather than genuinely evaluate. The externalization of the standard (onto the figure of the sage) makes it harder for self-deception to capture both: the sage can ask the question you don't want to hear. Over time, the sage's perspective is genuinely internalized as a character disposition — it becomes not an external check but a constitutive part of who you are (Ep 43).

**Socratic elenchus as internalized practice**: Vervaeke traces internalizing the sage to the Socratic tradition: Socrates functioned as an external exemplar for his companions, who were supposed to internalize his mode of questioning. The Stoics systematized this into explicit practice: take Socrates, Cato, or the idealized sage as the inner model. This is not mere imitation (copying behavior) but perspectival transformation: adopting the vantage point from which the sage would see your situation (Ep 43, Ep 04).

**Connection to Solomon effect**: Internalizing the sage achieves the Solomon effect (wiser judgment about others' situations than one's own) from the inside: by adopting the perspective of the sage (who is not yourself), you temporarily escape the first-person lock that makes self-assessment so difficult. The sage-perspective is the inner version of the third-person view that the Solomon effect shows produces better reasoning (Ep 43).
