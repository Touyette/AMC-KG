# Metta

> Pali: metta (Sanskrit: maitri); loving-kindness; Buddhist practice of systematically cultivating a warm goodwill and benevolent regard toward all sentient beings — starting with oneself, then expanding to loved ones, neutral persons, difficult persons, and finally all beings; the direct counteraction to alienation (the perennial problem of lost connectedness with others).

## Vervaeke's framing
Metta is the Buddhist practice specifically designed to address the perennial problem of alienation — the experience of disconnection from others, nature, and the ground of meaning. Where communitas addresses alienation at the collective level (dissolving social roles in shared encounter), metta addresses it at the individual level: it trains the agent's RR machinery to attend to the conditions for personhood and flourishing in every being encountered. In this sense, metta is the practice closest to Vervaeke's concept of agape — it is the Buddhist cultivation of the very mode of attending that agape names (Ep 39).

**The metta practice**: The practitioner begins by directing loving-kindness toward themselves — "May I be happy; may I be safe; may I be healthy; may I live at ease" — and then gradually expands the circle: to a loved one, to a neutral person, to a difficult person, to all beings in all directions. The practice is not about generating pleasant feelings but about training the disposition to attend to beings as having welfare that matters — as being objects of genuine concern rather than instruments or obstacles. It is a recalibration of the relevance realization system toward the conditions for personhood (Ep 39).

**Metta as agape**: Vervaeke identifies a deep structural parallel between metta (Buddhist loving-kindness) and agape (Christian unconditional love). Both are modes of attending to the other not for their particular qualities (Eros) or as members of one's group (Philia) but for the conditions of their personhood as such. Both are cultivated through practice, not merely felt as spontaneous emotion. And both are proposed as responses to alienation and as conditions for genuine wisdom (Ep 39).

**Opponent processing**: Metta functions as an opponent to the parasitic processing patterns that generate alienation: where parasitic processing narrows the agent-arena relationship around the self and its threats, metta deliberately expands it — training attention to register the welfare of others as genuinely relevant. This is the cognitive-existential mechanism by which the practice addresses alienation at the level of RR machinery rather than mere belief (Ep 39).
