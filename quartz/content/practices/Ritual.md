# Ritual

> Formalized, repeated, enactive practice in which participants enact a symbolic pattern — a myth — in a mode of serious play; not mere habit or ceremony, but enacted anagoge: a participatory embodiment of the deepest meaning-structures of a tradition that transforms the participants' relationship to what is sacred.

## Vervaeke's framing
Ritual is the third component of Mythos (symbol + story + ritual) and is described as "enactive Anagoge." Where symbol provides the participatory representation and story (myth) provides the narrative frame, ritual is the enactive dimension — the bodily, collective, repeated performance that allows participants to dwell in and be transformed by the symbolic pattern. Ritual does not merely communicate information about sacred realities; it instantiates them in the agent-arena relationship (Ep 35).

**Serious play**: Ritual operates in the mode of serious play — the participant is genuinely engaged (not ironically distant) while knowing that what is happening is enacted analogy rather than literal fact. This is the same mode as artistic or imaginative engagement: one is genuinely moved by what one knows to be representation. Vervaeke argues that this is not a defect but the cognitive-existential mechanism by which ritual works: the suspension of the literal/fictional distinction allows the symbolic to have participatory (not merely propositional) import (Ep 35).

**Turner's ritual process**: Victor Turner's anthropological account of ritual — as involving three phases (separation from normal structure → liminality → reincorporation) — is how ritual generates communitas. The liminal phase is the dissolution of social roles that creates the conditions for genuine encounter and transformation. Ritual is thus the communal practice that coordinates collective transformation; it is not merely an individual experience (Ep 37).

**Ritual and the collapse of meaning**: One dimension of the meaning crisis is the collapse of genuine ritual — the replacement of enacted anagoge with ceremony (which is routine performance without transformation) or with spectacle (which is passive observation without participation). When ritual becomes mere ceremony, Mythos collapses and with it the religio that bound the community to what is sacred. This is why Vervaeke argues that one response to the meaning crisis must include the recovery or creation of genuine ritual — practices that enact the deepest meaning-structures of the emerging worldview (Ep 39).
